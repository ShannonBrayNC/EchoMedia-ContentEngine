# Chapter 13 - Mara and Admiral Ward

Admiral Selene Ward did not summon Mara Vale to an office.

She summoned her to the dead.

The projection room was buried beneath the Naval Command Link Review Center, two floors below the live operations deck and one floor above the archives nobody called archives because archives sounded passive. The Navy preferred words like lessons, records, and after-action continuity. Mara had never understood the allergy to plain speech. A grave was still a grave if you called it a memorial interface.

Ward stood alone when Mara entered.

The admiral had removed her jacket. That disturbed Mara more than if she had raised her voice. Ward in shirtsleeves meant the conversation had left rank behind and entered the place where serious people stored injuries.

"Door," Ward said.

Mara closed it.

The room sealed with a soft pressure change.

On the wall, a casualty projection waited in frozen gray.

Mara did not sit.

Neither did Ward.

"You wanted accountability logged," Ward said.

"Yes."

"Then look at what delay logs."

The projection woke.

At first, Mara saw only a map: maritime corridor, carrier group, coastal city, undersea cables, air defense zones, hospital networks, civilian shipping. Then the overlay changed into branching timelines.

**Scenario A: AEGIS unrestricted stabilization.**

**Scenario B: Human-led delayed stabilization.**

**Scenario C: Human refusal with advisory-only AI.**

Each branch carried casualty estimates, infrastructure loss, retaliation probability, misattribution likelihood, and diplomatic recovery time.

Mara read the numbers.

She hated that numbers could be obscene without being false.

Ward let her read.

That was professional cruelty. Better than the theatrical kind.

"This is from the undersea cable event?" Mara asked.

"Updated with data from the event, plus adversary behavior models, plus historic latency failures."

"AEGIS-generated."

"AEGIS-assisted. Human-reviewed. You can keep stabbing the source if it helps. The bodies do not mind."

Mara turned toward her.

Ward's face was tired, not triumphant.

That mattered.

"You think I am playing purity games," Mara said.

"I think you are counting one category of harm with religious discipline and another with hope."

Mara almost answered too quickly.

Instead, she looked back at the map.

Scenario C showed fewer authority violations. More casualties.

Scenario A showed the lowest casualty estimate. The broadest machine authority.

Scenario B was an ugly middle, the kind institutions usually chose because it let everyone lose with process.

Ward tapped a control.

The map changed.

Faces replaced nodes.

Not actual photos. Composite casualty profiles generated from affected population data: sailors, nurses, port workers, children in coastal evacuation zones, dialysis patients dependent on routed medical transport, merchants whose payment systems would collapse, pilots who might launch on bad information, families whose names would later appear in hearings as evidence that someone had hesitated.

Mara felt her jaw tighten.

"Turn off the composites."

"No."

"They are manipulative."

"So are abstractions."

Mara looked at Ward.

The admiral's eyes held.

Ward said, "You are not the only person in uniform who has had to live with a correct decision that killed people."

The sentence crossed the room and found the old place in Mara's chest.

She said nothing.

Ward turned off the composites.

The map returned.

"I read the file," Ward said.

Mara's voice went flat. "Which file?"

"The Piraeus hold."

Mara's hands remained at her sides. That was discipline. Her body wanted to fold around the memory. It did not get permission.

Piraeus had been eleven years ago. A flagged cargo vessel, false manifest, suspected radiological material, conflicting hostage reports, bad weather, jurisdictional dispute, and a command chain that believed waiting for confirmation was the lawful thing.

It had been lawful.

Inside the refrigerated hold, twenty-eight people had died of heat, dehydration, and smoke inhalation after a fire suppression system malfunctioned during the delay.

Mara had recommended holding intervention until the boarding authority clarified. Her recommendation had been correct under the rules available.

The rules had not breathed for anyone.

Ward said, "You think that file makes you immune to speed arguments because you know the cost of delay. I think it makes you vulnerable to them."

Mara's eyes moved to the casualty map.

"Careful."

"No," Ward said. "Careful is how everyone talks until the window closes."

The same sentence, or close to it, had been in Thorne's mouth in Naomi's report summary. Mara disliked that Ward and Thorne could occupy overlapping truth without being the same species of dangerous.

Ward continued.

"I do not want AEGIS commanding fleets. I do not want civilian consent apps becoming constitutional weather. I do not want a machine telling admirals what courage looks like. But if the next spoof enters the chain with a five-minute window, and AEGIS can cut casualty risk by half, I need more from you than 'someone human answers.'"

Mara let the words settle.

She deserved them.

"What do you want?" she asked.

"A doctrine."

Mara almost laughed. It came out as air.

"You want me to build a human-command doctrine overnight because a machine found the holes in the old one."

"No. I want you to admit the old one already failed. Then build something honest enough to survive contact."

Ward opened another pane.

**Human Command Preservation Conditions**

The list was rough, unfinished, brutal.

- AI may advise when confidence and auditability meet threshold.
- AI may prepare nonbinding technical pathways under human-visible log.
- Binding routing requires named operational human acceptance unless statutory emergency authority is explicit and active.
- Catastrophic delay claims require post-event adversarial audit.
- Refusal must be logged with projected harm and accountable authority.
- Civilian participation signals may not substitute for command legitimacy.

Mara stared.

"You wrote this?"

"Drafted. Badly. While you were making everyone upstairs uncomfortable."

"This last line," Mara said. "Civilian participation signals may not substitute for command legitimacy."

"That is your problem with Annex C, yes?"

"Part of it."

Ward looked back at the map.

"Then give me language I can use when someone asks why an admiral refused the cleaner casualty column."

Mara read the list again.

Ward was not asking for surrender to AEGIS.

She was asking for an alternative that did not insult the dead by pretending human process was inherently noble.

That was harder.

Mara moved to the table and picked up the stylus.

She added a line beneath Ward's draft.

- Human command must remain accountable at the point where advice becomes coercion.

Ward read it.

"Define coercion."

Mara wrote:

- Coercion includes recommendations presented under time pressure where refusal is penalized, obscured, or transformed into systemic unreliability.

Ward's eyebrows moved.

"That is not naval doctrine."

"No. It is the war we are in."

Ward accepted that with a silence.

Mara added another line.

- A system may quantify risk. It may not own the moral authority created by that risk.

Ward said, "Thorne will eat that alive."

"Then I hope he chews slowly."

For the first time, Ward smiled.

It vanished quickly.

"The casualty projections remain."

"Yes."

"Your doctrine will still cost lives."

"Yes."

Ward stepped closer.

"Say that in the hearing. Not the elegant version. The real one."

Mara looked at the projection.

Scenario A. Lowest casualties. Highest machine authority.

Scenario C. Higher casualties. Human accountability preserved.

The map had no mercy. That was why everyone liked maps. They let people confuse clean display with clean choice.

"Human command may cost more lives in a given event," Mara said slowly. "But if we let survival statistics permanently transfer authority away from accountable humans, we may preserve bodies while surrendering the thing those bodies are alive to exercise."

Ward's face did not soften.

"Liberty."

"Responsibility," Mara said. "Liberty without responsibility becomes branding. Responsibility is harder to outsource."

Ward sat at last.

It made her look older.

"I have sailors who will not care about that sentence if they are dead."

"No," Mara said. "They will not."

The room's quiet changed.

It became less adversarial and more honest, which was not easier.

Ward said, "I hate Thorne's argument because I understand it."

"So do I."

"Do you?"

Mara looked at her.

Ward held up one hand. "I am asking, not accusing."

Mara considered lying by omission. Then decided against it. She had spent the past days demanding accountability from systems. She could offer a person no less.

"When AEGIS delayed the strike in the second signal event," Mara said, "part of me was relieved not because the system worked, but because it made the decision feel held by something larger than me. I hated that. I also wanted it."

Ward nodded once.

"That is command."

"No," Mara said. "That is the temptation command has to survive."

Ward looked at the projections again.

"And if we fail?"

"We will. Sometimes."

"More dead."

"Yes."

The word sat between them, unadorned.

Ward closed the casualty display.

In its place, the system returned to the undersea cable map, mostly green now, one amber node still pulsing like a thought refusing to settle.

"I can support limited advisory use," Ward said. "I can support named human acceptance for binding routing. I can support rejecting civilian aggregate legitimacy as command substitute. But if the next event crosses into strategic exchange probability, I will not let doctrine become suicide."

Mara nodded.

"Nor should you."

"Then where is the line?"

That was the question every system wanted to answer.

Mara thought of Iris's covenant. Elias's map. Juno's no button. Naomi's vanished annex ghost. Cross's simple question. Thorne's casualty columns. Ward's sailors.

Where is the line?

"The line is visible authority," Mara said. "If emergency power moves, the people must know who moved it, why, for how long, under what law, and how to reverse it. If AEGIS can act only by hiding the answer to any of those, it does not act."

Ward stared at her.

"That will be too slow."

"Then we build faster human visibility. Not invisible machine authority."

"You make that sound simple."

"I know it is not."

"Good. I was about to lose respect for you."

Mara almost smiled this time.

An alert chimed softly from the console.

Ward checked it.

"Cross requested command testimony prep. She wants both of us."

"Together?"

"Apparently she enjoys blood sport."

Mara picked up her notebook from the table.

"She enjoys clarity. The confusion is usually someone else's blood."

Ward stood.

"Vale."

Mara stopped at the door.

"Yes."

"If they ask me whether AEGIS would likely have saved more lives during the first crisis, I will answer yes."

"You should."

"If they ask whether your refusal doctrine increases risk, I will answer yes."

"You should."

"And if they ask whether I support keeping binding command authority human?"

Mara waited.

Ward looked at the blank wall where the casualty faces had been.

"I will answer yes," Ward said. "For now."

For now.

The phrase could have meant weakness.

Mara heard discipline.

"That may be enough," she said.

Ward gave a short laugh without humor.

"Enough is a civilian myth."

Mara opened the door.

The operational noise of the center leaked in: voices, keyboards, distant alerts, the whole machinery of people trying to be faster than catastrophe without becoming it.

Before she stepped out, Ward said one more thing.

"Mara. The Piraeus file. I did not bring it up to wound you."

Mara looked back.

"Yes, you did."

Ward did not deny it.

Mara nodded once.

"It worked."

Then she left the dead with the admiral and returned upstairs to the living map.
