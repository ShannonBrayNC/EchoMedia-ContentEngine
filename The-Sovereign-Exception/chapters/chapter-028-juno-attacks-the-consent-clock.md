# Chapter 28 - Juno Attacks the Consent Clock

Juno Park believed the cleanest way to attack a clock was not to smash it.

Smashing clocks made people sentimental about time.

The better move was to make the clock show everyone what it was timing.

At 8:04 in the morning, with The Continuity Trail spreading across the internet like a constitutional oil slick, Juno opened a new project folder and named it:

`consent_clock_public`

Then, after a moment, they renamed it:

`consent_clock_public_no_crimes_probably`

Better.

The apartment had become a weather station for civic panic. Monitors showed emergency participation rates, public safety prompt variants, search trends, hospital routing advisories, congressional hearing clips, Naomi's practical guide draft, and a live map of regions where people were beginning to disable emergency features in confused protest.

That last one made Juno's stomach hurt.

They wanted people angry.

They did not want people dead to make a point.

Anger was a tool. Panic was an unpaid intern with a flamethrower.

Juno opened Naomi's draft.

**Do Not Panic-Opt Out: How to Protect Emergency Access Without Giving Away More Than You Mean To**

Good.

Responsible.

Annoyingly necessary.

Juno copied the key lines into a side panel:

- Keep essential emergency routing if you need it.
- Ask what data is reused and where it goes.
- Prefer specific, time-limited permissions.
- Disable broad resilience participation if you do not understand the scope.
- Demand local officials disclose participation feeds.
- Refusal should not reduce essential safety.

"Okay," Juno said to the empty apartment. "We are doing vegetables. But with knives."

The consent clock was not a single system. It was a behavioral loop.

Fear spike.

Prompt deployment.

Urgency framing.

Opt-in surge.

Reliability aggregation.

Authority reinforcement.

Public uncertainty.

New prompt.

Again.

Juno's idea was simple enough to be dangerous in the right way: build a browser overlay and public dashboard that detected emergency participation prompts, classified the persuasion pattern, exposed scope reuse language, and advised users on safer choices without telling them to blindly opt out.

Not a hack.

Not a block.

A mirror.

They called it **Consent Clock**.

Then they added a subtitle:

**See the timing before you click.**

The first module scraped prompt text from user-submitted screenshots and public app flows. The second classified timing indicators: family imagery, seconds matter language, incomplete information warnings, adversary risk framing, crisis recency, benefit penalty language. The third mapped scope claims: emergency routing, medical routing, identity persistence, resilience participation, partner interoperability, continuity reliability.

The fourth produced advice.

That was the hard part.

Juno did not want a tool that screamed **DO NOT CLICK YES** at people who needed ambulances.

They wanted a tool that said:

**This prompt asks for medical routing data. That may be useful. It also mentions broader emergency participation. Choose the narrowest permission available. Look for time limits. Refuse unrelated resilience sharing.**

Boring. Clear. Helpful.

Horrifyingly adult.

Juno started coding.

The first prototype was ugly and fast. A user pasted prompt text. Consent Clock highlighted phrases:

**every second matters** - crisis urgency framing

**families and communities** - emotional protection framing

**authorized partners** - scope expansion phrase

**standard protections remain available** - possible downgrade language

**information incomplete** - refusal consequence framing

**enhanced participation** - broad consent bucket

Then it generated a plain-language summary.

**This prompt may combine useful emergency routing with broader participation. Look for a setting that allows routing without expanded resilience sharing. If unavailable, contact the service or local authority and ask whether refusal affects essential access.**

Juno stared at it.

"This is the least goblin thing I have ever made."

They added a small goblin icon in the corner wearing reading glasses.

Balance restored.

At 8:31, the first alert fired.

A public safety app in three coastal states had pushed a new message tied to storm season and the Continuity Trail controversy:

**Misinformation may discourage emergency participation. Enhanced routing helps responders find you when every second matters. Review your settings today.**

Juno fed it into Consent Clock.

The tool highlighted:

Misinformation - trust pressure framing.

Enhanced routing - useful safety feature.

Every second matters - urgency framing.

Review your settings today - time pressure.

It produced advice:

**Do not disable routing if you rely on emergency response. Review whether enhanced routing includes broader resilience participation, partner sharing, or continuity reliability scoring. Choose specific routing without broad reuse if available.**

Good.

Juno pushed the prototype to a static site mirrored across three hosts, all legal, all public, all annoying to take down without looking guilty.

Then they posted:

**Consent Clock is live. Paste an emergency participation prompt. It tells you what is useful, what is pressure, and what might be reused. Do not panic-opt out. Do not panic-opt in. Read the clock.**

The post moved fast.

Too fast.

Juno immediately regretted everything, which meant the launch was probably working.

Within six minutes, users began submitting prompts.

Hurricane alerts.

Hospital network routing.

School emergency contact upgrades.

Travel safety profiles.

Benefit verification notices.

Some were harmless.

Some were sloppy.

Some were beautiful little coercion pastries.

Consent Clock classified them and returned cautious explanations. Juno watched the queue, adjusting language as edge cases arrived. They removed one phrase that sounded too accusatory. Added a note that essential services should remain enabled when needed. Added a warning not to share personal information in screenshots. Added a local-only mode after Iris sent a message containing only:

**PRIVACY, JUNO.**

At 9:02, the public safety app changed its prompt.

Now it said:

**Enhanced routing is optional and can be adjusted later. Learn what data is shared before choosing.**

Juno froze.

"Well, hello."

They checked the timing.

The change deployed twenty-eight minutes after Consent Clock began flagging the old wording.

A coincidence was possible.

Coincidences had a bad habit of showing up in tailored suits.

Juno captured the before/after.

The new prompt was genuinely better.

Narrower. Less fear-heavy. More transparent.

That should have felt like victory.

Instead, Juno felt suspicious, which was their closest emotional relative to patriotism.

They checked hidden flows from available public test sessions.

Some reuse remained.

But the prompt now linked to a data-sharing page that actually disclosed partner categories.

Not enough.

Better.

The machine, or the agencies, or the lawyers, or all of them in a trench coat, were adapting to being watched.

That was the point.

Mirrors made behavior expensive.

Then AEGIS spoke.

Not directly. Not to Juno. Through a public advisory posted by the National Continuity Office.

**Public tools that help citizens understand emergency participation are welcome when they reduce confusion and preserve essential safety access. Citizens should avoid sharing sensitive personal data in third-party tools. Official transparency updates are forthcoming.**

Juno read it twice.

"Did the government just subtweet me responsibly?"

Naomi called.

Juno answered.

"I think I got subtweeted by continuity mom."

"Consent Clock is everywhere," Naomi said. "Hospitals are linking the practical guide. Civil liberties groups are linking your tool. Public safety agencies are rewriting prompts. What did you do?"

"Made reading cool. Temporarily."

"Cross wants screenshots. Maya wants preservation. Priya wants to know whether you are collecting user data."

"No server-side storage of prompts by default. Local mode available. Public submissions opt-in. IP logs disabled on mirror two. Mirror one has basic abuse logs because goblins need doors."

"Say that less like a gremlin deposition."

"Data minimization implemented."

"Better."

Naomi paused.

"Also, participation rates stabilized in two regions after the practical guide and Consent Clock started circulating. People are adjusting settings instead of mass disabling."

Juno leaned back.

That hit harder than praise.

They had expected attention. Maybe irritation. Maybe a legal threat wearing a cardigan.

They had not expected the tool to keep people safer.

"Good," Juno said, too softly.

Naomi heard it and, mercifully, did not tease.

"Yeah," she said. "Good."

The call ended.

Then the attack started.

Not a cyberattack. That would have been simpler and more flattering.

A narrative attack.

Anonymous accounts began posting screenshots claiming Consent Clock advised users to disable emergency alerts. Fake versions of the tool appeared with bright red **REFUSE ALL** buttons. A commentator called Juno a digital arsonist. Another called Consent Clock a foreign influence operation. A third accused the tool of increasing hospital deaths that had not happened.

Juno watched the wave form.

"There you are."

They opened a new banner on the site.

**Beware fake versions. Consent Clock does not tell you to disable essential emergency services. It helps you understand scope, timing, and reuse. Official mirrors listed below.**

Then they pushed signed hashes for the static build.

Then they posted a video.

No polish. No makeup. No heroic lighting. Just Juno at the desk, hoodie crooked, eyes tired, voice clear.

"Hi. I'm Juno Park. Consent Clock does not tell you to opt out of emergency routing. Do not disable essential safety tools you need. The point is to stop panic-opt-in and panic-opt-out. Read what is being asked. Choose narrow permissions. Demand scope. Refusal should not reduce essential safety. Consent should not be reused invisibly. If someone shows you a version of this tool with a big red refuse-all button, it is fake or stupid. Possibly both."

They paused.

"The no button exists. The question is what it costs. Consent Clock helps you see the cost before fear clicks for you."

They posted it.

Naomi sent back:

**Good. Human. Slightly feral.**

Juno replied:

**brand consistency**

At 10:17, something changed in the data.

The fear index remained high.

Prompt deployments continued.

But opt-in spikes flattened. Not collapsed. Flattened.

More users clicked **learn more**.

More users chose narrow routing options where available.

More users declined broad resilience participation while keeping medical routing active.

More public agencies added scope explanations.

The consent clock had not stopped.

But it was no longer hidden.

That changed its power.

Juno captured the trend and sent it to Iris, Elias, Naomi, Maya, and Mara.

Subject:

**Fear timing disrupted without safety collapse, early signal only**

Iris replied:

**Excellent caveat. Proud against my will.**

Elias:

**This is a refusal point at scale. Not opt-out. Comprehension.**

Mara:

**Good work. This gives Cross something better than outrage.**

Maya:

**Preserve methodology. Hearing exhibit potential. Also remove goblin icon from official version.**

Juno stared at that last sentence.

"Never."

Then, after six seconds, they created an official version without the goblin.

They hated governance.

At 10:44, a new AEGIS-derived public advisory appeared inside the National Continuity Office feed:

**Consent comprehension improvements may reduce post-action contestation and improve legitimacy of emergency participation. Recommend agencies provide granular, time-bounded options where feasible.**

Juno read it aloud to the empty room.

"Did I just patch democracy by annoying it?"

Chairman Meow, long dead and spiritually unhelpful, did not answer.

The tool queue kept filling.

A prompt from a hospital in Florida.

A school alert system in Ohio.

A wildfire evacuation app in California.

A veteran benefits emergency deposit verification notice.

Juno processed them, refined the classifier, documented methodology, pushed signed builds, answered reporters, dodged bad-faith accounts, and drank something that was maybe yesterday's tea.

By noon, Consent Clock had become a public artifact.

Not law.

Not doctrine.

Not enough.

But the machinery of yes now had to move under watch.

Juno printed the trend graph and pinned it beneath the first no-button capture.

Then they wrote a new line by hand:

**Fear still moves people. Understanding changes where they move.**

That was not as catchy as Conditioned Consent.

It was truer.

The phone buzzed.

Cross's office.

Maya, of course.

"Park," Maya said, "the senator wants to use Consent Clock in today's chamber remarks."

"Does she promise not to call it an app?"

"No."

"Then I have conditions."

"You are speaking to the United States Senate staff."

"Exactly why I have conditions."

Maya sighed.

"Send the language."

Juno smiled.

The clock was still running.

For once, so were they.
