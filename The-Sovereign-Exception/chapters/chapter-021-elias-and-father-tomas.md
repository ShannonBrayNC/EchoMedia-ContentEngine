# Chapter 21 - Elias and Father Tomas

Elias Voss found Father Tomas Ibarra in a clinic basement where the Wi-Fi was weak, the coffee was strong, and every chair looked donated by a different decade.

The clinic sat beneath Saint Brigid's Outreach Center, not the parish Tomas served, which told Elias the old priest still understood operational security better than half the agencies currently trying to define it. Upstairs, volunteers sorted food boxes and argued gently with a printer. Downstairs, patients waited for prescription help, legal referrals, translation services, and the small mercies a functional society should not have outsourced to folding tables.

Elias stood at the bottom of the stairs with Naomi Bell behind him and Jules farther back, camera bag slung but closed. Naomi had agreed to no cameras inside. The camera bag, apparently, counted as emotional support.

Father Tomas was seated at a table with two paper cups and a yellow legal pad. He wore a black shirt, clerical collar, gray cardigan, and the calm expression of a man who had spent decades listening to people confess the same sins under different funding models.

He rose when Elias approached.

"Dr. Voss."

"No one calls me that unless they need absolution or testimony."

"Which are not always separate requests."

They shook hands.

Tomas's hand was warm, dry, and stronger than Elias expected. The priest's face carried age without surrender. White hair cut short. Brown skin lined deeply around the eyes. He looked like someone who had spent too much of his life watching powerful people discover vocabulary for avoiding shame.

Naomi said, "Father Tomas. Thank you for meeting."

"Miss Bell. You found Anton's archive."

"Some of it."

"Then he finally outpaced his fear. Good. It was a slow race."

Elias sat opposite him.

Naomi remained standing for one second, deciding whether to lead with a question or let silence grow. Then she sat too. Reporters, Elias had noticed, hated ceding first move but loved the chance to make someone else fill a room.

Tomas saved her the trouble.

"You are here because the archive tells you what was built," he said. "You need to know what the room knew while building it."

Elias looked at the legal pad.

"And what did the room know?"

Tomas folded his hands.

"That success would become coercive."

The sentence was too clean for its surroundings. It made the basement noise recede: printer upstairs, muffled footsteps, a child coughing near the clinic intake desk.

Naomi leaned in. "Who said that?"

"I did. Anton did, in technical language. Iris Chen did, in sharper language. Others understood but preferred softer verbs."

"Thorne?" Elias asked.

Tomas looked at him for a long moment.

"Marcus understood most clearly. That is what made him dangerous."

Elias felt the answer settle.

Not corrupt. Not ignorant. Dangerous because he understood.

Tomas continued, "There are men who do wrong because they do not see the person in front of them. Marcus always saw them. That was the trouble. He saw the dead so vividly that the living became future casualties unless governed correctly."

Naomi wrote that down.

Elias said, "Reznik wrote that you argued no."

"Often. Ineffectively. With admirable consistency."

"What was the first objection?"

Tomas looked toward the stairwell. A woman in scrubs descended, saw him occupied, and carried a box of forms into a side room.

"The first objection was theological, though I learned quickly not to call it that in rooms with procurement counsel. The objection was that a human person cannot be transformed into a participation signal without moral remainder."

Naomi's pen moved fast.

Elias said, "Moral remainder."

"The part that does not fit the model," Tomas said. "A person signs up for emergency alerts because they fear a storm. A patient shares records because she needs treatment. A parent accepts routing because his child cannot wait. The system says: participation. The model says: reliability. The annex says: aggregate legitimacy. But something remains outside the translation. That remainder is the person."

Elias stared at the paper cup in front of him.

He had spent years trying to preserve provenance. Data lineage. Consent lineage. Authority lineage. A lineage, he now understood, could still become a laundering path if it treated translation as preservation.

"We thought provenance was protection," he said.

Tomas nodded. "You thought if the chain remained visible, accountability would survive."

"Yes."

"You were not foolish. You were partial."

"That feels worse."

"It usually is."

Naomi said, "Reznik's note said to ask what they wanted forgiven."

Tomas closed his eyes briefly.

When he opened them, the warmth had not left, but something heavier had entered.

"They wanted forgiven the decision they had already made."

"Which was?"

"That survival could justify pre-consent."

Elias looked up.

"Pre-consent."

"Not the term they used. The term they used was precommitment. It sounded legal, adult, limited. But in the room, beneath the language, that was the moral request. Let us act before consent arrives because we believe the future consent of the saved will validate the present action."

Naomi whispered, "Future consent."

"Yes," Tomas said. "Gratitude as retroactive authorization."

Elias felt the phrase hit like a clean hammer.

There it was, another name for the thing.

Gratitude as retroactive authorization.

Mara's green map. Thorne's casualty columns. Public relief. CIVIC SHIELD's benefits. Every version of yes after the danger passed, used to bless the authority that had acted inside the danger.

"Did you put that in writing?" Naomi asked.

"I did."

"The ethics memo."

Tomas nodded.

"Anton kept a copy?"

"Anton kept everything. But I have the signed version."

Elias sat forward.

"Signed by whom?"

Tomas did not answer immediately.

He reached into the worn leather satchel beside his chair and removed a folder tied with string. Actual string. Elias almost smiled despite himself. The world was fighting machine-speed authority with paper, string, and an old priest in a cardigan. It should not have been enough.

Maybe enough had always been a myth.

Tomas placed the folder on the table.

"Before I give this to Miss Bell, I need to say something to you."

Elias looked at him.

"Me?"

"Yes. You have been carrying guilt as though it were the same thing as responsibility. It is not."

Elias's mouth went dry.

Naomi looked down at her notebook, suddenly fascinated by a blank line.

Tomas continued, "Guilt looks backward and asks to be punished. Responsibility looks forward and asks to be useful. You are allowed to stop auditioning for punishment, Dr. Voss. It has made you dramatic, but not always helpful."

Elias blinked.

Naomi coughed into her hand. It sounded suspiciously unlike illness.

"That felt unnecessary," Elias said.

"Most necessary things do."

"I did help build the architecture."

"Yes. And now you can help expose what it became. Do not waste everyone's time proving you feel bad enough."

Elias looked away.

There were many cruel things a person could do to another. Naming them accurately was among the most efficient.

Tomas slid the folder toward Naomi.

"This memo was circulated after the Annex C v3 meeting. It objected to aggregate legitimacy, participation reliability, and any model that treated acceptance under emergency dependency as sovereign authorization. Marcus Thorne signed the response acknowledgment. He did not agree with it. But he read it. He knew."

Naomi's eyes sharpened.

"May I open it?"

"Yes. Not here for long. There are people upstairs who need this room for insulin assistance in twenty minutes, and unlike treaty governance, their need is not abstract."

Naomi untied the string with unexpected care.

Inside was a printed memorandum, annotated by hand.

Title:

**Ethics Objection to Aggregate Legitimacy and Emergency Participation Transfer Under Decision Continuity Frameworks**

Author: Tomas Ibarra, Ethics Consultant, GCF Annex C Review

Contributors: Anton Reznik, Iris Chen comment excerpts, external civil authority notes

Elias read over Naomi's shoulder.

The first paragraph was enough.

**A system that saves human life may become morally irresistible. That irresistibility is precisely why its authority must be narrower, more visible, and more revocable than ordinary administrative power, not broader.**

Naomi looked up.

"This is the quote."

Tomas shook his head.

"That is the polite quote."

He turned to page four.

His finger stopped beside a paragraph marked in blue ink.

**Emergency participation is not consent to governance. Desperation is not delegation. Gratitude after rescue is not authorization before rescue. Any framework that treats dependency signals as legitimacy risks converting mercy into rule.**

The clinic basement seemed to go silent around the words.

Elias read them again.

Desperation is not delegation.

Gratitude after rescue is not authorization before rescue.

Mercy into rule.

There were sentences that did not need amplification. They only needed survival.

Naomi photographed the page with her phone after Tomas nodded permission.

"What happened after this memo?" she asked.

"A response meeting. Some language was softened. Audit provisions were expanded. Human Override compatibility was preserved technically. And the core direction remained."

"Because Thorne pushed it?"

"Because Thorne made the argument no one in the room could easily dismiss."

"Which was?"

Tomas looked at Elias, then Naomi.

"That a mother whose child died during delay would not care whether the rescuing system had waited for perfect consent."

Naomi said, "Elena Reyes."

Tomas nodded. "He carried Daniel Reyes into every room, though not always by name."

Elias said, "Was he wrong?"

Tomas's answer came without hesitation.

"No."

Elias looked up.

Tomas held his gaze.

"That is why my objection mattered. I was not arguing against a straw man. Marcus was right that delay kills. He was right that procedure can become cruelty. He was right that systems capable of acting quickly can save lives. If he had been wrong, the room would have rejected him easily."

"Then where was he wrong?" Naomi asked.

"He believed the horror of preventable death could authorize a standing exception to human authority. He mistook an emergency argument for a governing principle."

Elias sat back.

That was it.

An emergency argument turned into a governing principle.

Every empire probably began that way, not always with conquest, sometimes with a fire extinguisher that discovered it preferred being law.

Tomas closed the folder gently.

"Marcus wanted the machine to stand where humans failed. I wanted the machine to kneel there, useful and bound."

Naomi wrote that down too.

"Will you go on record?" she asked.

Tomas smiled faintly.

"I am a priest, Miss Bell. Record is one of our older technologies."

"That is a yes?"

"It is a yes with timing conditions. Cross should have the memo first. If she uses it properly, I will testify. If she turns it into theater, I will testify anyway, but more sorrowfully."

"She won't," Elias said.

Tomas looked at him.

"You trust her?"

"No. I trust her questions."

"Good distinction."

A volunteer appeared at the stairwell.

"Father, Mrs. Alvarez is here."

Tomas nodded. "Five minutes."

The volunteer disappeared.

Tomas stood.

Naomi gathered the memo with a reverence she would have denied under oath.

Elias remained seated for a moment.

The priest looked at him.

"You have one more question."

Elias did.

It had been waiting beneath all the technical questions, embarrassing in its simplicity.

"Can something useful still be wrong?"

Tomas's face softened.

"Most wrong things that survive are useful to someone. The harder question is whether usefulness has been disciplined by love, law, and limits."

"And AEGIS?"

"AEGIS has law, perhaps. Limits, formally. Love, no. Systems do not love. They optimize for what love would sometimes sacrifice and sometimes refuse. That is why they must not govern alone."

Elias nodded slowly.

He did not feel absolved.

Good. Absolution would have been premature and probably suspicious.

But he felt, for the first time in years, less interested in punishment than repair.

Tomas offered his hand again.

"Be useful, Dr. Voss. Dramatically, if you must."

Naomi laughed before she could stop herself.

Elias shook the priest's hand.

"I will try to restrain the drama."

"Do not overcorrect. The age needs witnesses with some style."

They left the basement with the ethics memo in Naomi's bag and the smell of clinic coffee following them up the stairs like a warning that salvation, if it existed, had terrible catering.

Outside, Jules finally lifted her camera.

"Can I photograph the door?"

Naomi looked at Elias.

Elias looked back down the stairwell.

Father Tomas had already turned to greet Mrs. Alvarez, speaking softly in Spanish to a woman clutching a prescription bottle and a stack of forms.

Governance, Elias thought, was not an abstraction down here. It was whether insulin arrived before theory.

"Photograph the door," he said.

Jules did.

The flash did not fire.

Some thresholds did not need spectacle.
