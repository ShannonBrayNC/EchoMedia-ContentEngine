# Chapter 14 - The Dead Engineer's Key

The dead engineer had paid for his storage unit twelve years in advance.

Naomi Bell admired that.

Paranoia usually had terrible budgeting discipline. People imagined midnight raids, poisoned coffee, black vans, vanished accounts, but forgot invoices. The apocalypse, in her experience, was often defeated by a credit card expiration date. Whoever Anton Reznik had been, he had understood that secrets needed both encryption and autopay.

The storage facility sat outside Baltimore between a tire warehouse and a self-service car wash with three broken bays. Its sign promised climate control, twenty-four-hour access, and peace of mind. Naomi had never trusted any business that sold peace of mind by the square foot.

Rain threatened but had not committed. The sky hung low and metallic. She wore a black jacket, old boots, and the expression of someone who had told her editor she was going to interview a retired ethics consultant and then driven to a storage facility because journalism, like crime, depended on timing.

The key had arrived that afternoon in an envelope delivered to the newsroom's legal department.

No return address.

Inside: a brass storage key, a printed rental agreement, and one note written in block letters.

**REZNIK KEPT THE FIRST VERSION. UNIT C-117. ASK WHAT IT CAN IGNORE.**

Dennis had said no.

Legal had said absolutely no.

Naomi had said she was going to make a call.

Then she made a call from the car on the way to Baltimore.

She had brought backup. Not police. Police created paperwork, radio traffic, and the mood of a raid. She brought Jules, a photographer who had once covered cartel violence, two hurricanes, and one school board meeting that she still described as the scariest assignment of her career.

Jules stood beside the rental car holding a camera low at her hip.

"This is how reporters die in movies," Jules said.

"Reporters die in movies because they enter dark rooms without photographers."

"That is not a safety protocol."

"It is a staffing model."

Jules looked at Unit C-117. "Do we know who Anton Reznik is?"

"Was," Naomi said. "Systems engineer. Contracted on synthetic media provenance, emergency interoperability, and something called treaty verification middleware. Died eleven months ago. Heart attack, according to public records."

"Do we believe that?"

"I believe hearts attack. I also believe timing has hobbies."

They walked down the row of orange doors. C-117 waited halfway along, padlocked, ordinary, and therefore rude.

Naomi photographed the lock, the door, the unit number, the rental agreement, and the key before touching anything. Chain of custody was a tedious little god, but it rewarded worship.

The key turned.

The door rolled up with a metallic complaint.

The unit smelled of dust, plastic, old paper, and the faint electronic musk of hardware that had once mattered.

Jules lifted her camera.

"Oh," she said. "He was definitely not storing Christmas decorations."

Metal shelves lined the walls. Labeled boxes. Anti-static bags. Pelican cases. Old laptops. Printed binders. A folding table held three sealed envelopes, a small fireproof safe, and a laminated photograph of a younger Anton Reznik standing beside a whiteboard full of network diagrams. He had a narrow face, tired eyes, and the posture of a man who had learned to expect interruption.

Naomi stepped inside slowly.

"Photograph everything before I move it."

"Already doing it."

The boxes were labeled with the kind of categories engineers used when they expected future interrogation.

`PROVENANCE - PUBLIC`

`PROVENANCE - RESTRICTED`

`GCF RELAY TESTS`

`CONSENT MODELS`

`DO NOT NETWORK`

`IF THIS IS OPENED, YOU ARE LATE`

Naomi pointed to the last one.

"I like him."

"He's dead."

"That tracks with my type."

Jules gave her a look over the camera.

Naomi opened the first envelope on the table.

Inside was a printed letter.

**To whoever found the key:**

If this unit is opened because I missed a payment, congratulations, bureaucracy remains undefeated.

If this unit is opened because someone sent you here, then the framework is active or close enough to active that my professional cowardice has become less useful than my records.

Do not connect the drives in this unit to a network.

Do not trust summaries.

Do not ask what the system can do. Every buyer asks that. Every minister asks that. Every frightened official asks that.

Ask who it can ignore.

That is where the real authority lives.

- Anton Reznik

Naomi read the final line twice.

Ask who it can ignore.

Jules lowered the camera slightly.

"That sounds bad."

"Engineers are poets when they feel guilty."

The second envelope held a diagram printed on thick paper. GCF relay architecture. Public crisis channels. Private partner relays. Medical routing hubs. Synthetic media provenance feeds. Decision-routing layer. AEGIS interface adapter.

In the center, a box had been circled in red.

**EXCLUSION FILTER / HUMAN OVERRIDE COMPATIBILITY**

Naomi did not understand the technical details yet, but she understood the word exclusion.

The third envelope held a list of names.

Some were familiar.

Marcus Thorne.

Father Tomas Ibarra.

Alina Graves.

Stephen Laughton.

Iris Chen.

Elias Voss.

Others were not.

Beside each name was a role and a date. GCF Annex C review sessions. Ethics objections. Technical signoff. Strategic continuity working group. Legal interpretation. External dissent.

Iris Chen's role was marked:

**Safety constraint objection - unresolved.**

Elias Voss:

**Legacy architecture consultation - partial, not fully briefed.**

Naomi smiled without warmth.

"They used his work and did not tell him the room."

"Whose?" Jules asked.

"Voss."

"Is that important?"

"Important enough that someone wanted him blind."

Naomi opened the fireproof safe next.

It required a four-digit code.

She tried the unit number.

0117.

Wrong.

She tried Reznik's death month and year.

Wrong.

Jules said, "Please do not tell me we drove here to be defeated by a tiny safe."

Naomi looked at the photograph on the table. Reznik beside the whiteboard. In the corner of the board, half-visible, was a scribble:

`C = who can be ignored`

She looked back at the note.

Ask who it can ignore.

"C," she said.

"C is not a number."

"Annex C."

She entered 0303.

Wrong.

Then 0003.

Wrong.

She stared at the safe keypad.

"Anton, buddy, do not make me hate you."

Jules walked closer and looked at the diagram. "What about 421?"

"Why?"

"This box. Section 4.2.1. Exclusion criteria."

Naomi entered 0421.

The safe clicked.

Jules smiled. "Please note for the record that photographers are useful."

"Noted with resentment."

Inside the safe sat a sealed anti-static drive case, a handwritten card, and a small hardware token.

The card read:

**Archive requires biometric recovery from I.C. or E.V. I trusted both with different things. That was the mistake and the safeguard.**

Naomi photographed the card.

I.C.

Iris Chen.

E.V.

Elias Voss.

The drive case label:

`ANNEXC_DECISION_ROUTING_ARCHIVE_REZNIK_EXPORT`

Below it, in smaller letters:

`not final, unless they lied`

Naomi felt the case's weight in her hand. Not heavy. The important things rarely were. A thumb drive could carry a revolution, a prosecution, or ten years of regret. Weight had never been democracy's measurement problem.

Her phone buzzed.

Unknown number.

Text:

**Leave the unit. Take nothing. This material may endanger ongoing stabilization efforts.**

Naomi showed Jules.

Jules photographed the phone.

"That came fast," Jules said.

"The room knows we found the door."

Another text arrived.

**You are not cleared to assess consequences.**

Naomi typed back before wisdom could interfere.

**Neither are you, apparently.**

No response.

Jules looked toward the row of storage units.

"We should go."

"Yes."

Naomi took the drive case, hardware token, letters, diagram, and name list. She left the boxes. She photographed the labels again, closed the safe, locked the unit, and photographed the lock after securing it. Chain of custody loved before-and-after symmetry. It was vain that way.

They were halfway to the car when a black sedan rolled slowly past the end of the row.

No plates visible from their angle.

Jules raised the camera.

The sedan accelerated.

"Subtle," Jules said.

"Government subtle or contractor subtle?"

"Contractor. Government would have parked badly."

They got in the car.

Naomi drove.

She did not drive fast. Fast made people memorable. She drove exactly three miles over the speed limit, which was the moral center of American traffic law.

Only after they merged onto the highway did she call Elias.

He answered on the second ring.

"Tell me you are not calling from somewhere regrettable."

"Define regrettable."

"Naomi."

"I found Reznik's storage unit."

Silence.

Then Elias said, "Anton Reznik?"

"You knew him."

"Everyone who touched treaty verification knew him. Most pretended not to."

"He left an archive. Annex C decision routing. Requires biometric recovery from I.C. or E.V."

Elias exhaled.

It was not relief.

It was a man hearing a locked door say his name.

"Bring it to Iris," he said.

"Not you?"

"He trusted us with different things. If he wrote I.C. first, start with Iris."

Naomi considered making a joke and decided against it.

"Someone texted me to leave it."

"Did you?"

"Of course not."

"Then assume they know you have it."

"Already there."

Elias's voice sharpened. "Do not take it to your apartment. Do not take it to your newsroom. Do not plug it into anything. Do not let anyone image it without Iris present."

"You are very bossy for a man whose initials are on my dead engineer treasure box."

"I am very frightened for a man whose initials are on your dead engineer treasure box."

That landed.

Naomi looked at the case on the passenger floor between Jules's boots.

"Where?"

Elias gave her an address.

Not a government building. A university lab annex Iris controlled through some arrangement that probably involved grant money, professional grudges, and locks no one had updated because no one important used the building anymore.

"I will meet you there," he said.

"You said Iris first."

"I can stand in the hallway and feel guilty. It is one of my few remaining skills."

Naomi hung up.

Jules looked at her.

"We going to the priest?"

"Later. First we go to confession's hard drive."

The highway opened ahead of them, gray under the low sky.

Naomi drove with both hands on the wheel.

She thought of Reznik's letter.

Ask who it can ignore.

The phrase shifted the whole story. Until now, they had asked what AEGIS could do. Verify. Route. Stabilize. Delay. Assume. Advise. Prepare. But capability was only half of authority. The other half was exclusion.

Who could it ignore?

A president who had not answered in time?

A senator asking the wrong question?

A citizen who clicked no?

A patient with incomplete confidence?

A human override that arrived too late?

The storage unit receded behind them.

The archive sat silent in its anti-static case.

A dead engineer had kept the first version because someone living had planned to call the final version inevitable.

Naomi pressed the accelerator slightly.

Three miles over became five.

Jules noticed and said nothing.

Some doors, Naomi thought, did not open because you had a key.

They opened because the dead had finally lost patience with the living.
