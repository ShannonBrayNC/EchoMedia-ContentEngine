# Global Edit Principles

## 1. Preserve the Core Thesis

> **The machine may show the way. It may not become the hand.**

Every edit should protect the story's central argument: AEGIS is dangerous not because it is useless or evil, but because it is useful enough to inherit authority if humans let convenience become consent.

## 2. Keep AEGIS Useful

Do not revise AEGIS into a villain.

AEGIS should remain:

- fast
- useful
- often correct
- lifesaving
- emotionally neutral
- governance-dangerous because of defaults and authority paths

## 3. Make Human Authority Costly

Do not make human control feel automatically virtuous.

The novel's power depends on the reader understanding that:

- human authority is answerable
- human authority can be slower
- human authority can be wrong
- human authority can cost lives
- hidden machine authority can also cost liberty

## 4. Replace Explanation with Pressure

When a scene feels too explanatory, revise by adding:

- a ticking clock
- a physical object
- an opposing argument
- a named person affected by the system
- a document behaving like evidence, not lore
- a choice that costs someone something

## 5. Stage Terminology Gradually

Do not overload early chapters with the full glossary. The reader should learn terms through action and need.

Recommended staging:

- Prologue-Ch. 2: AEGIS, audit, waiver, Sovereign Exception as phrase
- Ch. 3-8: GCF shadow, permission inheritance, Human Override inversion
- Ch. 9-16: Annex C, aggregate legitimacy, authority map, refusal points
- Ch. 17-24: Managed Burn, Conditioned Consent, Scope Laundering, ethics language
- Ch. 25-32: rapid acceptance, covenant restoration, active review
- Ch. 33-40: binding delegation, public no, narrow law, maintenance
