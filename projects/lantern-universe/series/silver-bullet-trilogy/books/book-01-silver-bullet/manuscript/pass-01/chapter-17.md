# Chapter 17 — The Process Does Not Know Him

The form had no field for what mattered.

Jack stared at the dropdown menu and felt a tired, irrational hatred for the word other. Other was where systems put the human remainder. Other was where pain went when it did not fit the billing code, the case type, the risk category, the workflow state.

The intake coordinator waited across the desk with patient eyes and a pen held over paper. She was not unkind. That made the scene harder to resent cleanly.

"Just choose the closest option," she said.

"Closest to what?"

"To the reason for the request."

Jack looked at the screen again. Workplace issue. Personal dispute. Safety concern. Documentation request. Other.

His life had become all of them and none of them.

"Other," he said.

The coordinator clicked. A text box opened.

"Brief description," she said.

Brief was another door closing.

Jack tried. He wrote one sentence. Deleted it. Wrote another. It sounded defensive. Deleted that too. Behind him, someone coughed in the waiting area. A phone rang. The building smelled like carpet cleaner and old paper.

Finally he typed:

`I need to preserve context around a sequence of events that has been misrepresented in social and professional settings.`

The coordinator read it, then nodded as if she understood enough to proceed and not enough to be burdened.

"Do you have supporting documents?"

Jack almost laughed. He had documents the way a drowning man had water.

"Yes," he said. "But they require context."

"You can upload attachments after submission."

Attachments. As if truth were a file size problem.

When he left, he sat in the truck with both hands on the wheel and let the parking lot settle around him. Inside, the process had been polite. It had also reduced him to a category and a text box. No one had meant harm. The harm had not needed intention.

He opened his phone and dictated a note while the engine idled.

"Process captures fragments. Process does not hold the person. Add context memo before uploading anything."

He stopped recording and listened to the note once. His voice sounded older than he felt.

Then he drove home slowly, because pain had made him late once and he refused to give the day another piece of himself for free.
