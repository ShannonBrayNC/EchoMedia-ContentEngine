# Chapter 5 — The Circle at the Edge

The phrase appeared three times before Jack let himself count it.

`We just want everyone safe.`

Maren said it first, or maybe only first where Jack could hear. Then a neighbor used a version of it beside the mailboxes, eyes turned slightly away as if direct contact might invite a transfer of guilt. Then it appeared in a group text from someone who had never used the word safe without talking about weather or traffic.

Three points made a line. Jack hated that his mind worked that way, but it had kept him alive through worse places than a suburban driveway.

He copied the phrases into his notes.

`safe / careful / patterns / concern`

Words like that should have been soft. On the page, they looked staged.

By evening, Jack had the sense of standing outside a house where all the windows were lit but every curtain was drawn. People were talking. He could feel the heat of it. He could not hear enough to answer.

At 6:12, a message arrived from Kendra, who usually sent memes, bad puns, or screenshots of impossible customer requests.

`Hey. I don't know what's going on, but maybe give people space for a little while.`

Jack looked at the message until the backlight dimmed.

Space. Another clean word. It meant absence when someone else requested it. It meant exile when you were the one being moved.

He typed, `Who is people?`

Deleted it.

Typed, `What did Evelyn say?`

Deleted that too.

He finally wrote, `I hear you. I also need you to understand there is context missing.`

The typing bubble appeared. Disappeared. Appeared again.

`I care about you. That's why I'm saying it.`

Concern with teeth.

Jack set the phone face down and pressed his fingers against his eyes until stars opened behind them. He could handle open hostility. Open hostility had a shape. This was different. This was kindness arranging the furniture in a cell.

He stood and walked the hallway because the body needed motion or it would turn the pressure inward. The house felt smaller than it had that morning. Each room carried some version of him: the office where he worked, the kitchen where he forgot meals, the bedroom where sleep came armed.

In the living room, he stopped before the dark television screen and saw his reflection: one shoulder held too high, one hand curled, face older than the day had any right to make it.

He said aloud, "I know what happened."

The room did not answer.

But the sentence existed now, in his own voice, and for the moment that had to count.
