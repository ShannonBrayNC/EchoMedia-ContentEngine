# Chapter 16 — The Body Betrays Him

Jack was late because his shoulder locked in the parking lot.

Not dramatically. There was no fall, no shout, no scene anyone would know how to interpret correctly. Just a flash of pain as he reached for the door handle, followed by a hard stillness that stole thirty seconds he did not have.

Thirty seconds became two minutes. Two minutes became the difference between arriving composed and arriving visibly strained.

The receptionist looked up when he entered. Her eyes flicked to his jaw, his hand, the sweat at his hairline. Jack saw the assessment happen. Pain translated into agitation. Strain translated into instability. A body doing its best became a witness for the wrong side.

"Mr. Mercer? They're ready for you."

Of course they were.

The room held three chairs, a water bottle, and the same careful air that now followed him everywhere. Jack lowered himself into the open seat and felt every eye register the way he protected the left side of his body.

"Are you all right to continue?" Harlan asked.

A decent question. A dangerous one.

If he said no, the meeting record would carry refusal. If he said yes, every wince afterward could be written as inconsistency. He imagined the note: appeared distressed, declined assistance, insisted on proceeding.

"I'm able to continue," he said. "For the record, I am managing a physical injury. I do not want that interpreted as emotional state."

The room changed again. Not much. Enough.

The note-taker wrote something down.

Jack wanted to ask what. He did not. Another demand could become another description. He drank water instead, though his hand shook slightly when he lifted the bottle.

The questions were procedural. The answers were factual. The pain moved underneath everything like a second conversation nobody else could hear.

Halfway through, Harlan asked him to explain a message thread. Jack did, carefully. He gave sequence. He gave missing context. He avoided adjectives. At the end, one of the men said, "I can see how someone might read it differently."

Jack felt the old heat rise.

"Differently from the full sequence," he said.

His tone sharpened on full. He heard it. So did they.

For the rest of the meeting, he kept one hand flat on the table and let the pain nail him in place.

Later, in his notes, he wrote:

`Physical pain affected timing and presentation. State clearly in context record.`

Then he added the harder sentence:

`My body can be used against me if context is missing.`
