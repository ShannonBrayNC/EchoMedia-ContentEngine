# Chapter 7 — Everyone Is Careful Now

By morning, the world had learned to speak around him.

Jack heard it in the way people stopped using names. They said situation. They said concern. They said people are trying to be thoughtful. Every phrase arrived wrapped in padding, as if softness could hide the impact.

At the grocery store, Mrs. Havel from two streets over saw him near the pharmacy aisle and turned her cart too quickly toward produce. Jack stood with a bottle of ibuprofen in his hand and watched her pretend not to hurry.

He told himself not to care.

His body cared anyway.

The old alertness came up through his spine and spread behind his eyes. Distance to exit. Three people between him and the door. Fluorescent light buzzing overhead. A child laughing somewhere behind him. His left shoulder tight enough to pull his neck out of alignment.

None of it was danger. All of it was data.

At home, he found a message from a client account manager waiting in his inbox.

`We are going to pause your direct involvement for now. This is not a judgment. Just a precaution while things are clarified.`

Not a judgment. Just a precaution.

Jack copied the phrase into his notes. Then he copied the timestamp. Then he sat back and let the anger move through him without touching the keyboard.

By afternoon, the silence had become more specific. People who usually answered fast answered late. People who usually joked answered clean. Nobody asked what happened. That was how he knew they thought they already knew enough.

He called Kendra anyway.

"I'm not asking you to take a side," he said.

"That's what everyone says when they want someone to take a side."

The answer landed harder because she sounded tired instead of cruel.

Jack pinched the bridge of his nose. "Then ask me a question. Any question that isn't already built around her version."

Kendra did not speak.

There it was: the unasked question. The one everybody carried like a glass they were afraid to drop.

"I don't know what to believe," she said finally.

Jack looked at the open file on his screen, at the growing list of phrases and dates and careful withdrawals. "That would feel better if people hadn't already started acting like they do."

He ended the call before his voice could give someone another thing to interpret.

In the silence afterward, he added a new heading to the timeline:

`Social temperature drop.`

It sounded clinical. It had not felt clinical. It had felt like discovering that every room had been warned about him before he arrived.
