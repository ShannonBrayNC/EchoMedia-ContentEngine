# Chapter 6 — The Shot Lands

The first consequence arrived in a calendar invite that had been removed.

Jack stared at the empty slot where the client call had been. He refreshed. Checked the archive. Checked the cancellation notice. There it was, formal and bloodless.

`Removed from attendee list.`

No explanation.

He sent one message to the project lead.

`I noticed I was removed from the 2 p.m. call. Was that intentional?`

The reply took seventeen minutes.

`Temporary. Just giving things room to settle.`

Room again. Space again. Settle, as if Jack had made turbulence by existing inside his own life.

He stood too quickly and pain flashed white from shoulder to jaw. For a second he saw only the edge of the desk and the red notification dot pulsing on the monitor. He lowered himself back into the chair before his legs finished deciding whether to hold.

Work had been the last room where the facts still mattered first. A permission issue was a permission issue. A log was a log. Either the script failed or it ran. Either the record existed or it did not. Now even that clean architecture had vents in it, and the story was moving through.

By late afternoon, two more doors had narrowed. A community meeting link no longer worked. A friend postponed dinner with language polished smooth by fear. Nobody said Evelyn's name. Nobody had to.

At 4:38, Jack opened the message from Evelyn again and compared it to the notes he had gathered. The contradiction was small enough to miss if you wanted to miss it.

She referenced a conversation as if it had occurred after an event.

It had occurred before.

Jack found the timestamp. Then the message. Then the log that placed him somewhere else when the implication required him to be available. Not a full answer. Not rescue. But a hard edge the story could not bend without breaking.

He copied the items into a folder called `context` and made three backups: local, encrypted drive, cloud. Then he added a plain text file:

`Contradiction 001:`

He wrote the sequence once, then again in simpler language. He removed adjectives. Removed anger. Removed anything a hostile reader could treat as performance.

At the bottom he typed:

`The story requires this order to be false.`

The sentence sat there, small and stubborn.

Outside, the neighborhood had gone gold with evening. Jack watched a car slow near his house and continue on. It was probably nothing. Most things were probably nothing until they formed a pattern.

He saved the file.

Act I had begun with a good day that should have held.

It ended with Jack understanding that the day had not broken by accident. Something had been aimed.
