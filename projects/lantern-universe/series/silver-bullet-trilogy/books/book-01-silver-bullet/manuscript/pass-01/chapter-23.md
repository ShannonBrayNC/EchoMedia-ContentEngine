# Chapter 23 — The Room Turns Inside Out

The office had always been the safest room because it asked the least of him emotionally.

Machines did not care how he looked when he was tired. Scripts did not mistake pain for guilt. Logs did not lower their voices when he entered. The office was where Jack went when human language became too slippery and he needed things that failed honestly.

Then the certified letter arrived.

He opened it at his desk and felt the room change around the paper. The letter did not say anything new. That was almost worse. It repeated the same smooth concerns in a more formal skin, requested patience, preserved options, avoided specifics. The office, which had held his work and order and usefulness, now held the language of removal.

Jack stood too fast and the chair rolled back hard into the wall.

The sound cracked through the room.

He froze, listening as if someone else had made it. His heart kicked once, then again. The monitors looked suddenly too bright. The folders on the screen looked less like order and more like proof of a life under siege.

He stepped into the hallway and left the letter on the desk.

For several minutes he stood in the kitchen with the lights off. The office glowed behind him, blue and white and wrong. He had thought safety belonged to places. It did not. Safety belonged to meaning, and meaning could be contaminated.

The letter had turned the room inside out.

Eventually he went back because avoidance had a cost too. He did not sit. He photographed the letter, scanned it, saved it, then placed the original in a physical folder. His hands moved with a care that felt almost ceremonial.

Then he wrote:

`Office no longer feels safe after formal letter. Place-state changed.`

The sentence was strange. He kept it.

Future Jack might need to know why he stopped working at that desk, why the laptop moved to the kitchen table, why a room full of tools became unusable for a time. The wound deserved continuity.

Before shutting down, he looked once more at the empty chair.

A room could remember what happened inside it.

So could a man.
