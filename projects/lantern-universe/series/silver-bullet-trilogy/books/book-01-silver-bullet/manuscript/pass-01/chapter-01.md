# Chapter 1 — The Good Day That Should Have Held

Jack Mercer trusted routines more than promises.

The first thing he did each morning was listen to the house. Not with fear, not exactly. Habit. A low mechanical hum from the refrigerator. The faint tick of the hallway vent. The old pop in his shoulder before he moved too fast and paid for it. Each sound told him the world still had edges.

He sat up slowly, let the pain arrive before he argued with it, and waited for his right hand to stop trembling from the effort of doing what used to be nothing. The doctors had given the injury names. Jack had given it procedures. Sit. Breathe. Feet on floor. Shoulder back. Do not reach without thinking.

On the desk beside the bed, his phone glowed with the usual overnight debris: work alerts, calendar reminders, a delivery notice, two messages he did not open yet. He read the sender lines, not the contents. Sender first. Timestamp second. Subject third. The order mattered. Order kept the world from turning into noise.

By seven, he had coffee, a heating pad under his shirt, and three monitors awake in front of him. Code windows, compliance notes, a half-finished diagram for a client who needed a system made understandable before it broke. That was what Jack did well. He found broken sequences and made them visible.

A message came in from a colleague asking if he could jump on a call. Jack typed back before the second chime.

`Give me ten. Reviewing the logs.`

He did not say his shoulder was locking. He did not say the pills made the morning feel wrapped in gauze. He did not say he had slept three hours and still woke like someone had called his name from another room. Competence was easier when it did not need a witness.

The call was ordinary. That was what he would remember later: how ordinary it had been. A migration timeline. A permission issue. A joke about how every urgent ticket had apparently been urgent since 2019. Jack laughed, and the laugh did not feel forced.

For a few minutes, he was only what he had built himself back into: useful, precise, alive in the work.

After the call, he wrote three notes in the project log. Not because he expected trouble. Because records mattered. A record was not truth, but it could keep truth from drowning.

At 9:42 a.m., he stepped outside to check the mailbox. The light had that clean late-morning angle that made the driveway look almost new. A neighbor lifted a hand. Jack lifted his back.

Normal. Painful, imperfect, but normal.

He would think about that word later. Normal was not safety. Normal was only the name people gave to a room before the door locked behind them.
