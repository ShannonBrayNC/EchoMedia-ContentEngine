# Chapter 20 — The Man in Their Story

Jack met the other Jack in a sentence someone thought was careful.

"I know you're trying to manage how this looks," Devin said.

They were standing outside a coffee shop neither of them had wanted to enter. Devin had asked to meet in person, which Jack had mistaken for courage. Maybe it was. Maybe courage had limits now.

"Manage how this looks?" Jack repeated.

Devin winced. "I don't mean it like that."

But someone did. That was the problem. Someone had built a version of Jack who managed appearances instead of preserving context. Someone had turned documentation into optics, restraint into calculation, pain into performance.

Jack could feel the other Jack standing beside him: colder, smoother, less wounded, more useful to the story.

"What do they think I am?" Jack asked.

Devin looked down at the sidewalk.

"Say it."

"They think you're controlling the narrative."

The laugh came before Jack could stop it. One short sound, sharp enough that a woman at the next table glanced over. Jack saw the glance and hated the laugh immediately.

There. Another piece for the wrong man.

He lowered his voice. "The narrative is controlling my life. I'm trying to preserve sequence."

"I believe you."

"No," Jack said, not unkindly. "You believe enough to feel bad. That's not the same."

Devin did not argue.

After they parted, Jack sat in his truck without starting it. The windows reflected his face in pieces. Glass in the side mirror. Glass in the phone. Glass in the dark screen of the dashboard.

He thought of every identity he had carried with effort: sailor, engineer, father, survivor, the man who fixed what others could not see. Now there was another identity circulating without his consent, and people were answering it as if it had arrived with credentials.

In his notes, he wrote:

`Public double: controlling, unstable, managing optics, unsafe to trust.`

Then he wrote below it:

`Self: preserving sequence under pressure.`

The two lines sat on the screen like opposing testimonies.

Jack did not know which one the world would choose.

He only knew which one was his to carry.
