# Chapter 15 — Jack Documents Everything

By the third night, Jack's folder system had become more honest than any room he entered.

He built it like infrastructure. Inbox exports. Screenshots. Call notes. Access logs. Message fragments. A directory for original items. A directory for context notes. A directory for questions that had no safe recipient yet.

The structure calmed him until it frightened him.

He sat back from the monitor and looked at the nested folders glowing on the screen. It was all defensible. It was also the kind of thing someone could photograph and call obsession.

That was the genius of the frame. It turned survival into a symptom.

Jack opened a new file called `why-this-record-exists.md` and wrote the sentence before he could talk himself out of it.

`This is not revenge. This is sequence preservation.`

He stared at it. Too clean. Too much like something written for a future reader who had already decided to distrust him.

He added:

`I am preserving dates, words, and context because the story moving through other people has separated those things.`

Better.

The work took hours. He named files with timestamps because memory could bend under pressure and he refused to make his future self carry everything alone. Each item received a context note. What it showed. What it did not show. Why it mattered. What could be misunderstood.

That last field became the most important.

What could be misunderstood?

Everything.

At 1:32 a.m., he found himself writing a note about the soup. He almost laughed, but the laugh broke apart before it became sound. Soup did not belong in an evidence folder. Soup belonged in kitchens, in sickness, in ordinary human care.

And yet the soup mattered because it had arrived with instructions. It was proof of a world where kindness and removal could share a doorway.

He wrote:

`Lena brought food. Also advised distance. Preserve both facts together.`

Both facts together. That was the work now.

Truth was not only the correction of lies. Truth was the refusal to let anyone split the human from the record.

When Jack finally closed the laptop, dawn had begun to pale the window. He had not slept. His shoulder was a field of static. His head felt packed with glass.

But the folder existed.

The story had learned to travel without him.

Now the truth had a place to wait.
