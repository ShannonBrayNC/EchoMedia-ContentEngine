# Chapter 2 — The First Wrong Note

The wrong note was not a sound. It was a pause.

Jack heard it during a call that should have lasted five minutes. A simple question, a simple answer, a small matter of scheduling. He gave the answer, waited for the usual acknowledgment, and instead found a pocket of silence where trust had been.

"Right," Maren said finally. "I just wanted to make sure."

The words were harmless. The tone was not. Jack sat still, hand hovering above the mouse, and looked at the timer in the corner of the screen. Two minutes, fourteen seconds. A harmless call did not need a silence like that.

"Make sure of what?" he asked.

Another pause. Smaller, but not small enough.

"Nothing. Just details."

People said details when they did not want to say context. Jack had learned that in the Navy. He had learned it again in hospitals, in conference rooms, in every system where the form mattered more than the person filling it out. Details were safe. Context had teeth.

When the call ended, Jack did not move. He replayed the exchange in his head until the words lost shape. Maybe he had imagined it. Maybe pain and bad sleep had sharpened a normal hesitation into something else. Hypervigilance was good at making weather out of dust.

He opened the call log anyway.

Time. Duration. Participants. Notes.

He wrote one line in a private file he had never intended to keep:

`09:58 — Maren paused before answering. Tone changed. No stated reason.`

Then he stared at the sentence and hated it. It looked paranoid on the screen. It looked like evidence from a future he did not want. He almost deleted it.

Instead, he saved the file.

By noon, two other small things had happened. A message thread went quiet after his reply. Someone who normally answered with a joke gave him a thumbs-up emoji and nothing else. A meeting invite disappeared from his calendar and then returned ten minutes later with no explanation.

None of it meant anything by itself.

That was the problem.

Jack knew how systems failed. They rarely failed all at once. They produced little inconsistencies first, hairline cracks thin enough to deny until the wall opened. He made lunch and forgot to eat it. He stood in the kitchen with the refrigerator door open, feeling cold air on his knees, trying to decide whether he was watching something begin or simply watching himself become afraid.

At 1:17 p.m., his phone buzzed again.

This time the message preview was only four words.

`We need to talk.`
