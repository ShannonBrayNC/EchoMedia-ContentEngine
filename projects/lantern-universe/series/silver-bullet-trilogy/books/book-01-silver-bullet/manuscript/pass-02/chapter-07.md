# Chapter 7 — Everyone Is Careful Now

By the next morning, Briar County had learned to be careful around him.

Jack felt it before anyone said enough to confirm it. Northstar messages arrived with fewer contractions. The Grayline Neighborhood Thread stopped tagging him in practical questions about trash pickup and road work. At Northstar Market, the cashier who usually asked about his shoulder scanned his items with professional brightness and no questions at all.

Brightness could be a wall.

Jack bought heat patches, coffee, and a frozen dinner he would later forget to eat. On the way out, he passed Mrs. Havel near the produce display. Her cart angled away as soon as she saw him. A lemon rolled from the top of her bag and came to rest near his shoe.

For one absurd second, neither of them moved.

Jack bent carefully, picked it up with his good hand, and held it out.

"You dropped this."

"Thank you," she said. Her fingers brushed the lemon, not his hand. "Hope things settle soon."

He almost asked what things. He almost asked who had given the neighborhood permission to speak in weather reports. Instead he nodded, because a man arguing with an old woman over a lemon would travel badly.

Outside, the automatic doors opened onto a parking lot too bright to look at directly. Jack sat in his truck and wrote the exchange into his phone before memory could smooth it.

`Northstar Market. Mrs. Havel avoided direct contact. Phrase: hope things settle soon.`

The note looked ridiculous. That did not make it useless.

At home, an Eastbridge Review Office message waited in his inbox. It thanked him for his patience and said Northstar had paused his project access while the matter was being clarified. Clarified by whom, from what, and against which version of events remained unstated.

He copied the message into the sequence record. Then he opened the old Halden Ridge notes and stared at work he could no longer touch.

A week earlier, Jack had been the person people called when CivicCloud failed to make sense. Now the same system displayed his absence with clean efficiency.

Kendra texted just after lunch.

`I don't know what's going on. I hope you're okay.`

He read the sentence three times. It contained no accusation. It also contained no question. That was how the world had begun to protect itself: by expressing care without requesting truth.

Jack typed back, `I'm not okay. But I know the sequence.`

Her reply took eight minutes.

`Maybe give people space for now.`

He placed the phone face down and listened to the refrigerator hum.

Space was what people asked from the person already being removed.
