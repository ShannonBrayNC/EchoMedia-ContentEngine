# Chapter 21 — Old Wounds, New Language

The phrase was new. The room it opened was not.

`Everyone just wants to know this can be handled safely.`

Harlan Pierce wrote it in a follow-up email, probably without malice. The line appeared in the middle of a paragraph about process steps and expected review timing, but Jack did not make it past safely the first time.

The kitchen dropped away.

Not gone. Still there: sink, cracked tile, refrigerator hum, morning light on the coffee cup. But underneath it another room pressed upward. A room with different walls, different uniforms, different expectations. Another time when careful language had made him smaller before anyone asked what happened.

Jack placed both hands on the counter.

Mug. Sink. Tile. Window. Chair.

The old grounding exercise felt humiliating every time it worked.

He said the words under his breath until the present returned. Briar County. Grayline Road. Eastbridge email. Not then. Now.

The distinction mattered because pain could make a liar out of sequence. If he let the old wound write the present, the record would become less accurate. If he pretended the old wound was not speaking, the record would become less honest.

He opened a voice note because typing felt too narrow.

"Language repeated. Emotional response larger than current sentence alone. Separate historical echo from present sequence. Do not omit either."

He played it back and heard the strain under his own control. For a moment he wanted to delete it. It sounded like weakness, or worse, like something someone else could make into weakness.

Instead he saved it.

Later, he added Harlan's line to the context packet with a note:

`Phrase activated prior injury. Current relevance: repeated safety language contributes to social/process frame. Historical echo noted separately from event sequence.`

That was the best he could do: two truths, held apart enough to remain themselves.

In the afternoon, Marcus texted for the first time in days.

`I don't know how to talk to you right now.`

Jack almost typed, Then ask. He almost typed, You used to know. He almost typed nothing.

Finally he wrote, `Start with what you actually know.`

No reply came.

The house settled around him as evening moved in. The vent ticked. A car passed. The same sounds. Different rooms inside them.

Jack saved the file and wrote one more line for the future self who might doubt the day:

`The truth does not become weaker because it hurts. But hurt must be named so it does not take over the truth.`
