# Chapter 2 — The First Wrong Note

The wrong note arrived in a pause too small to defend in language.

Jack heard it during a Northstar follow-up call that should have lasted four minutes. The Halden Ridge issue had been resolved. The workspace had synced. The account board wanted status language for a client note, and Jack gave them exactly that: plain, neutral, useful.

"So we're stable," Maren Vale said.

"Stable enough to stop everyone from forwarding the same screenshot to each other," Jack said.

Normally, Maren would have laughed. She had the dry, tired laugh of someone who had survived too many project dashboards. Instead, the call opened around a silence.

Jack watched the timer in the meeting window: 04:18, 04:19, 04:20.

"Right," Maren said. "I just wanted to make sure."

The words were fine. The tone was not. It had a carefulness that did not belong to the project.

"Make sure of what?"

Another pause. Shorter this time, because she had heard herself create the first one. "Nothing. Just details."

Jack kept his face neutral even though no cameras were on. Some training outlived the environment that made it necessary. "The details are in the log. I updated it after the call."

"I saw. Thanks."

The call ended politely. Politeness was not the same thing as safety.

Jack sat back and listened to the house: vent, refrigerator, a truck downshifting on Grayline. The same sounds as the morning, but the room had tilted. He told himself he was tired. He told himself pain made pattern recognition hungry. He told himself a pause was not a fact.

Then he opened a private note.

`09:58 — Maren paused before answering. Tone changed. Used phrase: just details. No stated reason.`

He hated the note. It looked like the beginning of a file made by someone already losing the argument. He almost deleted it, then remembered every system failure that had begun with one engineer deciding the early warning was too awkward to write down.

He saved the note.

The second wrong note came from the Grayline Neighborhood Thread. Mrs. Havel posted a reminder about trash pickup. Jack replied with a thumbs-up because the county had changed the schedule twice that month. Three people reacted to Mrs. Havel. Nobody reacted to Jack. A small thing. A ridiculous thing. A thing that had not happened before.

At 12:17, Kendra Sloane texted him.

`Hey. Everything okay?`

Jack looked at the message for a long time. Kendra did not ask broad questions unless she already had a narrow fear.

`Why?` he wrote.

The typing bubble appeared, vanished, returned.

`No reason. Just checking.`

Just details. Just checking.

Jack added the phrase to the note and felt the first cold thread of recognition pull tight.

Most things were nothing until they repeated.
