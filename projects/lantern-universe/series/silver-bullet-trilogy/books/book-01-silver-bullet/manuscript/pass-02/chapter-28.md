# Chapter 28 — The Witness Thread

Kendra chose Halden Park because open air made fear easier to carry.

She sat on the far end of a bench near the pond, both hands wrapped around a paper cup she had not been drinking. Jack noticed the location first: public enough to feel safe, quiet enough to talk, close enough to the footpath that leaving would be simple. Kendra had planned her own exit before offering him anything.

He respected that.

"I don't want my name in it," she said before hello.

"It won't be."

"I mean it, Jack."

"I know."

She looked at him then. Really looked. He saw the moment the story had to compete with the person in front of her: the guarded posture, the careful shoulder, the exhaustion around his eyes, the folder in his hand held like something fragile instead of victorious.

Kendra reached into her bag and took out two printed pages. The Grayline screenshot and a handwritten note beneath it.

`This language existed before the later explanation. I noticed the timing after reviewing the thread.`

Her handwriting was neat except for the word timing, where the pen had pressed hard enough to dent the paper.

Jack took the pages with both hands because ceremony seemed appropriate and anything less felt careless.

"This matters," he said.

"It doesn't feel like enough."

"Enough might not be available. That doesn't make this nothing."

She wiped one eye angrily, as if the tear had betrayed her. "People act like asking questions means choosing the wrong side."

"That's how the story protects itself."

"I should have asked sooner."

Jack wanted to answer yes. The yes was true. It was also not the only truth.

"You asked now," he said.

For a while they watched the pond. A child dragged a stick along the fence. Two ducks argued over a piece of bread they should not have had. Life kept offering absurd little proofs that the world was not built around his collapse.

Kendra took a breath. "There was another woman at Lena's that night. I don't remember her name. Gray hair, red scarf. She said something about patterns too. I thought it was gossip."

Jack did not move. "Could you recognize her?"

"Maybe. I don't know. I don't want—"

"I won't push."

She nodded, relieved and disappointed in herself at the same time.

After she left, Jack stayed on the bench with the pages in his lap. The wind lifted the corner of the note. He placed his hand over it.

A record had become human for a moment.

That made it stronger.

That made it more vulnerable too.
