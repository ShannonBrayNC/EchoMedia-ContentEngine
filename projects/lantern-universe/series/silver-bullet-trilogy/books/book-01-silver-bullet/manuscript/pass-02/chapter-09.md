# Chapter 9 — The First Lost Ally

Marcus had once known how to help without making Jack feel handled.

That was why the distance hurt before Marcus named it. Jack remembered the rain, the borrowed generator, the way Marcus had called him an idiot with enough affection to make accepting help possible. He remembered a Saturday at Stokes Veterans Hall when Jack's shoulder had started trembling during a volunteer setup and Marcus had quietly moved the heavy boxes without announcing the rescue to the room.

Trust was not a speech. It was accumulated evidence.

They met at The Switchyard because Marcus said phones were making everything worse. He arrived early, which meant he was nervous. Jack found him at a corner table with untouched coffee and both hands around the cup.

"I need to step back for a bit," Marcus said.

No weather talk. No preface. Jack almost respected him for that.

"From me?"

Marcus looked at the table. "From the situation."

"The situation is wearing my name."

Marcus flinched.

Jack wanted to make him look up. Wanted to put the sequence record between them and move his finger down the page until friendship remembered what order meant. Instead he sat still. Every visible wound had become interpretable.

"Do you believe her?" Jack asked.

"I believe something happened."

"Something always happened. That's how false versions survive. They borrow pieces of the true thing."

Marcus rubbed his thumb along the cup seam until the cardboard softened. "I have my kids half the week. I have the school board thing. My name gets pulled into this, it follows me too."

There it was. Not betrayal in a black coat. Fear in a familiar jacket.

Jack could have used that against him. The urge came sharp and fast. Instead he let the urge pass and hated how practiced that made him.

"I needed you to ask before stepping away," Jack said.

"I'm asking now."

"No. You're explaining after choosing."

Marcus closed his eyes. For a moment he looked like the man in the rain again, tired and loyal and human. Then the moment passed.

Afterward, Jack sat in the truck with the engine off and wrote the entry.

`Marcus Vale: requested distance. Reason: family/social risk. Did not claim belief. Did not request full context before decision.`

The note was accurate. It was also brutal. It turned years of friendship into a status change because the world had begun doing that first.

Jack added one more line:

`Loss should not erase prior care.`

He saved the file before grief could make him delete it.
