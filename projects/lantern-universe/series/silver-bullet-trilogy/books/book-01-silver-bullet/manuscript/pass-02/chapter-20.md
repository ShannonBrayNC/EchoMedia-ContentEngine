# Chapter 20 — The Man in Their Story

Jack met the other Jack in Devin Cross's mouth.

They were sitting in the back corner of The Switchyard Cafe because Devin had asked for neutral ground and Jack had almost laughed at the phrase. Neutral ground was a myth people invented after the ground had already moved.

Devin kept turning his coffee cup by the cardboard sleeve. "I think people are worried you're trying to manage the narrative."

The cafe noise thinned around Jack. Espresso machine. Chair legs. A train in the distance. The words remained.

"Manage the narrative," Jack repeated.

Devin looked miserable. "I'm telling you what I heard, not what I think."

But that was how the other Jack traveled: inside disclaimers, carried by people who wanted credit for not fully believing him.

This other Jack was smoother than the real one. He did not wake to shoulder pain and house sounds. He did not forget meals. He did not sit with printed notices trying to separate exact truth from useful anger. He managed optics. He controlled. He built records because he wanted power over the story, not because context was being stripped from his life one careful phrase at a time.

The other Jack was easier to fear because he was easier to understand.

"Do you know why that phrase works?" Jack asked.

Devin shook his head.

"Because once someone says I'm managing the narrative, anything I do to restore sequence becomes evidence for them. If I stay quiet, the story hardens. If I answer, I'm controlling. If I document, I'm obsessive. If I don't document, the record belongs to everyone else."

Devin looked away.

"I should have asked before repeating anything."

"Yes," Jack said.

The word sat between them, hard and clean. Devin accepted it without flinching, which counted for something.

After they parted, Jack sat in his truck and looked at his reflection in the dark dashboard screen. The face looking back was thinner than he expected. Older. One shoulder held too high. One hand resting carefully in his lap as if even stillness required negotiation.

He opened the notes app and made two columns.

`Public double:` controlling, unstable, managing optics, unsafe to trust.

`Self:` preserving sequence, injured, angry, careful, still responsible for accuracy.

The two lists did not cancel each other. The false one was simpler. That made it stronger in public. The true one was heavier. That made it harder to carry.

Jack saved the note under `identity-fracture`.

For the first time, he understood that the story was not only changing what people thought had happened.

It was trying to create a man who could replace him.
