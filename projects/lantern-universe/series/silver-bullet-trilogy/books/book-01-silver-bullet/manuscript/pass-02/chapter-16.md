# Chapter 16 — The Body Betrays Him

Jack was late because his shoulder locked in the Briar Civic Annex parking lot.

Not late enough to justify itself. Not late enough to become visible as a problem everyone could name correctly. Two minutes. Three at most. A small delay born from a small movement: reaching across the passenger seat for the folder, forgetting for half a second that his body kept old agreements even when his mind moved on.

Pain went white from shoulder to jaw.

He froze with the folder half-lifted and one hand gripping the steering wheel. The Civic Annex windows reflected the truck back at him. In the glass, he looked paused, suspiciously still, like a man deciding whether to enter.

By the time he walked inside, his shirt was damp at the collar and his left hand had curled close to his body. The receptionist glanced up, then down at the appointment list.

"Mr. Mercer? Eastbridge Review? They're ready."

Of course they were. Systems were always ready when bodies were not.

The room held Harlan Pierce, a Northstar representative named Calder, and a second review staffer who introduced herself as Ames and then said nothing for ten minutes. Jack sat carefully. Too carefully, he realized, when Harlan's eyes dropped to the way he angled his shoulder.

"Are you able to continue?" she asked.

A decent question. A dangerous one.

If he said no, the record might carry unwilling. If he said yes, every visible pain response might become emotional state. He placed the folder on the table with his right hand.

"I'm able to continue," Jack said. "For context, I am managing a physical limitation. If I pause or adjust posture, that should not be interpreted as agitation."

Ames wrote something.

Jack wanted to ask what. He did not. The room had become a translation engine, and every request risked becoming another label.

They reviewed the Eastbridge notice. Harlan asked him whether he understood why Northstar had paused his CivicCloud access.

"I understand the action," Jack said. "I do not understand the basis."

Calder sighed through his nose. It was small. Jack heard it anyway.

Pain returned halfway through Jack's explanation of the Grayline screenshot. He stopped for water, and the pause stretched. Harlan's pen waited over the page.

"Take your time," she said.

He did. Not because she offered permission. Because rushing would let the body write the wrong story.

After the meeting, he sat in the truck until his vision steadied. Then he dictated a note:

`Body affected timing and presentation. Context given in room. Verify meeting summary preserves exact wording.`

He stopped recording and listened once.

His voice sounded tired.

Not unstable. Not guilty. Tired.

He saved the note before anyone else could name it first.
