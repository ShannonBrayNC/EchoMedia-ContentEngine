# Chapter 24 — The False Mercy

The offer came from Marcus, which made it harder to hate.

He asked to meet at Halden Park near the old footbridge, away from Grayline and away from Northstar. Jack arrived early and sat on a bench facing the pond, folder in a canvas bag beside him. Ducks moved over the water with the blank confidence of creatures who had never been summarized by a review office.

Marcus stood for a while before sitting.

"I talked to Lena," he said.

Jack almost laughed. Not because it was funny. Because of course the people asking for space had found each other inside it.

"Okay."

Marcus rubbed his hands together. "Nobody wants this to keep getting worse."

"For who?"

"For anyone. You included."

That part was true enough to matter. Marcus looked tired. There were shadows under his eyes, and his jacket was zipped wrong at the bottom like he had dressed while thinking about something else.

"There may be a way to quiet it down," Marcus said.

Jack looked at the pond. "Quiet is not the same as corrected."

"I know. But maybe corrected isn't available right now. Maybe you write something short. Not admitting anything. Just acknowledging impact. People can stop bracing for whatever comes next."

There it was. A door labeled peace.

Jack could see the paragraph before Marcus finished explaining it. A statement of regret. A careful note about unintended harm. No admission, no details, no sequence. A document that would let everyone say the matter had been handled while preserving the shape of the story that made handling necessary.

The terrible part was that Jack wanted it.

He wanted the calls back. He wanted Northstar to restore CivicCloud access. He wanted Mrs. Havel to talk about trash pickup like trash pickup was all that existed. He wanted Marcus to call him an idiot in the old voice. He wanted to stop waking with the record already waiting in his chest.

"If I do that," Jack said, "the record will remember the shape, not the footnote."

Marcus did not answer.

"And you know that," Jack said more softly.

Marcus looked at the water. "I know I'm scared for you. I also know I'm tired."

That was honest. It did not make the offer safe.

At home, Jack wrote the suggested statement. He made it humane and careful and almost beautiful. Then he saved it in a folder called `not-sent`.

Under it he wrote:

`False mercy offer. Relief in exchange for ambiguity. Do not confuse quiet with restoration.`

He did not delete the draft.

Truth required preserving the temptation too.
