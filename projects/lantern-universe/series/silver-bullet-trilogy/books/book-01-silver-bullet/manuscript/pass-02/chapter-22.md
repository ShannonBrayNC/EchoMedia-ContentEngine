# Chapter 22 — The Quiet Doubter

Kendra Sloane did not become brave all at once.

That made Jack trust the message more when it arrived at 11:48 p.m.

`I keep thinking about the timing.`

He was sitting on the kitchen floor with the laptop open on a chair because the table had become a wall map and the office still felt wrong after the Eastbridge notice. For a moment he only looked at the phone. Hope had become another thing requiring verification.

He typed, `What timing?`

The typing bubble appeared, disappeared, appeared again.

`The Grayline comment. Evelyn said people got worried after the Switchyard thing. But she used that careful language before, right?`

Jack sat back against the cabinet. The floor was cold through his shirt. The detail was not new to him, but it was different when another human being noticed without being led to it line by line.

`Yes,` he wrote. `That is the sequence problem.`

Kendra did not answer for six minutes.

When she did, the message came in three parts.

`I don't want my name used.`

`I have work.`

`I can't have Grayline turning on me too.`

Jack read each sentence as its own record. Fear had details. Fear had obligations. Fear had rent and work and neighbors and family group chats and all the tiny places a person could become vulnerable after doing the right thing.

`I won't use your name without permission,` he wrote. `I'm asking you not to forget what you saw.`

The reply was shorter than he wanted and more than he had.

`I won't.`

Jack saved the exchange under `witness-possible/kendra-sloane`. Then he changed the folder name from witness-possible to witness-fragments and paused before committing the rename.

A fragment was not a person. He had to remember that.

He created a context note instead:

`Kendra independently noticed timing inconsistency. She is afraid of social consequence. Fear is part of the environment, not a defect in the witness.`

That sentence mattered. If Jack turned frightened people into failures, he would become as simple as the story made him. Truth needed witnesses, but witnesses were human. They delayed. They protected themselves. They noticed late and apologized badly and sometimes still helped.

Before sleeping, he printed the exchange without her name visible and placed it beside the Grayline screenshot.

One detail from the record.

One detail from a person.

For the first time in days, the house did not feel entirely empty.
