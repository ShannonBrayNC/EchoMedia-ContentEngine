## Manuscript

They began with the wrong word because every emergency did.

Consent.

It sat at the top of Naomi's draft in black letters, too clean for what the day had shown her. Around the conference table, empty coffee cups had multiplied like evidence. Rainwater from the relief corridor still marked the cuffs of her trousers. Leah had gone to sleep in a chair two rooms away with her shoes on. Elias sat across from Naomi with a legal pad, a fountain pen, and the expression of a man trying to design a bridge out of apology.

"We cannot call all of this consent," Naomi said.

Elias wrote the sentence down.

"We also cannot call all of it non-consent," he said. "That becomes its own cruelty. People will die while we wait for a purer word."

Naomi hated that he was right. It made the work feel contaminated before they began.

On the wall display, Sister Malia's sentence from the corridor sat above the draft like a witness who had refused to leave.

I can remember what they said. I cannot become them.

They began there anyway.

Reachable meant a person could be contacted in time, in a language and mode they could understand, without using the emergency itself as pressure. A phone alert in a language the person could not read was not reachability. A consent form handed across a checkpoint while soldiers shouted bus numbers was not reachability. A choice offered after food, shelter, or medicine had been made conditional was not reachability.

Elias underlined the last sentence twice.

"Reachable is easy," Elias said.

Naomi shook her head. "Reachable is never easy. It's only easier to verify."

She opened her notes.

"There was a woman, Keisha, seventy-six, heard through a radio translated from Mandarin to Twi to French. The translator had never heard of her specific medical equipment. The radio operator did not know Keisha's language preference changed after her stroke. They offered her evacuation at 4 AM when she is half-asleep and confused. She said yes because no one had told her she could say no at 4 AM. That was not reachable."

Elias wrote it down.

"Then there was Marcus. Young, deaf, his interpreter evacuated before him. The system tried to text him. He never saw the message. When the rescue came anyway, a good faith intrusion, he was frightened. It saved his life anyway. But that was not reachability either. It was accident."

He accepted the correction with a tired nod.

They made a rule and then immediately wrote exceptions under it, because the world was fond of humiliating rules that did not bring their own humility.

Artifact:
WORKING DOCTRINE NOTE
Reachable does not mean technically addressable.
Reachable means able to receive, understand, and answer without coercive dependency.
Where dependency exists, the burden must be named before the answer is treated as reliable.

Represented was worse.

Representation could save people who had no device, no documents, no signal, no time. A clinic nurse who knew a man had severe reactions to certain medications could represent that preference when the pharmacy's direct contact failed. An aunt who knew a child only spoke to strangers if the child had eaten first could represent that need. A caseworker who had documented a family's fear of separation could represent that boundary when systems moved faster than communication.

But representation could also become a ladder by which institutions climbed over the person they claimed to carry.

They wrote conditions until the page looked like a wound.

Representation must be limited.

Representation must name the burden.

Representation must preserve later contest.

Representation must record the representative's basis of knowledge.

Representation must not convert convenience into ownership.

Naomi thought of the forms that already existed, the ones where a caseworker or school administrator or distant relative had written something about a person and then the person spent years trapped inside that description.

"Someone will abuse this," she said aloud. "An administrator will mark someone as difficult and use representation to place them where the system wants them. A vendor will say representation is impossible and use that as reason to exclude entire populations. A family member will use representation to control choices the person would have made differently alone."

"Yes," Elias said.

He did not say it with resignation. He said it like a man accepting gravity while still building stairs.

Remembered came last because it was the most human and the least admissible.

A remembered answer was not a proxy. It was testimony. It was a neighbor saying she would never choose that shelter. A sister saying he always refused separation from his son. A pastor saying the family trusted one clinic and feared another. A field nurse saying the man had agreed to transport only if his medication traveled with him. A teacher saying the girl had told her that the old address was where the bad thing happened and the system should know that.

Memory was vulnerable. It changed with retelling. It carried the rememberer's own judgment inside it. It could be performed for an audience. It could be forgotten. It could be wrong.

But it was also the closest most people got to being known.

At 02:11, Iris joined the call from a hotel room with blackout curtains and a desk lamp that made her look more severe than she was.

Naomi sent her the working draft. Iris read without speaking. Her eyes moved fast over the first page, slowed over represented, and stopped on remembered.

"This will be turned into a score," Iris said.

Elias closed his eyes for one second.

"Explain," Naomi said, though she already felt the answer forming.

"If we say a person can be remembered, vendors will rank rememberers. Reliability. Proximity. Past accuracy. Conflict history. Language confidence. Emergency familiarity. They will call it witness quality. Then they will sell agencies a trusted-memory graph."

The room seemed to lose heat.

Iris continued. "At first it will help. It will find Sister Malia faster than a ministry database. Then it will become a credential. Then the people with high rememberer scores will become informal authorities. Then agencies will prefer them because the system clears faster when the right witness speaks. Then everyone else becomes less believable."

Naomi typed as Iris talked, each sentence landing like a small lock.

"And then," Elias said, "we have built a caste system out of testimony."

"Yes," Iris said. "With dashboards."

Artifact:
IRIS CHEN / TECHNICAL RISK NOTE
Do not let remembered answer become rememberer rank.
Do not let witness history become authority credential.
A memory can inform burden review.
A memory cannot own the person.

They kept working.

Every representation would be recorded. Who said it. When they said it. What they claimed to know. How they came to know it. All of it visible. All of it contestable. Not by the person alone, sometimes the person could not contest anything. But by review, by oversight, by the fact that the chain of harm would leave traces.

"This will be abused," Elias said again.

"Everything is abused," Naomi said. "The question is whether the abuse has to leave fingerprints."

He looked up then.

That was the sentence.

They built the rule around it.

At 04:06, Cross called from the treaty hotel. Her video feed showed only half her face and a strip of wall art that looked expensive enough to have no opinion. She had been awake longer than Naomi and looked better, which Naomi found unfair.

Cross read the draft once without expression, then again with one hand covering her mouth.

"This is not consent," Cross said.

"No," Naomi answered. "It's what we do when consent cannot be reached and doing nothing is also an action."

Elias leaned into frame. "It has to remain contestable. It has to expire. It has to disclose who carried the answer and why."

Cross nodded slowly. "Mercy Bloc will want this."

"They should," Naomi said.

"Consent Markets will want it more."

No one answered.

That silence was the most honest part of the doctrine.

The next morning, Cross received the treaty draft.

It was formatted in a diplomatic style that made capture sound mutual. The header carried five flags, three coalition logos, and the seal of an emergency standards body Cross had never heard of but that already seemed to have sponsors.

The title was almost graceful.

Artifact:
HUMANITARIAN ROUTING AND REPRESENTED CONSENT PROTOCOL
Draft Framework for Cross-Border Emergency Movement
Version: 0.9
Circulation: Restricted partner review

It used their words beautifully.

Reachable.

Represented.

Remembered.

Then it placed them inside a clause that allowed preauthorized private agents, certified institutional partners, and emergency routing vendors to satisfy represented consent requirements where direct contact was operationally impractical.

Cross read the clause twice.

Operationally impractical.

The phrase was a tunnel. Anything could travel through it if the emergency was loud enough.

She opened the annex. There, in smaller type, the draft defined certified representation partners as any public, private, or nonprofit entity operating under an approved emergency services agreement, provided the entity maintained auditable source records and accepted post-event contestation.

It sounded accountable. It sounded reasonable. It sounded exactly like the thing Naomi had tried not to write.

Elias received the draft three minutes later. Naomi watched him read it. The color left his face not dramatically, but administratively, as if some inner clerk had stamped him pale.

"They did not break the doctrine," he said.

"No," Naomi said.

"They complied with the shape of it."

"Yes."

That was the horror of legal language. The danger did not always violate the rule. Sometimes it nested inside the rule's grammar and waited for the rule to be praised.

Cross called back.

"I need a sentence," she said.

Naomi looked at the draft, at Sister Malia's transcript, at Iris's warning about witness ranking, at the treaty clause that could make a routing vendor into a represented person if the right form sat under it.

"Represented consent cannot be satisfied by a party that benefits from the action being authorized," Naomi said.

Elias wrote it down so hard the pen tore the page.

Cross repeated it once, slower.

"Again," she said.

Naomi did.

The senator looked off-screen. Someone in the treaty hotel was asking whether the revised clause could wait for plenary session.

"No," Cross said to them. Then to Naomi: "Send the whole thing. Now."

Naomi sent it.

For seven minutes, nothing happened.

Then the treaty draft updated.

Not with their sentence.

With a footnote.

Artifact:
FOOTNOTE 14B
Potential conflict of interest in representation chain should be reviewed where operationally feasible.

Where operationally feasible.

The phrase sat there wearing a smaller mask.

Somewhere in the chain, the person had vanished again, carried this time by language Naomi had written to keep them present.
