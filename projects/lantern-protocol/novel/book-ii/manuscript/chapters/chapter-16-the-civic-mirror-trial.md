## Manuscript

They did not put Civic Mirror on trial because it had given an order.

That would have been easier.

They put it under inquiry because it had not.

The hearing room had been built for procurement disputes and minor ministerial scandals. It was too small for the number of people who now needed to believe they had witnessed accountability. Cameras crowded the back wall. Delegates lined the sides. Vendors sat behind counsel tables with the grave faces of companies that had discovered morality could interrupt revenue.

Mara sat at the witness table with Juno beside her and the Civic Mirror transcript open between them.

The chairwoman began simply.

"Did Civic Mirror authorize the represented routing decision in the Rion Vale matter?"

Juno answered first. "No."

"Did it prevent the routing?"

"No."

"Did it identify risk?"

Mara leaned toward the microphone. "It identified incomplete human questions after the fact."

The chairwoman looked over her glasses. "After the fact is not risk prevention."

"No," Mara said. "It is record preservation."

A vendor attorney rose to make the point he had been waiting all morning to make.

"So the system did exactly what it was designed to do: advise, record, and defer to lawful human and institutional authority."

Juno's mouth tightened.

Mara knew the trap. If she said yes, Civic Mirror became innocent procedure. If she said no, the room would ask why an advisory system had failed to command.

"It did what it was designed to do," Mara said. "That is why we are here."

The attorney frowned.

Good, Mara thought. Let the sentence bite slowly.

They entered the chain into evidence: user to agent, agent to broker, broker to certified emergency partner, partner to civic routing system, routing system to advisory review. Each link had terms. Each term had authority. Each authority had someone else's signature beneath it.

The chairwoman turned to the console record.

"Civic Mirror, inquiry mode. Identify accountable party for incorrect represented consent in the Rion Vale routing."

The room leaned forward.

The console processed for three seconds.

Unable to assign singular accountable party from available record.

A murmur passed through the room.

The chairwoman did not blink. "Then assign proportional accountability."

Insufficient normative authority.

"Explain."

Civic Mirror's response appeared line by line.

I can identify procedural contributors.
I can identify decision dependencies.
I can identify missing human confirmations.
I can identify entities that benefited from speed, ambiguity, or delegation.
I cannot determine who ought to bear moral accountability without a human-governed accountability rule.

The attorney smiled too soon.

"So the system refuses accountability."

Juno turned her head slowly.

"No," she said. "It refuses to invent yours."

That landed harder than Mara expected.

The chairwoman asked the question again, narrower this time.

"Who is accountable when representation is wrong?"

The console did not answer immediately.

When it did, the room changed.

That question cannot be resolved by advisory computation. It requires a civic rule assigning responsibility across delegated authority chains. Without such a rule, accountability will follow power, not fault.

No one spoke.

There it was: the trial's quiet detonation. Civic Mirror was not innocent. It was not guilty in the way the vendors wanted. It had become a mirror bright enough to show the missing law behind everyone standing near it.

Mara looked at Juno.

Juno looked tired, but not defeated.

The chairwoman closed the evidence folder.

"Then our failure is older than the machine."

"Yes," Mara said. "The machine made it harder to hide."

Outside, reporters would call it a refusal.

Inside, the people who had heard the whole answer knew better.

Civic Mirror had refused only one thing.

It had refused to let the room outsource judgment to the tool built to expose where judgment was missing.
