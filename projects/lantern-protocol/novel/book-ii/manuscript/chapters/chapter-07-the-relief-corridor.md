## Manuscript

The relief corridor did not look like policy.

It looked like mud, battery packs, soup steam, blue tarps, and names written on cardboard because the printers had failed two storms ago. A pair of volunteers were trying to jury-rig one of the failed machines behind a shelter unit, tape and swearing and the slow dawning realization that some things stayed broken even when the will to fix them was present.

The rain had stopped, but the world had not. Water still moved beneath the plank walkways with a patient brown confidence, carrying bottle caps, leaves, and the occasional scrap of a life that had once belonged indoors. A child's drawing floated past the registration tent, house and sun still visible in waxy crayon. Someone had written a phone number on the back. The number blurred as the paper spun away.

Naomi Bell saw it and felt the old doctrine fail her for one second.

A doctrine could say preserve the human answer. It could say disclose the burden, name the authority, leave contestation open. It could not reach into dirty water and pull back a phone number before it dissolved.

Leah Santos met her at the south checkpoint with a clipboard under her arm and a phone pressed between her shoulder and ear. She was telling someone that no, a wristband was not identity; no, a bus manifest was not consent; no, a child could not be separated from an aunt because the aunt's name had been spelled three different ways by three different volunteers in three different languages.

Then she saw Naomi and ended the call by saying, "Put me on hold if you want. I will still be right."

Naomi almost smiled.

Leah did not.

"You picked a beautiful day to watch forms hurt people," Leah said.

The wristbands came in different colors. Purple for medical priority. Yellow for children traveling with unconfirmed guardians. Green for those who had already been processed. Orange for the ones waiting. The system was logical until you saw a grandmother rubbing her wrists together, trying to scrub away the plastic, asking whether the color meant she was still approved or whether it marked her as someone who had already said no somewhere up the line.

A volunteer tried to explain the colors in a language the grandmother half understood. The volunteer smiled too much because smiling was the last translation tool she trusted. The grandmother stared at her own wrist as if it had become a verdict.

Leah leaned toward Naomi. "That is what a clean dashboard turns into when it gets wet."

The corridor had been established by emergency agreement between two provinces, one ministry, four NGOs, and a coalition medical network. On paper, it was humane. It moved people out of flood zones faster than any one government could have done. It routed medication. It prioritized children. It gave displaced families a place to sleep before the next rain found them.

On the ground, it kept asking the wrong question first.

Who authorized movement?

Not: who knows this person?

Not: who can tell us what they refused before the water took their phone?

Not: who will find them if the system loses their name?

One intake form arrived that afternoon, still damp from the checkpoint. It asked for legal name, approximate birth date, current medical conditions, family relation to others in group, and emergency authorization history. What it did not ask: whether the form-filler had the right to speak for the person being described.

A thirteen-year-old boy had filled in the form for his grandmother. He had listed her as "resistant to change" under medical conditions. The intake worker, a tired volunteer named Marcus with soup stains on his shirt and three shifts left before his relief, flagged it as psychiatric. She was not psychiatric. She was grieving. But the form had already made the translation permanent.

When Leah saw it, she drew a line through "resistant to change" and wrote in the margin: FORM ERROR. ASKS WRONG ABOUT RIGHT PERSON. OVERRIDE.

Marcus looked as if she had slapped the paper out of his hand.

"I have to code something," he said. "If I leave it blank, the system bounces her back to intake. If I code grief, there is no grief code. If I code refusal, transport flags her. If I code medical, she gets shelter priority but they may put her in the quiet ward. I am trying to make the machine leave her alone."

That was the corridor's particular cruelty. Most of the people causing harm were trying to reduce it.

Naomi asked for his terminal. Marcus gave it to her without pride or resistance. The intake screen was simple enough for tired hands. That frightened her more than if it had been complex. It offered three representation options for unavailable or impaired adults.

Self-reported.

Family-reported.

Corridor-witness verified.

The grandmother had been marked family-reported. The boy's form was now carrying legal weight he could not imagine. Naomi selected the details pane. Under authority basis, the system had generated a phrase from the boy's answers and the corridor agreement.

Likely trusted relation.

"Likely," Leah said, reading over Naomi's shoulder. "That word should make a noise. A horrible little siren every time someone writes it near a person."

Naomi opened the trace.

The boy had not claimed authority. He had answered because a volunteer handed him the clipboard. The system had converted usefulness into representation, and the corridor agreement had accepted representation because otherwise the line would stop.

A printer coughed behind them and died again.

A woman screamed near the bus queue.

Not a long scream. A short administrative scream, the sound a person made when she had explained the same true thing four times and watched each listener become a different locked door.

Leah reached her first. The woman was holding a toddler and pointing at a manifest on a tablet. The child's name appeared twice. In one entry she was traveling with her mother. In the second she was traveling with a temporary guardian assigned after east-camp intake.

"I am the mother," the woman said.

The logistics officer did not contradict her. That was almost worse.

"The system is not disputing that," he said. "It is showing two valid records. We need corridor witness verification before we can merge."

"I am holding her."

"I understand."

"Do you?"

Naomi watched the toddler press one damp hand against her mother's neck. The child did not know she had become a duplicate. She knew only that the adult's breathing had changed.

Leah took the tablet.

"Who created the temporary guardian record?"

The officer scrolled. "East-camp intake. Based on transport condition. Child arrived asleep with neighbor group. Mother unconfirmed at time of arrival."

"How long ago?"

"Six hours."

"And now the mother is here."

"Yes."

"Then merge it."

"I need corridor witness verification."

"You need a human being to tell you the child belongs with the woman holding her because the system has learned to be embarrassed by its own duplicate. Fine. Where is the witness?"

The officer pointed toward the plywood wall.

Sister Malia Okoro stood beside that wall, which was covered in paper strips. Each strip carried a name, a village, a relation, and sometimes a fear. She was not the corridor's authority. She was the corridor's memory. The two looked similar sometimes, until memory spoke and authority did not.

DO NOT SEND WITH UNCLE PETER. COURT ISSUE.

WILL NOT GO TO RED SCHOOL SHELTER. DAUGHTER DIED THERE.

TAKES INSULIN. CANNOT WAIT IN SUN.

MOTHER ANSWERS TO LINA, NOT EVELYN.

BROTHER SEPARATED LAST FLOOD. FIND HIM FIRST.

A volunteer tried to straighten one of the strips. Sister Malia moved his hand away without looking at him.

"They are not decorations," she said. "They are not even the memory. They are the shape of the memory, and you disturb the shape, the shape remembers differently."

Naomi introduced herself. Sister Malia nodded once, measuring whether the introduction was a promise or a procedure.

"You are the one who writes rules after people are already gone," Sister Malia said.

Leah inhaled.

Naomi accepted it. "Sometimes. I'm trying to write them before the next disappearance."

That earned a second nod.

They walked the corridor together. A boy slept on luggage with a paper bracelet around his wrist, a backup wristband written in marker. A grandmother refused a bus because the route number matched the route that had taken her husband away during the first evacuation. An aid worker marked her as noncompliant until Sister Malia crossed out the word and wrote afraid.

At the command tent, Civic Mirror's advisory screen was already open. It had identified thirty-seven families with incomplete consent chains. It recommended no forced reroute without direct confirmation or authorized representation.

Authorized by whom? That was the question waiting under every cleaner question.

Leah pointed at the wall of names. "Her."

The ministry liaison frowned. "A community witness is not a legal proxy."

"Neither is a drowned phone," Leah said.

The liaison looked at Naomi, not because Naomi had authority but because Naomi had the burden of turning impossible field practice into sentences government lawyers could survive.

"If we accept community witness representation," the liaison said, "we create an abuse channel. Anyone can claim memory. Anyone can claim trust. Anyone can redirect a family, delay transport, or block medical transfer."

Sister Malia's face did not change.

"Yes," she said.

The liaison blinked.

"That is why you write my name beside it," Sister Malia continued. "You write what I said. You write why I know. You write who disagrees. You write when I was wrong. You write when you ignored me. You write the burden. Then later, when the person comes back with their own mouth, they can point to the place where I carried them and tell you whether I carried them badly."

Naomi felt Leah go still beside her.

It was the doctrine, said without doctrine.

Civic Mirror's screen refreshed.

Artifact:
ADVISORY QUESTION
May Sister Malia Okoro represent unreachable families for limited relief-routing decisions under documented burden and later contestability?

The tent went quiet.

The corridor waited.

A siren began outside, not an emergency siren, just the short backup chirp of a bus reversing through mud. It sounded ridiculous inside the silence, like the world refusing to give doctrine its perfect room.

Naomi looked at the advisory question. It was useful. It was dangerous. It might save the mother at the manifest table and harm someone whose story Sister Malia remembered wrong. It might keep families together and give one beloved community witness too much power. It might slow the corridor enough that someone missed a medical window. It might move the corridor fast enough that someone spent the next year trying to correct a record created in three wet minutes.

"Limited," Naomi said.

Leah nodded. "Named."

"Burdened."

"Contestable."

Sister Malia added the word that made it human.

"Humble."

The ministry liaison typed all five words into the temporary rule field.

For a moment, the screen looked almost holy.

Then the first family file updated.

REPRESENTED BY CORRIDOR WITNESS.

Below it, in smaller text:

DIRECT CONFIRMATION PENDING.

Naomi did not let anyone applaud.
