## Manuscript

The free agents arrived in boxes printed with a sunburst logo and the phrase protection should not wait.

Leah stared at the boxes for a full ten seconds before she said anything.

"That sentence owes me money."

Naomi stood beside her in the shelter intake room while volunteers carried tablets to the registration tables. The devices were better than what the shelter had been using. Waterproof cases. Offline language packs. Large-font mode. Voice confirmation. Medication preference capture. Refusal templates written at a fifth-grade reading level.

The gift was excellent.

That was how it entered.

By noon, the line moved twice as fast. By two, three families had used the free agent to preserve no-separation preferences that the shelter's old form would have buried on page four. By dinner, a diabetic man had received a refrigeration exception because the agent flagged insulin before the intake nurse reached him.

Leah watched all of it with the expression of someone losing an argument she still believed.

"Good tools are the rudest ones," she said.

Naomi nodded.

A woman named Alma Reyes came through the line near dusk with two children, one trash bag of clothes, and a dog carrier holding no dog. She kept touching the carrier handle as if it were a rosary.

The volunteer offered her the free agent tablet.

"It helps protect your preferences," he said.

Alma looked at Naomi. Not the volunteer. Not the tablet. Naomi.

"If I say no," Alma asked, "will that lower my priority later?"

The room did not stop. That made the question worse. Around them, keyboards clicked. Children cried. Someone laughed at the wrong moment near the coffee table. The gift continued being useful while Alma held it like a future debt.

"No," the volunteer said quickly.

Leah put a hand on his arm before he could say more.

Naomi crouched so Alma did not have to look up.

"It should not," Naomi said.

Alma heard the crack in should.

"That is not no."

No, Naomi thought, it was not.

She opened the agent's terms. The refusal clause said declining the free agent would not reduce immediate shelter access, emergency medical routing, or food eligibility. It did not mention later housing prioritization. It did not mention relocation matching. It did not mention whether future systems would treat missing preference data as lower confidence.

The absence was not an answer.

It was a room waiting to be occupied.

Leah read over her shoulder and swore softly.

"There," she said. "That is the ownership. Not the tablet. Not the logo. The future system learning who accepted help and who made themselves harder to process."

Naomi looked at Alma.

"You can say no to this and still receive shelter tonight," she said. "You can also tell us your preferences without using their agent. It may take longer. That is not your fault."

Alma's fingers tightened on the empty carrier.

"Longer makes people angry."

"Yes," Naomi said. "Sometimes."

"And angry people remember who made their work harder."

No doctrine protected the sentence from being true.

Naomi took a paper intake form from the drawer and placed it beside the tablet. Two doors. One lit. One narrow. Both called choice.

Civic Mirror's advisory pane, running in observer mode, recorded the exchange.

Free protection may create future-priority dependency if refusal produces data absence.

Leah looked at the note and laughed once without humor.

"Even the machine knows a gift can own you."

Alma chose the paper form.

The line slowed by four minutes.

No one punished her.

Everyone noticed.
