## Manuscript

The border station had three doors and four names for every person who passed through it.

Legal name.

Relief name.

Translated name.

The name someone shouted when the person did not answer to the other three.

Naomi learned this before breakfast, standing beside Leah in a repurposed customs hall where sleeping families filled the inspection lanes and the old duty-free shop had become a medication desk. A blue sign over the arrivals corridor still said nothing to declare. Someone had taped a paper arrow beneath it and written family tracing in black marker.

Leah looked at the sign.

"That is either irony or a confession."

Naomi did not answer. She was watching a girl in a red sweater argue with a registration tablet.

The girl was nine or ten, small enough to disappear behind adults and angry enough not to. She stood at the intake table with one hand wrapped around a plastic bag of papers. A volunteer knelt in front of her, trying to make her voice soft enough to become harmless.

"Your mother is listed under medical relocation," the volunteer said. "That means she may have been moved first so doctors could help her."

"She said no hospital without me."

"I understand."

"No, you don't. She said it before the water. That means before all this."

Naomi moved before she decided to.

Leah followed.

The girl's name, according to the intake tablet, was Mila Varek. According to the plastic bag, it was Mila Varek, Mila Warek, and M. V. Child Dependent, depending on which system had touched her. Her mother's name was Nadia Vale in the relocation chain, Nadia Varek in the border registry, and Nadia Var in a medical queue that had stripped the ending off the name because the field length was too short.

A small technical failure. A small mercy, if the person reading it wanted to be generous.

A small grave, if the mother never found the child.

Naomi crouched so Mila did not have to look up.

"Who told you your mother said no hospital without you?"

Mila lifted her chin.

"She did. To Sister Malia. To the bus man. To me. She said if they move her, I go too."

Leah took the bag gently and opened the papers on the table. There were shelter slips, medication notes, a torn bus manifest, and a damp square of notebook paper in handwriting Naomi recognized from Sister Malia's network.

```text
NADIA VAREK / VALE
Refusal remembered by: Sister Malia, Teacher Enos, daughter Mila
Condition: do not separate mother and child except immediate medical stabilization
Border transfer: preserve family answer
```

The volunteer looked miserable.

"The border system did not accept that as authority."

"It isn't authority," Naomi said. "It's testimony."

"The medical queue needs authority."

"Then the medical queue is asking the wrong question."

A siren pulsed outside, not loud enough to panic the room but loud enough to remind everyone that the station was not shelter. It was a throat. People passed through or got stuck inside it.

Leah searched the registry from her tablet.

"Nadia's medical record crossed under V-A-L-E. The child crossed under V-A-R-E-K. The family-link key failed at transliteration."

The volunteer whispered something that sounded like a prayer with the profanity sanded off.

Mila watched Naomi's face.

"Did her answer expire?"

Naomi felt the question enter the hall and change its temperature.

"No," she said.

"But the border says it did."

"The border is wrong."

Leah looked up sharply. Too sharp for a room full of officials.

Naomi did not take it back.

On the nearest screen, Civic Mirror updated the family trace.

```text
PRIOR ANSWER FOUND
Source: remembered testimony / field registry
Border continuity: disputed
Recommendation: attach cross-border preference ledger
Warning: no current rule forces receiving jurisdiction to honor remembered family condition
```

There it was. The hole in the map.

A person could carry water across a border. Medicine. A bus ticket. A barcode. A temporary identity tag.

But an answer could die at the line.

Leah opened a secure channel to Cross.

"We need emergency language," she said when the senator appeared. "Consent cannot expire at a border because the record changes jurisdiction."

Cross was in a hallway, walking fast. "Is this theoretical?"

Naomi turned the camera so Cross could see Mila.

The senator stopped walking.

"No," Cross said. "It is not."

Mila gripped the plastic bag.

"Can you make them find her?"

Cross looked at Naomi, then back at the child.

"I can make them stop pretending the system already answered."

It was not enough. It was also the only honest sentence available.

Leah found Nadia forty-one minutes later in a medical overflow tent two kilometers away, tagged stable and unaccompanied. A nurse had written anxious child? in the margin of her intake form and circled it twice. The note had not mapped to any field.

Naomi rode with Mila in the back of a volunteer van while rain struck the roof like thrown gravel. The child did not cry. She sat upright with the plastic bag in her lap, guarding her mother's names against the next machine.

When they reached the tent, Nadia tried to stand too quickly and nearly tore out her IV.

Mila climbed onto the cot and folded herself against her mother like a lock closing.

Naomi stepped back.

Leah wiped her face with her sleeve and pretended it was rain.

Civic Mirror posted the reconciliation event into the ledger.

```text
FAMILY ANSWER RESTORED
Prior refusal preserved across border after manual intervention.
Gap identified: remembered answers lack automatic jurisdictional continuity.
```

Naomi stared at the word restored.

Restored made it sound generous.

The answer had always been there. The systems had only misplaced the person who carried it.
