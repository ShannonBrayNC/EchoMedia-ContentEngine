## Manuscript

The border list had six hundred names and four hundred spellings.

Leah spread the pages across the chapel floor because the table was too small for the living and the missing. Children stepped carefully between columns while volunteers read names into three phones, each connected to a different database that claimed to be the source of truth.

Naomi knelt beside her.

"This one is the same person," Leah said, tapping two entries. "Amina Qadir here. Amina Khadir there. Same daughter. Same scar. Different border."

"The system split her."

"No. People split her. The system believed them."

Outside, families waited under tarps for reunification notices. Mercy Bloc had opened the corridor for minors and medical cases. Consent Markets vendors had offered identity resolution support at no cost. The ministry had accepted. The result was speed with seams.

A girl named Laila arrived holding a folded intake card that had softened at the corners from rain and touch.

"My mother said no buses without my brother," she told Naomi.

Her brother Sami had already crossed under an emergency respiratory transfer. His name matched the clinic feed. Laila's did not. Her mother's refusal lived in one system as family-unit preference, in another as transportation note, and in a third as expired shelter context.

Expired.

Naomi hated the word before the child asked the question.

"Did my mother's answer expire at the border?"

Leah looked away.

The border officer was not cruel. He was worse for the moment: careful.

"If I let her through on a bad match, she could disappear," he said.

Laila gripped the folded card with both hands.

"If you do not," Naomi said, "she disappears here."

Naomi opened Civic Mirror in observer mode and fed it the records. It did not decide. It mapped contradiction.

Name variance.
Kinship variance.
Authority variance.
Prior family-unit refusal detected.
Risk of wrongful separation: high.
Risk of wrongful transfer: non-trivial.
Human review required.

The officer gave a tired laugh. "Human review is what I am doing."

Leah's voice sharpened. "Then be human all the way."

She placed three witnesses on record: the camp teacher, the clinic nurse, and a grandmother who knew both children by the songs their mother sang when washing clothes. None of them had biometric authority. All of them had memory.

The vendor system recommended hold pending identity confidence.

Civic Mirror recommended preserve sibling unit unless specific countervailing harm is documented.

The treaty draft had no clean rule for names across the border.

So the officer wrote one sentence by hand.

Prior family-unit refusal preserved across border under disputed identity record; review to follow; witnesses attached.

Laila crossed toward the clinic corridor with Naomi walking beside her until the gate officer told Naomi she could go no farther.

No one cheered. Cheering would have made it feel simple.

Afterward, Leah gathered the papers and wrote the children's names again on a blank page, one spelling beneath another, until the variants stopped looking like errors and started looking like the path a person had taken through other people's systems.

"A name is not a key," she said.

Naomi folded the witness forms.

"What is it?"

Leah looked toward the border gate.

"A promise to keep looking."
