## Manuscript

The activation request came with casualty estimates attached.

Cross hated that first. Not the estimates. Those were necessary. She hated the formatting: three columns, two confidence bands, red shading where the dead became persuasive. A chart could tell the truth and still arrive dressed for coercion.

Mercy Bloc requested immediate activation of the fast humanitarian exception across three flood corridors and two border intake zones.

No individual record review before routing.

Represented consent by certified field authority accepted as sufficient.

Human contestability after stabilization.

Thorne read the request twice and placed it on the table between them as if paper could become heavier when enough people were not yet dead.

"If we block it," he said, "we own the delay."

Amara stood near the wall display with her notebook open. She had not sat since dawn. No one had. The maps moved too often for furniture to feel honest.

"If we approve it," she said, "we own the disappearance of objections we know exist."

Cross looked at the mortality band again.

Best case: thirty-two avoidable deaths if activation was blocked for twelve hours.

Worst case: one hundred eighteen.

The number did what numbers do when they are placed in front of officials with signatures. It tried to become the only moral fact in the room.

On the secondary screen, Civic Mirror displayed the counter-record: prior refusals, family-separation warnings, medical exceptions, shelter trauma flags, disputed names, contested translations. Not all of them would matter. Some would be wrong. Some would be old. Some would save no one.

Some were people.

Thorne's voice lowered. "We can require post-event review."

"Post-event review does not keep a child with her aunt," Amara said.

"Neither does drowning with perfect consent."

No one answered because the sentence was unfair and useful, which made it politically close to ideal.

The Mercy Bloc envoy joined by video. Her name was Dr. Sana Elian, a field surgeon with gray at her temples and blood under one fingernail. She did not look like a villain. Cross would almost have preferred one.

"We are not asking you to abandon consent," Elian said. "We are asking you not to let procedural purity kill people you will later memorialize with better language."

Cross folded her hands.

"The exception lets field authorities represent entire categories without record review."

"For hours," Elian said. "Not forever."

"Hours are long enough to move someone beyond recovery."

"And long enough for water to move through a ward."

The room returned to the chart.

Thirty-two to one hundred eighteen.

Civic Mirror did not recommend approve or block. It had learned the humility of refusing final verbs. It did, however, add an advisory note.

Activation under casualty pressure creates material consent distortion. If approved, require live exception sampling, protected refusal lanes, sibling-unit preservation, and automatic whole-record review.

Thorne rubbed his eyes.

"That slows it down."

"Less than blocking," Amara said.

"More than Mercy Bloc wants."

Cross looked at Elian on the screen. "Would Mercy Bloc accept conditional activation?"

Elian's jaw tightened. For the first time, the surgeon became an envoy.

"If conditions delay movement, the deaths remain yours."

There it was. Not cruelty. Leverage.

Cross took the activation order and wrote four conditions by hand beneath the signature line. Live sampling. Refusal lane. Sibling preservation. Whole-record review.

Thorne stared at the pen.

"You are choosing to be blamed by everyone."

"No," Cross said. "I am choosing where the blame leaves evidence."

She signed conditional activation.

The corridors opened within the hour.

By nightfall, people were alive who would have died.

By morning, the first family was missing.
