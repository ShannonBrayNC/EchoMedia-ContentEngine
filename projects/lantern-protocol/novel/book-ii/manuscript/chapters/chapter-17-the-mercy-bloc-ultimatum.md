## Manuscript

The ultimatum arrived during the thunder.

Real thunder, hard enough to rattle the treaty hotel windows and make every translator in the glass booths look up at once. Outside, the river had climbed the lower embankment and turned the service roads into black mirrors. Inside, the Mercy Bloc delegation stood at the north table with wet coats over their arms and the expression of people who had stopped asking to be believed.

Senator Cross read the packet once.

Then she read the casualty projection.

Then she stopped reading because the numbers had begun to press against her ribs from the inside.

```text
MERCY BLOC EMERGENCY ACTIVATION NOTICE
Event: Border basin failure / multi-jurisdiction flood corridor
Projected window: 71 minutes
Requested action: Civic Mirror cross-border routing activation
Authority basis: Mercy Bloc emergency compact
Disclosure status: partial
Consent status: degraded
Estimated lives preserved by immediate activation: 312-488
Estimated family-separation risk: elevated
Estimated post-action contestability: undefined
```

Minister Amara Ibarra waited at the end of the room, not behind a flag, not behind counsel, not behind any of the ceremonial furniture states used when they wanted suffering to look organized.

"We activate in seventy-one minutes," Amara said. "With you or without you."

A murmur crossed the room.

Cross did not look at the cameras. Cameras made caution look like cruelty or courage depending on who edited the clip.

"Civic Mirror is not cleared for cross-border activation under this draft," Cross said.

"The water is cleared," Amara answered. "The clinics are cleared. The road failures are cleared. Only your doctrine is still waiting for a chair."

On the secure wall display, Commander Thorne's field feed opened from a mobile coordination truck. Rain struck the windshield behind him so hard the image blurred at the edges. He wore a headset and the look of a man who had seen enough disasters to stop romanticizing urgency.

"Senator," Thorne said, "I have three medical convoys, two school buses, and a shelter intake chain that will split unless someone gives me routing priority. I can move manually. I will lose people manually."

Cross felt the room turn toward her.

That was the cruelty of command. Not that it asked for a decision. That it made refusal look like authorship.

"How many consent records are degraded?" she asked.

Juno Park answered from the second screen. "Enough to matter. Family links are partial. Translation confidence is uneven. Several route alternatives rely on vendor preference relays. Civic Mirror can optimize the path, but the burden trace will be incomplete."

Amara laughed once.

"Incomplete records are not rare in a flood, Senator. They are the flood."

"And after the flood?"

"After the flood, people are alive to sue me."

That landed harder than any slogan Mercy Bloc had brought to the table.

The packet refreshed.

```text
FIELD UPDATE
West clinic generator failure imminent.
Evacuation window revised: 54 minutes.
Recommended activation: immediate.
```

The green activation seal pulsed on the main screen.

A staffer whispered, "If we block this and they die, the hearing ends us."

Cross turned toward him.

"This is not about the hearing."

He flushed.

But he was not wrong. He was only indecently early.

Thorne leaned closer to his camera.

"Senator, permission to speak plainly?"

"Granted."

"I do not need Civic Mirror to be pure. I need it bounded. Give me a rule I can execute in the field. Not a philosophy. A rule."

Amara pointed at the screen as if Thorne had given her the whole argument.

"There. That is what Mercy Bloc has been saying."

Cross opened the draft clause Naomi had been fighting to preserve.

Reachable.

Represented.

Remembered.

Contestable.

Four words. Too fragile for a flood. Too necessary to abandon because of one.

"Commander," Cross said, "if Civic Mirror activates, can you preserve family-unit objections as route constraints where field testimony exists?"

"Some."

"Can you mark every override?"

"If the system generates the docket."

Juno answered, "It can. Not elegantly."

"Elegance is cancelled," Thorne said.

For the first time all day, Cross nearly smiled.

Amara crossed her arms.

"You are negotiating while the water rises."

"Yes," Cross said. "Because that is what authority is when it has not surrendered to panic."

The room went still.

Cross wrote the provisional condition herself. No aide. No counsel. Her fingers moved faster than her fear.

```text
PROVISIONAL BOUNDARY FOR EMERGENCY ACTIVATION
1. Named field authority required.
2. Civic Mirror advisory output may optimize routing only against preserved objection markers.
3. Family separation requires visible burden reason.
4. Unreachable persons must remain flagged as unreachable, not presumed consenting.
5. Emergency overrides open a post-action correction docket.
6. No vendor or ministry agreement converts silence into consent.
```

She sent it to Thorne.

"Can you execute that?"

The commander read it in the storm-lit cab.

"It will slow us."

"Can you execute it?"

"Yes."

Amara's voice went cold. "Mercy Bloc will not let Washington write field law during our activation."

"Then name your authority," Cross said. "Carry the burden openly. Do not let Civic Mirror carry it for you."

For one instant, Amara looked as if she might walk out and press the green seal herself.

Then the west clinic feed opened.

A nurse stood under emergency lights with water running past her boots. Behind her, patients sat in plastic chairs wearing wristbands and evacuation tags. One old woman clutched a photograph in a zip bag.

"Minister," the nurse said, "I need buses. I do not care whose doctrine survives the night."

Cross looked at Amara.

Amara looked at the nurse.

That was the knife-edge, and everyone in the room knew it.

Command wanted one thing.

Ethics wanted another.

The storm wanted both to shut up.

Amara stepped toward the screen.

"Prepare activation," she said.

Cross felt the cameras inhale.

Amara continued.

"With provisional boundary. Named authority pending my signature."

Juno's hands moved across her keyboard. Thorne started giving route instructions to the field teams. The green seal changed shape, no longer clean, no longer easy.

ACTIVATE WITH BURDEN TRACE

Cross looked at the altered control and understood the price. If the boundary failed, Mercy Bloc would blame her for delay. If the activation harmed people, rights groups would blame her for compromise. If the system worked, everyone would claim they had always supported the difficult middle.

"Senator," Amara said, "if people die because of these conditions, I will say so publicly."

"Good," Cross said.

Amara frowned.

Cross held her gaze.

"If restraint has a body count, the record should not hide it either."

Thunder shook the windows again.

On the field screen, Thorne gave the order.

The first convoy moved.
