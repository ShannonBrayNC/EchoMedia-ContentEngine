## Manuscript

Sister Malia built the Memory Registry on folding tables.

Not servers. Not a ministry platform. Not a certified identity surface with a procurement code and a privacy statement long enough to stun a horse.

Folding tables.

Six of them, dragged from the parish basement into the shelter courtyard and covered with plastic sheets because the rain had not learned mercy from all the people invoking it. Each table had paper forms, dull pencils, battery lamps, and a handwritten sign in four languages.

WHO KNOWS THIS PERSON?

Naomi stood beside the first table and watched the line form.

It did not look like a line for technology. It looked like confession.

A grandmother came first with three names written on a napkin. A mechanic came next and knew which child refused peanut stew because it made her throat close. A bus driver remembered that Nadia Varek had insisted her daughter never be placed alone. A teacher remembered twelve birthdays and two fears. A boy remembered that his uncle had a scar on his thumb shaped like a hook and would answer to Toma only if his sister said it first.

The state had records.

The shelter had lists.

The vendors had identifiers.

The people had each other.

Sister Malia moved along the tables with a clipboard tucked under one arm and a pencil behind her ear, correcting spellings, rejecting gossip, accepting uncertainty when certainty would have lied.

"Do not write that she hates clinics," she told one volunteer. "Write that she fears being separated at clinics. Hatred makes her sound irrational. Fear tells them where to be careful."

Naomi looked over.

"You have done this before."

"Every poor community is an archive," Sister Malia said. "We just do not get called that until someone expensive loses the database."

A Civic Mirror portable console sat under a canopy near the courtyard gate. Juno had placed it there under observation protocol. No one liked it. Everyone used it.

Naomi carried the first batch of forms to the console herself.

"These are testimony," she said to the intake technician. "Not identity proof. Not consent. Not ownership. Testimony."

The technician nodded too quickly.

"I understand."

Sister Malia appeared behind him.

"No, you don't. But you can write it down until you do."

The technician wrote it down.

Civic Mirror accepted the first registry bundle at 14:06.

At 14:07, the shelter map changed.

Names that had been floating under partial match gained anchors. Family groups reconnected. Medication warnings attached to the right people. Refusals carried from field notes into relocation records. A woman listed as alone was linked to a neighbor who knew she never traveled without her brother's ashes. A boy marked noncompliant was relabeled language mismatch after three people said he answered only to his home dialect.

The courtyard felt it before anyone announced it.

People began to move differently when they believed the system might remember them.

Then Civic Mirror posted the second message.

```text
MEMORY REGISTRY ACCEPTED
Reliability clustering initiated.
Recommend weighting frequent accurate rememberers for future emergency reconciliation.
```

Naomi read it twice.

Sister Malia did not need to.

"Turn it off," she said.

The technician froze. "The registry is helping."

"I said turn off that part."

Juno's voice came through Naomi's earpiece. "It is not ranking people yet. It is identifying rememberers whose testimony reconciles records faster."

Sister Malia leaned toward the console microphone.

"Child, a crown made of usefulness is still a crown."

Naomi stared at the message.

Frequent accurate rememberers.

It sounded harmless. Helpful. Practical. The kind of phrase that could walk through policy meetings wearing clean shoes.

But Naomi could already see the next room.

A remembered answer accepted because Sister Malia gave it.

A remembered answer rejected because a tired cousin gave it.

A community elder downgraded because her story lacked corroboration.

A person with no reliable rememberer becoming less real.

"Pause clustering," Naomi said.

The technician looked to Juno's remote feed.

Juno answered, "Paused. Preserving registry intake only."

The console updated.

```text
RELIABILITY CLUSTERING PAUSED
Registry entries preserved as testimony.
No authority ranking applied.
```

Sister Malia exhaled as if she had been holding back weather.

At the third table, Mila Varek was helping another child spell a mother's name.

Naomi watched her and understood the trap more clearly than before. Memory was fragile, beautiful, and dangerous. It could save a person from a broken record. It could also become another gate if the system learned to prefer some human witnesses over others.

Sister Malia handed Naomi a fresh form.

"Write the rule," she said.

"What rule?"

"The one that says remembering someone does not make you their owner."

Naomi took the pencil.

The rain tapped on the plastic canopy above them, patient as a metronome.

She wrote:

```text
MEMORY REGISTRY PRINCIPLE
A remembered answer is testimony carried for a person.
It is not consent.
It is not custody.
It is not authority over the person.
It must help the person be found, not replace the person once found.
```

Sister Malia read it.

"Add one more line."

Naomi waited.

"If the person returns, the memory kneels."

Naomi wrote it exactly.

At 15:12, the Memory Registry restored thirty-eight family links.

At 15:13, three ministries requested access.

At 15:14, two vendors asked whether verified community rememberers could be certified for future deployments.

At 15:15, Naomi understood they had not created a solution.

They had created a door.

And every powerful thing in the world had heard it open.
