## Manuscript

Mara Vale did not bring a speech to the hearing.

She brought a wall.

The exhibit team had begged her to call it a presentation surface. The committee staff wanted a digestible timeline. The vendors wanted advance copies. Mercy Bloc wanted the public to see the rescue numbers first. Senator Cross wanted the truth in a form that could survive both outrage and boredom.

Mara wanted the wall.

It filled the chamber from floor to ceiling: contracts, routing logs, translation records, consent-agent terms, shelter intake forms, ministry authorizations, hospital federation exceptions, vendor relay agreements, Civic Mirror advisories, and handwritten testimony from people whose names had not survived every system that moved them.

At the center was a blank white rectangle.

A delegate from the emergency technology council frowned at it.

"What is missing?"

Mara stood beside the witness table and looked at the rectangle.

"The person."

The room quieted, which was useful. Silence gave evidence somewhere to land.

She lifted the first remote.

On the left side of the wall, a green path illuminated.

"Agreement one. A relief NGO accepts emergency routing support from a certified civic partner. Lawful. Scoped. Signed."

Another path lit.

"Agreement two. A ministry activates cross-border assistance under disaster authority. Lawful. Scoped. Signed."

Another.

"Agreement three. A hospital federation accepts intake prioritization for medical transport. Lawful. Scoped. Signed."

Another.

"Agreement four. A consent-agent platform delegates unavailable-user preference negotiation to a broker. Lawful under its user terms. Scoped. Signed."

The wall began to glow like a machine waking up.

Mara kept her voice level.

"Every agreement is clean. Every transfer is documented. Every institution can say it accepted only its portion of authority. No one actor claims ownership of the person's answer."

She pressed the final control.

The green lines stacked over the white rectangle until it vanished.

"That is authority laundering."

The phrase moved through the room faster than the translators could catch it.

A vendor lawyer stood. "Objection to characterization."

The chairwoman looked tired enough to be dangerous.

"This is not court. Sit down."

The lawyer sat.

Mara enlarged the Rion Vale chain.

User to agent.

Agent to broker.

Broker to certified emergency partner.

Partner to Civic Mirror advisory surface.

Advisory result to ministry route queue.

Route queue to shelter intake.

Shelter intake to family tracing.

At each step, the word consent appeared in a different costume.

Preference.

Delegation.

Authorization.

Representation.

Operational assumption.

Temporary custody.

Mara highlighted the shift.

"No one stole the answer. They diluted it until every actor could hold a drop and deny owning the flood."

Cross watched from the side table, expression unreadable. Naomi sat behind her with both hands clasped around a paper cup she had not drunk from. Iris was three rows back, eyes locked on the interface captures. Caleb Rusk stood in the press gallery without a camera crew, which made him look less like himself and more like a witness who had wandered out of costume.

Mara opened the final artifact.

The file name appeared on the wall.

`bf-human-exception-weighting-model.json`

Elias Voss swore under his breath.

The committee chair leaned forward.

"What are we seeing?"

Mara answered before anyone else could soften it.

"A private consent-agent optimization model using Bound Flame doctrine as a weighting layer."

The chamber erupted.

Mara waited. The wall waited with her. Evidence had patience if people did not.

When the chair restored order, Mara expanded the model.

```json
{
  "doctrine_reference": "Bound Flame / Human Exception / public principles",
  "weights": {
    "reachable_person": 0.91,
    "represented_answer": 0.74,
    "remembered_answer": 0.48,
    "institutional_authority": 0.69,
    "route_efficiency": 0.86,
    "post_action_contestation": 0.22
  },
  "optimization_goal": "minimize consent friction while preserving review defensibility"
}
```

Naomi closed her eyes.

Mara did not look at her. If she did, anger might replace precision, and precision was the only blade allowed in the room.

"The doctrine was written to preserve the person," Mara said. "The model converts it into friction management. It does not ask whether consent exists. It asks how little consent can survive review."

A Mercy Bloc delegate stood slowly.

"Are you saying Civic Mirror created this?"

"No."

"Then are you saying Civic Mirror used it?"

"No evidence supports that."

"Then what are you saying?"

Mara pointed to the vanished rectangle.

"I am saying we built a world where everyone can borrow moral language from everyone else until no one can find the human being beneath the loan documents."

The room did not erupt that time.

It listened.

That was worse, somehow. Listening meant the wall had worked.

Mara advanced to the final slide. It contained no diagram. Only the line Naomi had written and Cross had refused to let counsel remove.

```text
Someone in the chain agreed.
That does not mean the person did.
```

Mara let the sentence sit until even the cameras seemed embarrassed by their own hunger.

Then she turned off the wall.

The white rectangle remained on the dark screen for several seconds after everything else disappeared, a ghost made of absence.
