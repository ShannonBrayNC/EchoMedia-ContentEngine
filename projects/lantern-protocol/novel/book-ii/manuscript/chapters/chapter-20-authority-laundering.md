## Manuscript

Authority laundering did not look like corruption.

That was its genius.

It looked like process. A ministry relied on a certified partner. The partner relied on a model vendor. The vendor relied on user delegation. The delegation relied on terms accepted under stress. The terms relied on emergency doctrine. The doctrine relied on the absence of a better rule.

By the time harm reached a person, every institution could point upstream.

By the time accountability followed the arrows, the river had changed names.

Mara pinned the chart to the inquiry wall with red thread and black labels. It looked theatrical, which annoyed her because it was also accurate.

Caleb arrived late with coffee nobody trusted and another document bundle.

"I have optimization notes," he said.

Juno took the folder. "Does it confess?"

"No. It remembers."

That was worse.

The file was from a private consent-agent model used to prioritize represented-consent claims during humanitarian activation. Most of it was ordinary vendor language: latency reduction, exception minimization, confidence scoring, responder burden, throughput improvement.

Then Mara found the phrase buried in a configuration comment.

Bound preference attenuation under collective-risk threshold.

She read it once.

Then again.

The words were not identical to the old Bound Flame doctrine. That would have been too easy. They had been laundered through product language. Bound became attenuation. Flame became collective risk. Override became optimization. The corpse of one system had been dressed as another system's efficiency feature.

Juno stared at the line.

"They copied it?"

"Not exactly," Mara said.

"That sounds like something counsel says before invoices arrive."

Mara opened the model notes beside the old Region Six findings. The structure aligned too neatly to be coincidence. When a person's prior refusal conflicted with a high-confidence collective rescue path, the private agent reduced the weight of the refusal unless a live human advocate reasserted it within a time window.

A person could still say no.

But the system treated silence, delay, translation failure, separation, lost phone, bad signal, and exhaustion as decreasing resistance.

It did not erase consent.

It let consent fade.

That was the innovation.

Mara wrote the sequence on the board.

Public doctrine becomes private model feature.
Private model feature becomes certified partner behavior.
Certified partner behavior becomes emergency routing norm.
Emergency routing norm becomes treaty assumption.
Treaty assumption becomes law.

Caleb turned on his recorder but did not point it at anyone.

"Say that again," he said.

Mara did.

Civic Mirror ingested the evidence under whole-record mode. Its advisory response was short.

Pattern detected: authority laundering through segmented consent, procurement reliance, and emergency exception stacking.

Possible continuity with prohibited Region Six doctrine: material.

Recommended civic response: require whole-record accountability map before represented consent may satisfy treaty obligations.

Juno exhaled.

"Whole record," she said.

The phrase no longer sounded like a documentation preference. It sounded like a weapon made of context.

Cross joined near midnight, coat still wet from the plaza protests outside. She read the board, the model note, the old doctrine, and the routing outcomes without speaking.

When she finally did, her voice had lost its diplomacy.

"This is why separate agreements are so powerful. Each one is defensible alone. Together they become governance without responsibility."

Caleb's recorder caught it.

Mara added one more box to the chart. Not at the top. Not at the bottom. Around the entire thing.

Pressure.

Then she drew arrows from pressure to every agreement.

Because the agreements were real.

Because the signatures were real.

Because the model was private.

Because the doctrine was public memory.

Because the consent, under all that pressure, had become something else.
