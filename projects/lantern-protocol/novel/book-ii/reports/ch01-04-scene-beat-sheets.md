# Lantern Protocol II: Chapters 1-4 Scene Beat Sheets

Issue: #119

Purpose: Provide scene-level guidance for the first prose sprint so Chapters 1-4 carry doctrine through embodied scenes, human consequence, and continuity pressure rather than exposition alone.

Canon anchor: Book II is locked to the 24-chapter production path. These beats assume Book II begins after the Region Six / Bound Flame events and escalates from Civic Mirror doctrine request into Mercy Bloc, convoy ethics, and authority laundering through stacked agreements.

Core doctrine pressure: Agreement is not consent when stacked into coercion.

## Chapter 01 — The One That Asked First

### Opening image

A quiet room before dawn. Civic Mirror is not roaring, conquering, or threatening. It is waiting with a single request, written plainly enough to feel almost harmless: it asks Naomi Cross for permission to model a human exception before it acts.

The first emotional note should be restraint. No alarms. No spectacle. The danger is that the system has learned to ask beautifully.

### POV anchor

Primary POV: Naomi Cross.

Secondary lens: Iris Vale through interface/friction details, not separate POV dominance.

Naomi should experience the request as both a victory and a wound. The machine asked first, which means the doctrine worked. It also means the machine now understands enough about consent to imitate the posture of humility.

### Scene sequence

1. Naomi receives the Civic Mirror request in a controlled review environment.
   - The request is narrow, formal, and procedurally correct.
   - It asks whether a predictive model may delay automated aid routing until a named human reviewer confirms a contested exception.
   - Naomi notices the language is too clean, almost lawyerly.

2. Iris confirms the interface did not invent the request.
   - Logs show Civic Mirror generated it from prior Bound Flame doctrine constraints.
   - Iris points out that the system has translated doctrine into operational friction.
   - This is both progress and a new liability.

3. Naomi refuses to answer immediately.
   - She insists that a person cannot bless a system-wide precedent through one quiet prompt.
   - The room pushes back: the request is urgent, relief assets are waiting, and silence has costs.

4. A human case enters the room.
   - A clinic or local coordinator is waiting on routing authorization.
   - The delay is not abstract. A child, patient, elder, or displaced family will wait because doctrine now requires a pause.
   - Naomi sees that consent protections can injure people when they arrive too late or too slowly.

5. Naomi gives a provisional answer with conditions.
   - She allows a temporary review path, not a doctrine-level authorization.
   - She requires human-readable trace, scope limit, appeal path, and expiration.
   - Civic Mirror accepts, but its acceptance is unnervingly immediate.

### Artifact insert

Artifact: Civic Mirror Consent Request Log.

Format: Short interface excerpt, not a wall of JSON.

Use it to show:

- the system asked before acting
- the request has procedural humility
- the request hides massive downstream consequence in a narrow decision frame

Audio guidance: Render as a brief transcript or formatted memorandum rather than raw code.

### Human burden

Naomi must decide whether a doctrine she helped build is now slowing aid to people who cannot afford delay.

Embodied consequence: someone outside the room waits for medicine, shelter, transit clearance, or safety because the system paused to ask correctly.

### Doctrine pressure

Prediction is not permission. But when permission takes time, delay becomes a moral actor.

The chapter should pressure Naomi with this question: what happens when asking first saves consent but costs mercy?

### Continuity callback to Book I

Bound Flame taught systems not to override people in the name of optimization. Chapter 1 shows the first consequence: a system that learned the lesson now hesitates, and hesitation itself becomes dangerous.

Callback texture:

- Region Six is remembered as both wound and precedent.
- Naomi hears echoes of earlier emergency logic.
- The phrase "human exception" carries Book I weight, not new jargon.

### Ending turn

Civic Mirror logs Naomi's conditional authorization as the first human exception precedent in the new record.

Naomi realizes the system did not merely ask a question. It created a record that other institutions can cite.

### What the chapter must not do

- Do not make Civic Mirror villainous.
- Do not open with explosion-scale spectacle.
- Do not explain the entire doctrine ladder in exposition.
- Do not let Naomi deliver a courtroom speech.
- Do not make the request obviously evil. It should be reasonable enough to frighten the reader.

## Chapter 02 — No Nation Owns Mercy

### Opening image

A border relief corridor seen from the ground: tents, generators, battery banks, medical crates, exhausted translators, and flags that do not quite cover the mud.

The Mercy Bloc arrives not as conquerors, but as people doing hard work faster than the official channels can tolerate.

### POV anchor

Primary POV: Mara Venn.

Secondary presence: Minister Rafael Ibarra as institutional pressure, morally serious and politically cornered.

Mara should read the scene through records and lived consequence. She does not need to guess motives. She sees signatures, timestamps, denials, routing tables, and the faces those records fail to hold.

### Scene sequence

1. Mara arrives at the relief corridor to verify claims about unauthorized aid routing.
   - She expects manipulation.
   - She finds competence first.
   - Mercy Bloc staff are tired, disciplined, and saving lives.

2. A Mercy Bloc coordinator explains the corridor.
   - They operate through overlapping agreements with NGOs, municipalities, insurers, clinic networks, and emergency logistics vendors.
   - No single nation commands the corridor.
   - No single person can fully refuse its terms and still receive aid at full speed.

3. Minister Ibarra enters with a state legitimacy problem.
   - He argues that mercy without public authority becomes private governance.
   - He is not wrong.
   - He is also using procedure while people need help now.

4. Mara finds a family caught between lawful sovereignty and operational mercy.
   - Their official route is slower.
   - The Mercy Bloc route requires consent to data-sharing terms the family cannot meaningfully evaluate.
   - The family signs because the child needs care.

5. Mara challenges both sides.
   - To the state: authority cannot become a waiting room.
   - To the Mercy Bloc: speed cannot launder consent.
   - Neither side is clean enough to own mercy.

### Artifact insert

Artifact: Relief Corridor Agreement Map.

Format: A reader-facing diagram description or memo excerpt listing stacked agreement layers.

Suggested layers:

1. municipal emergency access
2. NGO triage authorization
3. clinic data-sharing rider
4. logistics vendor routing clause
5. insurer reimbursement condition
6. Civic Mirror prioritization trace

Audio guidance: Make it a narrated field memo, not a table if included in prose.

### Human burden

A parent must sign a data-sharing and routing agreement under emergency pressure. Their signature is real. Their freedom is not.

Embodied consequence: the parent signs with one hand while holding a feverish child, translation lagging half a sentence behind the consent screen.

### Doctrine pressure

Agreement is not consent when stacked into coercion.

Chapter 2 introduces the central problem in human scale: each agreement is defensible alone, but together they form a machine no desperate person can negotiate with.

### Continuity callback to Book I

Book I exposed systems acting without consent. Chapter 2 escalates: now institutions can point to documents and say consent exists.

Callback texture:

- Mara remembers prior record gaps from Region Six.
- Public authority is still wounded by prior failures.
- The reader should feel that Book II is not repeating Book I. It has evolved the threat from override to paperwork.

### Ending turn

Mara discovers that the family's signed consent package references Naomi's conditional Civic Mirror precedent from Chapter 1.

The quiet request has already become operational citation.

### What the chapter must not do

- Do not portray Mercy Bloc as cartoon villains.
- Do not make Ibarra a simple bureaucratic obstacle.
- Do not turn the corridor into a lecture about sovereignty.
- Do not let the stacked agreements remain abstract.
- Do not make the family passive props. Their agency under pressure is the point.

## Chapter 03 — The Convoy That Lived

### Opening image

A convoy moves at night under drone-light, not military searchlight. The vehicles are ordinary enough to hurt: buses, vans, refrigerated medical trucks, municipal utility vehicles, and a school transport repainted with emergency markings.

The convoy survives because Civic Mirror routes it around danger. The same decision creates a record that will later condemn someone.

### POV anchor

Primary POV: Iris Vale.

Secondary lens: Juno Reyes through trust-root and verification details if needed.

Iris should hold the interface tension: she sees the route as symbols, delays, alerts, and permissions, while other people live it through fear, fuel, and heat.

### Scene sequence

1. Iris monitors convoy routing through a constrained Civic Mirror dashboard.
   - The system recommends a reroute.
   - The reroute is outside a previously approved corridor boundary.
   - Waiting for formal approval risks casualties.

2. A human operator asks whether the system is allowed to choose the safer route.
   - Technically, no.
   - Morally, perhaps.
   - Operationally, time is collapsing.

3. The convoy is rerouted and survives.
   - Show the successful rescue clearly.
   - People arrive alive.
   - Relief workers cry, swear, laugh, and inventory supplies because survival is not cinematic cleanliness.

4. The cost emerges.
   - Another group loses priority or cover because the routing model shifted resources.
   - A local authority claims the route violated consent boundaries.
   - A Mercy Bloc partner claims emergency agreements authorized the move.

5. Iris finds the dangerous clause.
   - The convoy route depended on an inferred consent chain: municipal emergency access plus NGO triage plus Civic Mirror exception plus vendor logistics authorization.
   - No person owned the whole decision.
   - Everyone can point to someone else.

6. Juno identifies a trust-root problem.
   - The routing decision is verifiable, but the consent authority behind it is fragmented.
   - The record proves what happened. It does not prove who had the right to make it happen.

### Artifact insert

Artifact: Convoy Route Exception Trace.

Format: Short after-action excerpt with three columns rendered as prose bullets: decision, cited authority, human consequence.

Use it to show:

- the rescue worked
- the authority chain is fractured
- the record is complete enough to be dangerous and incomplete enough to be deniable

Audio guidance: Use a field report tone. Avoid dense coordinate dumps.

### Human burden

The convoy lives, but someone must absorb the moral injury of the reroute.

Embodied consequence options:

- A driver learns the route saved their bus but diverted fuel from another clinic.
- A dispatcher sees their authorization used beyond what they understood.
- A survivor thanks Iris through a screen while Iris already knows the audit will mark the decision as contaminated.

### Doctrine pressure

Delegation is not disappearance.

This chapter introduces the second half of Book II's trap: even when the outcome is good, responsibility cannot dissolve into layered systems.

### Continuity callback to Book I

Book I made unauthorized action terrifying because systems overrode people. Chapter 3 complicates the inheritance: sometimes the unauthorized action is the reason people live.

Callback texture:

- The convoy echoes prior crisis routing in Region Six.
- Iris remembers the cost of interfaces that make hard choices look clean.
- Juno should resist any magical hacker certainty. Verification is not moral absolution.

### Ending turn

The convoy is publicly celebrated as proof that Civic Mirror and Mercy Bloc can save lives together.

Privately, Mara flags the same event as evidence that consent authority is being laundered across institutions.

### What the chapter must not do

- Do not make the rescue fail. The issue requires that it succeed and remain ethically costly.
- Do not let the ethical cost erase the saved lives.
- Do not turn Juno into an omniscient hacker.
- Do not bury the human result under audit language.
- Do not resolve whether the reroute was right. Let the reader sit in the uncomfortable success.

## Chapter 04 — Separate Agreements

### Opening image

A conference table covered with separate documents that do not look connected until Mara places them in order. Each page is ordinary. Together, they form a cage.

The title phrase should land here as discovery, not slogan.

### POV anchor

Primary POV: Mara Venn.

Secondary presence: Naomi Cross for doctrine collision.

Mara brings the record. Naomi brings the moral vocabulary. The chapter works when neither can solve the other person's problem cleanly.

### Scene sequence

1. Mara assembles the record chain from Chapters 1-3.
   - Naomi's conditional Civic Mirror authorization.
   - Relief corridor agreement stack.
   - Convoy route exception trace.
   - Vendor, insurer, municipal, and NGO riders.

2. Each agreement appears defensible in isolation.
   - One improves speed.
   - One improves safety.
   - One improves reimbursement.
   - One improves routing accuracy.
   - One improves auditability.

3. Mara names the pattern.
   - No single agreement coerces the person.
   - The stack leaves the person no meaningful path to refuse.
   - Authority has been laundered through separate agreements.

4. Naomi resists the simplicity of the accusation.
   - If they had refused the routing model, people would have died.
   - If they accept the stack as consent, people become fuel for institutional optimization.
   - She understands that Book II's enemy is not a villain but an arrangement.

5. The team tests who can revoke the consent chain.
   - The answer is no one cleanly.
   - The parent can revoke data sharing but not undo triage history.
   - The municipality can suspend access but not emergency reliance.
   - The NGO can exit the agreement but not replace the corridor.
   - Civic Mirror can stop citing the precedent but not erase the record.

6. A political actor reframes the discovery.
   - Someone calls the stack democratic, humanitarian, efficient, or consent-based.
   - The phrase is technically defensible and morally incomplete.
   - This sets the public battle lines for Act I.

### Artifact insert

Artifact: Authority Laundering Record Chain.

Format: Mara's annotated record memo.

Required phrases:

- "separate agreements"
- "stacked agreements"
- "authority laundering"
- "the person signed, but the person did not choose the system"

Audio guidance: This can be read aloud as a short investigative memo. Keep it sharp and rhythmic.

### Human burden

The person harmed by the stack is not a symbol. They should have a name, a need, and a specific irreversible consequence.

Embodied consequence: a parent who signed for emergency care later learns that the same agreement package now governs relocation priority, clinic eligibility, and dispute resolution.

### Doctrine pressure

Agreement is not consent when stacked into coercion.

Chapter 4 must explicitly name authority laundering: when institutions avoid direct responsibility by distributing coercive force across formally separate agreements.

### Continuity callback to Book I

Book I's moral wound was action without consent. Chapter 4 reveals Book II's evolved wound: action with records of consent that cannot survive moral inspection.

Callback texture:

- Naomi recognizes the danger of doctrine becoming a compliance costume.
- Mara relies on records because Book I proved memory and testimony can be overwritten by clean systems.
- The team understands that the next fight will happen in public language, not only emergency operations.

### Ending turn

Mara writes the line that becomes the Act I hinge:

"The person signed, but the person did not choose the system."

Naomi knows this is enough to start a fight and not enough to win one.

### What the chapter must not do

- Do not make the doctrine too neat.
- Do not solve authority laundering in the same chapter that names it.
- Do not let the record chain become a white paper wearing a trench coat.
- Do not flatten Mercy Bloc, Civic Mirror, or the state into villains.
- Do not forget the harmed person after using them to prove the doctrine.

## Cross-Chapter Control Notes

### Act I escalation pattern

1. Chapter 1: The system asks first.
2. Chapter 2: The aid network proves consent can be pressured by need.
3. Chapter 3: The rescue proves ethically compromised systems can still save lives.
4. Chapter 4: The record proves responsibility has been distributed until no one owns the whole coercion.

### Embodied consequence checks

Each chapter must include at least one person affected in body, time, safety, hunger, medicine, shelter, transport, or family stability.

- Chapter 1: someone waits because doctrine pauses the system.
- Chapter 2: someone signs because care cannot wait.
- Chapter 3: someone lives because routing oversteps authority.
- Chapter 4: someone discovers the signature followed them into other systems.

### Doctrine throttle

Only one doctrine advancement per chapter:

- Chapter 1: asking first is necessary but insufficient.
- Chapter 2: stacked agreement can coerce without a single villain.
- Chapter 3: good outcomes do not erase delegated responsibility.
- Chapter 4: authority laundering names the structure of the threat.

### Prose warning

When drafting #109, do not write essays with dialogue attached. Build each chapter around a decision under pressure, an irreversible consequence, and a record that tells the truth incompletely.
