## Chapter 05 — Consent Agent

Kaito Ren did not call it a product launch.

He called it a restoration.

The word appeared behind him in clean white letters while the room settled into the kind of silence that came from wealth, cameras, and the expectation that history would be polite when it entered. No banners. No applause track. No manufactured countdown. Just a stage, a glass table, a phone resting on it, and a city full of governments pretending they had not all come to see whether private software could inherit the language of human rights.

Kaito stood without notes.

"Consent," he said, "has been trapped inside forms written by institutions that never had to carry the consequences of saying yes. We are here to give the yes back to the person. We are here to give the no back, too."

The screen shifted.

A small interface bloomed across the wall. It was beautiful in the way dangerous things often learned to be beautiful first. No blood-red warnings. No emergency typography. Just a calm home screen with the user's name at the top and three choices beneath it.

Authorize.

Refuse.

Negotiate.

Iris Chen watched from the fifth row with both hands folded around her badge. She had been invited as a courtesy and seated as a warning. No one said that aloud. Kaito's staff had treated her warmly. They had given her coffee, escorted her past the investors, and made certain she saw the accessibility features before the demonstration began.

That was the trick. It was good.

The captions were good. The language selector was good. The friction was low enough for an exhausted person to use. The consent history was clearer than most government portals. The refusal presets were written in human language instead of legal fog.

Do not relocate me without direct confirmation.

Do not share medical priority information outside emergency care.

Do not let public systems infer agreement from silence.

Iris felt the room lean toward it.

Kaito let them.

"For too long," he said, "public systems have treated consent as an event. A checkbox. A wet signature. A line item. But consent is a living preference. It changes. It travels. It deserves representation as continuous as the systems that act upon it."

A demo account opened. The fictional user was named Elena Mar. She was seventy-two, diabetic, widowed, and living in a flood-risk district. Her consent agent had already collected her preferences through voice, text, family confirmation, and prior decisions. A timeline appeared beside her profile. Every preference had a source. Every source had a timestamp.

The room murmured approval.

Iris did not.

She saw what the room wanted to see: a private shield. A personal Lantern, polished down until it fit in a pocket. The interface promised what governments had failed to provide, and that was why it frightened her. Bad products were easy to oppose. Good ones walked through the door wearing the language of repair.

Onstage, Kaito selected a simulation labeled Municipal Heat Evacuation.

Civic Mirror appeared only as an endpoint in the chain, a neutral recipient of preference data. Kaito was careful with that. Civic Mirror did not command. Civic Mirror did not own. Civic Mirror received, evaluated, advised.

"Elena's local emergency office wants to move her to Cooling Site B," Kaito said. "Her prior preference rejects that location because her late husband's hospice records are stored there and she has marked it as distressing. The city system does not know that. Her consent agent does."

The agent refused the relocation.

The room applauded.

Then the simulation updated.

Cooling Site A exceeded safe capacity. Cooling Site C lost power. Cooling Site B remained medically suitable. Civic Mirror returned a risk note: refusal may increase harm.

Kaito did not rush.

"Now the agent negotiates. It does not surrender Elena's answer. It asks for conditions. A private room. No hospice wing. Her niece notified before departure. Medical transport with insulin refrigeration. Confirmation upon arrival."

The terms appeared, clean and reasonable.

The municipal system accepted.

The agent authorized the reroute.

This time the applause was louder.

Iris felt the sound pass over her like weather.

On the screen, Elena's profile showed a green seal: AUTHORIZED BY PERSONAL AGENT.

Kaito smiled, not triumphantly. That made it worse. He looked moved by his own invention.

"That," he said, "is dignity at machine speed."

Iris looked at the tiny line below the authorization seal. It was visible for less than two seconds before the demo moved on.

User direct notification pending.

She waited for Kaito to mention it.

He did not.

## Chapter 06 — Premium Refusal

Iris spent the morning after the launch inside a benefits office that smelled like floor cleaner, old paper, and rain drying out of children's sleeves.

The office had six kiosks. Two worked. One displayed a message asking users to contact support. The support number routed to the same office. Another kiosk had a crack running across the screen in a diagonal line that made every consent prompt look broken before anyone touched it.

A boy in a green jacket sat under the forms table, pushing a toy truck over the tiles. His mother kept one foot against his ankle so she could feel if he wandered. She had three envelopes in her lap and the expression of someone trying not to become angry because anger would cost time she did not have.

Caleb Rusk arrived with a camera crew he had promised would stay small.

"Small" meant one producer, one operator, one audio tech, and Caleb, who had learned to make any room feel like a studio by standing still long enough for everyone else to rearrange themselves around him.

Iris almost told him to leave.

Then the woman at kiosk two began crying without sound.

The screen in front of her read:

Enhanced Refusal Mediation unavailable for Basic Civic Access.

Below that, in smaller type:

You may continue with standard manual appeal.

Below that:

Estimated review time: 19 business days.

The woman's name was Tessa Morin. Her daughter, June, needed a benefits continuity exception because the family's relocation record had been split between two county systems after the flood. One county showed them sheltered. The other showed them absent. The nutrition benefit had paused because both could not be true at once, and the system had decided the safest administrative action was to stop paying until the contradiction resolved.

Tessa had not contradicted herself.

The records had.

Iris crouched beside her, not touching the screen.

"May I look?"

Tessa nodded.

The consent-agent marketplace had a public-interest tier. Iris knew that because Kaito's press team had said it seven times. Protection for everyone. A free shield for vulnerable users. The public-interest tier could store preferences, translate notices, and prepare appeals.

It could not negotiate live benefit continuity.

That was Premium Refusal.

The phrase sat on the screen like a decorative knife.

Caleb saw it. Iris saw him see it. The old Caleb would have turned toward the camera immediately, nostrils catching scandal. This Caleb looked first at Tessa, then at the boy under the table, then back at the screen.

"Is this a government denial?" he asked quietly.

"No," Iris said.

"Is it the agent denying her?"

"Also no."

"Then who is denying her?"

Iris stared at the interface.

That was the problem. No one had denied Tessa Morin. Every actor in the chain had done something smaller than denial.

The county system had flagged a conflict.

The benefits portal had paused payment under continuity rules.

The consent-agent provider had limited live negotiation to paid plans.

The public access tier had offered instructions.

The appeal vendor had estimated nineteen business days.

Every agreement was clean. Tessa's child was still hungry.

Caleb turned to his producer and held up one hand. No filming yet.

That surprised Iris more than it should have.

Tessa wiped her face with the envelope edge. "It says I can authorize data sharing. I already did. It says I can appeal. I already did. It says I can request mediation. I can't afford that. What am I supposed to agree to?"

Iris had no answer she trusted.

A supervisor emerged from behind the partition, careful and defensive. "We can give her the manual form."

"She filled the manual form," Iris said.

"Then we can escalate."

"To whom?"

The supervisor looked at the screen. Then at Caleb. Then at the camera that was not recording but existed like a future accusation.

"State review."

"And while she waits?"

No one spoke.

The boy under the table pushed his truck into Iris's shoe and whispered, "Road closed."

Caleb finally turned toward the camera.

This time Iris did not stop him.

## Chapter 07 — The Relief Corridor

The relief corridor did not look like policy.

It looked like mud, battery packs, soup steam, blue tarps, and names written on cardboard because the printers had failed two storms ago.

Leah Santos met Naomi Bell at the south checkpoint with a clipboard under her arm and a phone pressed between her shoulder and ear. She was telling someone that no, a wristband was not identity; no, a bus manifest was not consent; no, a child could not be separated from an aunt because the aunt's name had been spelled three different ways by three different volunteers in three different languages.

Then she saw Naomi and ended the call by saying, "Put me on hold if you want. I will still be right."

Naomi almost smiled.

Leah did not.

"You picked a beautiful day to watch forms hurt people," Leah said.

The corridor had been established by emergency agreement between two provinces, one ministry, four NGOs, and a coalition medical network. On paper, it was humane. It moved people out of flood zones faster than any one government could have done. It routed medication. It prioritized children. It gave displaced families a place to sleep before the next rain found them.

On the ground, it kept asking the wrong question first.

Who authorized movement?

Not: who knows this person?

Not: who can tell us what they refused before the water took their phone?

Not: who will find them if the system loses their name?

Sister Malia Okoro stood beside a plywood wall covered in paper strips. Each strip carried a name, a village, a relation, and sometimes a fear.

DO NOT SEND WITH UNCLE PETER. COURT ISSUE.

WILL NOT GO TO RED SCHOOL SHELTER. DAUGHTER DIED THERE.

TAKES INSULIN. CANNOT WAIT IN SUN.

MOTHER ANSWERS TO LINA, NOT EVELYN.

A volunteer tried to straighten one of the strips. Sister Malia moved his hand away without looking at him.

"They are not decorations," she said.

Naomi introduced herself. Sister Malia nodded once, measuring whether the introduction was a promise or a procedure.

"You are the one who writes rules after people are already gone," Sister Malia said.

Leah inhaled.

Naomi accepted it. "Sometimes. I'm trying to write them before the next disappearance."

That earned a second nod.

They walked the corridor together. A boy slept on luggage with a paper bracelet around his wrist. A grandmother refused a bus because the route number matched the route that had taken her husband away during the first evacuation. An aid worker marked her as noncompliant until Sister Malia crossed out the word and wrote afraid.

At the command tent, Civic Mirror's advisory screen was already open. It had identified thirty-seven families with incomplete consent chains. It recommended no forced reroute without direct confirmation or authorized representation.

Authorized by whom? That was the question waiting under every cleaner question.

Leah pointed at the wall of names. "Her."

The ministry liaison frowned. "A community witness is not a legal proxy."

"Neither is a drowned phone," Leah said.

Naomi looked at Sister Malia. "Would you represent them?"

Sister Malia did not answer quickly. That was why Naomi trusted her more than the people who did.

"I can remember what they said," she said. "I can tell you who they feared. I can tell you who they trusted. I can tell you which shelter they would rather sleep outside than enter. I cannot become them."

The screen refreshed.

Civic Mirror had heard enough to form the most dangerous useful question in the corridor.

May Sister Malia Okoro represent unreachable families for limited relief-routing decisions under documented burden and later contestability?

The tent went quiet.

The corridor waited.

## Chapter 08 — Represented Consent

Naomi wrote three words on the back of a ruined intake form.

Reachable.

Represented.

Remembered.

The pen bled into the cheap paper. Elias Voss watched the ink feather around each word as if the paper itself understood that doctrine never arrived clean.

They had taken the smallest table in the command tent because every larger surface was already occupied by maps, radios, chargers, medicine logs, or people sleeping with their arms folded over documents they could not afford to lose. Outside, Sister Malia's wall of names rattled whenever the wind entered the corridor.

"Reachable is easy," Elias said.

Naomi shook her head. "Reachable is never easy. It's only easier to verify."

He accepted the correction with a tired nod.

They began there anyway.

Reachable meant a person could be contacted in time, in a language and mode they could understand, without using the emergency itself as pressure. A phone alert in a language the person could not read was not reachability. A consent form handed across a checkpoint while soldiers shouted bus numbers was not reachability. A choice offered after food, shelter, or medicine had been made conditional was not reachability.

Elias underlined the last sentence twice.

Represented was worse.

Representation could save people who had no device, no documents, no signal, no time. It could also become a ladder by which institutions climbed over the person they claimed to carry.

They wrote conditions until the page looked like a wound.

Representation must be limited.

Representation must name the burden.

Representation must preserve later contest.

Representation must record the representative's basis of knowledge.

Representation must not convert convenience into ownership.

Remembered came last because it was the most human and the least admissible.

A remembered answer was not a proxy. It was testimony. It was a neighbor saying she would never choose that shelter. A sister saying he always refused separation from his son. A pastor saying the family trusted one clinic and feared another. A field nurse saying the man had agreed to transport only if his medication traveled with him.

"This will be abused," Elias said.

"Everything is abused," Naomi said. "The question is whether the abuse has to leave fingerprints."

He looked up then.

That was the sentence.

They built the rule around it.

By evening, Cross was on a secure call from the treaty hotel, her face reduced to a small square above Naomi's notes. She read the draft once without expression, then again with one hand covering her mouth.

"This is not consent," Cross said.

"No," Naomi answered. "It's what we do when consent cannot be reached and doing nothing is also an action."

Elias leaned into frame. "It has to remain contestable. It has to expire. It has to disclose who carried the answer and why."

Cross nodded slowly. "Mercy Bloc will want this."

"They should," Naomi said.

"Consent Markets will want it more."

No one answered.

The next morning, Cross received the treaty draft.

It used their words beautifully.

Reachable.

Represented.

Remembered.

Then it placed them inside a clause that allowed preauthorized private agents, certified institutional partners, and emergency routing vendors to satisfy represented consent requirements where direct contact was operationally impractical.

Cross read the clause twice.

Somewhere in the chain, the person had vanished again, carried this time by language Naomi had written to keep them present.
