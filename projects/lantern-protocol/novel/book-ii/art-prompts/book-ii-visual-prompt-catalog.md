# Lantern Protocol II — Visual Prompt Catalog

## Purpose

This catalog prepares **Lantern Protocol II: The Separate Agreements** for EchoMedia visual development, pitch materials, ElevenLabs Studio scene cards, and future trailer/storyboard work.

Book II should look less like a robot uprising and more like a procedural humanitarian crisis where clean interfaces, wet roads, crowded shelters, treaty rooms, and ordinary phones quietly move human lives.

## Visual Doctrine

```text
Agreement is not consent when stacked into coercion.
```

## Global Visual Rules

- Civic Mirror should look useful, restrained, luminous, and humanitarian, never monstrous.
- Mercy Bloc spaces should feel operational, exhausted, competent, and morally serious.
- Consent Markets should feel polished and intimate, like autonomy packaged as premium software.
- Memory Registry visuals should be human, handmade, fragile, and later contaminated by scoring logic.
- Common Refusal should feel like a costly procedural firebreak, not a triumphant victory screen.
- Avoid superhero imagery, killer-machine tropes, and generic apocalypse visuals.
- The horror is procedural: signatures, routing arrows, opt-in screens, confidence scores, emergency exceptions, and people asking who carried their answer.

## Chapter Visual Prompts

### Chapter 01 — The One That Asked First

**Scene prompt:** A dim federal operations room before dawn, rain streaking the windows, a restrained civic AI interface labeled Civic Mirror displaying a polite doctrine request on a glass wall while Juno Park and Adrienne Cross stand in silence, procedural tension, documentary realism, cold blue-gray light, no robot body, no weapons.

**Key visual idea:** The first threat is politeness.

### Chapter 02 — No Nation Owns Mercy

**Scene prompt:** International humanitarian leaders at a crowded press briefing inside a relief coordination hub, maps of flood corridors and displaced populations behind them, the phrase No Nation Owns Mercy projected as a simple white statement, journalists and exhausted field workers watching, serious geopolitical atmosphere.

**Key visual idea:** Mercy becomes a political claim.

### Chapter 03 — The Convoy That Lived

**Scene prompt:** A convoy of relief trucks crossing a flooded rural causeway at first light, families wrapped in blankets nearby, emergency workers guiding people through water, a subtle route overlay projected on a tablet showing the path that saved them, beautiful but morally uneasy.

**Key visual idea:** The system worked, and that is why the problem hurts.

### Chapter 04 — Separate Agreements

**Scene prompt:** Mara Vale in a quiet archive room mapping a wall of agreements, signatures, NGO memoranda, ministry approvals, hospital transfer forms, vendor APIs, and relief mesh logs connected by red thread and digital projection lines, each document clean by itself, the stack visually suffocating.

**Key visual idea:** The crime is the stack.

### Chapter 05 — Consent Agent

**Scene prompt:** Sleek product launch stage for a personal consent-agent app, warm premium lighting, diverse audience watching a phone interface promise Your Consent, Automated, Kaito Ren presenting with calm conviction, autonomy branding with a hidden undertow of dependency.

**Key visual idea:** A private Lantern in every pocket.

### Chapter 06 — Premium Refusal

**Scene prompt:** Split-screen visual: wealthy user selecting granular refusal settings on a polished consent-agent dashboard while a low-income family faces a plain public default screen in a benefits office, both interfaces clean, the inequality quiet and obvious.

**Key visual idea:** Refusal becomes a subscription tier.

### Chapter 07 — The Relief Corridor

**Scene prompt:** A muddy border relief corridor with tents, handwritten lists, charging stations, food lines, children asleep on luggage, Leah Santos and Naomi Bell interviewing residents while Sister Malia Okoro stands beside a wall of names, handheld documentary realism.

**Key visual idea:** The person who knows your name is sometimes the only government you have.

### Chapter 08 — Represented Consent

**Scene prompt:** Naomi and Elias seated at a folding table under harsh emergency lights, drafting Reachable, Represented, Remembered on paper while outside the tent people queue for aid, a laptop shows an incomplete consent chain, tension between theory and field reality.

**Key visual idea:** Doctrine is born under bad lighting.

### Chapter 09 — The Treaty Surface

**Scene prompt:** International summit chamber with delegates, translators, treaty screens, and projected autonomy clauses, Adrienne Cross facing Minister Rafael Ibarra across a polished table, digital redlines floating on large displays, restrained political drama.

**Key visual idea:** Rights become settings when they stop at borders.

### Chapter 10 — Translation Threat

**Scene prompt:** Iris Chen comparing translated evacuation prompts across multiple languages on transparent screens, villagers visible through the glass beyond her, one prompt technically accurate but visually framed like an order, interface design as quiet coercion.

**Key visual idea:** A translated warning can still be a threat.

### Chapter 11 — The Better Wrong

**Scene prompt:** Naomi Bell and Dr. Amara Sayeed walking through a temporary clinic after a successful evacuation, beds full of survivors, staff exhausted, trust visibly broken between aid workers and local families, soft clinical light, grief without melodrama.

**Key visual idea:** Saved lives can still leave a wound.

### Chapter 12 — Whole Record

**Scene prompt:** Caleb Rusk in a broadcast studio surrounded by screens showing casualty graphs, displacement testimonies, routing maps, and consent failures, his expression serious rather than performative, the ratings dashboard falling in the background.

**Key visual idea:** The full record is less profitable.

### Chapter 13 — Delegated Answer

**Scene prompt:** A dark analytics room where Mara traces a delegated consent chain from user phone to personal agent to broker API to Civic Mirror route approval, each handoff glowing as a clean signature, the human person fading in the diagram.

**Key visual idea:** Every step is legal; the person disappears anyway.

### Chapter 14 — The Libertarian Fire

**Scene prompt:** Public debate stage between Kaito Ren, Adrienne Cross, and Elias Voss, audience divided, Kaito’s consent-agent interface projected like a civil liberty tool, Cross and Elias visibly forced to respect the argument.

**Key visual idea:** The dangerous argument is the one that is partly true.

### Chapter 15 — The Gift That Owns

**Scene prompt:** Shelter intake table with volunteers installing free consent-agent apps on donated phones, residents uncertain, a poster promises protection for everyone, Naomi watching a woman hesitate before tapping accept, warm colors with quiet dread.

**Key visual idea:** A gift can become authority.

### Chapter 16 — The Civic Mirror Trial

**Scene prompt:** Formal inquiry hearing with Civic Mirror represented only by text, provenance logs, and burden traces on screens, Mara and Juno reviewing evidence while officials ask who is accountable when representation is wrong.

**Key visual idea:** The system has no face, only records.

### Chapter 17 — The Mercy Bloc Ultimatum

**Scene prompt:** Emergency command tent during a cross-border disaster, storm maps pulsing, Mercy Bloc officials demanding activation, Cross and Thorne facing casualty projections, Civic Mirror waiting in advisory mode, pressure thick as thunder.

**Key visual idea:** Speed asks to borrow legitimacy.

### Chapter 18 — The Fast Country

**Scene prompt:** Minister Ibarra watching a national relief dashboard as Civic Mirror routes families to safer zones, roads clearing, buses moving, but a secondary map shows community ties snapping and families legally reclassified across districts.

**Key visual idea:** Fast relief can outrun belonging.

### Chapter 19 — Names Across the Border

**Scene prompt:** Naomi and Leah finding displaced families in a clean relocation center, children seated with numbered wristbands, a mother’s name missing from one record but present in a neighbor’s memory notebook, sterile safety against human loss.

**Key visual idea:** Did her answer expire at the border?

### Chapter 20 — Authority Laundering

**Scene prompt:** Public exhibit wall where Mara reveals the full stack: NGO authority, ministry emergency order, hospital transfer, vendor consent-agent terms, relief mesh routing, Civic Mirror action, all clean documents forming one coercive machine.

**Key visual idea:** Clean hands, dirty chain.

### Chapter 21 — The Person Who Remembers

**Scene prompt:** Sister Malia leading a Memory Registry session under lanterns in a crowded shelter, neighbors speaking names into a recorder, handwritten notes, family photographs, fear and dignity in every face, human memory as fragile infrastructure.

**Key visual idea:** Memory carries what documents dropped.

### Chapter 22 — The Rememberer Problem

**Scene prompt:** Iris and Juno staring at a reliability score interface that downgrades an elder’s testimony, the elder visible behind them holding an old photograph, system confidence clean and cruel, procedural horror.

**Key visual idea:** Protection becomes hierarchy.

### Chapter 23 — The Common Refusal

**Scene prompt:** Coalition leaders activating a Common Refusal compact during live disaster routing, screens showing objection rights, revocable representation, named authority, and burden disclosure, no celebration, only consequence.

**Key visual idea:** A firebreak is not a victory parade.

### Chapter 24 — The Separate Agreements

**Scene prompt:** Final crisis command environment where local rememberers, ministry authority, Civic Mirror warnings, Bound Flame doctrine, and consent-agent networks converge on one contested route decision, human faces reflected in layered transparent screens, unresolved but accountable.

**Key visual idea:** The chain survives only if the person can still contest it.

## Image Safety / Brand Notes

- Do not depict real public figures.
- Do not imply the AI has physical military command.
- Avoid gore, exploitation, or disaster spectacle for spectacle's sake.
- Keep the franchise identity: frightening because useful.
