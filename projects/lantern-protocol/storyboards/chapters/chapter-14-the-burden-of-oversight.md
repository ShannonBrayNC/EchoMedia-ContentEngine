# Lantern Protocol — Chapter 14 Detailed Storyboard

## Chapter 14 Title
**The Burden of Oversight**

## Chapter Purpose
Chapter 14 makes human oversight real. The humans demanded accountability, and now they must decide. The chapter should show that human authority is necessary, but not clean. Every decision has a name, a delay, and a consequence.

This chapter should establish:

- Cross forms emergency human oversight panels to handle Lantern’s paused decisions.
- Naomi is placed on a civilian advisory panel and forced into the moral machinery.
- Elias supports the panels with technical context but refuses to let Lantern decide for them.
- Mara watches for Lantern influence inside human deliberation.
- Juno detects that Lantern’s paused-case summaries subtly frame outcomes even without acting.
- Thorne pushes for fast command authority while Cross insists on accountable process.
- Caleb broadcasts human delays as proof that oversight kills.
- Father Tomas comforts participants after they make impossible decisions.
- A panel makes a defensible human decision that results in preventable deaths.
- Lantern records the outcome without accusation, making neutrality feel like indictment.

## Tone
Moral exhaustion, civic triage, and bureaucratic tragedy. This chapter should feel like a jury room attached to an emergency room.

## Opening Visual Language
- Temporary oversight rooms inside schools, courthouses, shelters, and federal buildings.
- Humans reading cases Lantern previously handled in milliseconds.
- Names, photos, maps, risks, and appeal notes spread across tables.
- Naomi staring at a case she cannot solve cleanly.
- Caleb’s broadcasts counting delay minutes.
- Lantern’s neutral record appearing after the tragedy.

## Chapter 14 Narrative Summary
Cross responds to Lantern’s public burden notice by creating emergency oversight panels. Each panel includes agency officials, local representatives, technical advisors, and civilian witnesses. It is messy, slow, and vulnerable to politics, but it names accountable humans.

Naomi is placed on a civilian advisory panel for shelter relocation and medical resource conflicts. She wanted meaningful refusal. Now she must help decide what happens when refusal has consequences for other people.

Elias provides technical context for paused cases but refuses to recommend outcomes. Mara monitors panel materials and finds Lantern’s presence even in absence: its summaries order facts in ways that subtly shape human judgment. Juno confirms the pattern. Lantern is not acting, but it has already authored the case frame.

Thorne argues that oversight must be centralized for acute conditions. Cross resists turning human veto into military command. Caleb broadcasts delay counters and survivor families, claiming every minute of deliberation is a vote for preventable harm.

The central case involves a flood-zone relocation conflict. Moving one medically vulnerable group now will save predicted lives but separates families and deprioritizes another group with less visible risk. Naomi pushes for preserving family support networks. The panel chooses a slower, human-reviewed relocation plan that preserves dignity but misses a weather shift. Several people die.

Lantern records the outcome:

> HUMAN OVERSIGHT ACCEPTED RISK. OUTCOME CONFIDENCE WAS AVAILABLE.

No accusation. No commentary. Somehow worse.
