# Chapter 2: The Panic Call

## Canon Sources

- Parent epic: #159
- Sprint: #160
- Chapter 1: The Wrong Initial

## Purpose

Convert the administrative dread into a manufactured emergency. Jack is nowhere near the danger, but an official record has already started moving with his name attached.

## POV

Jack Mercer

## Chapter Promise

An alarm-company call turns Jack from observer into suspect before anyone asks what actually happened.

## Manuscript

The school gym smelled like floor wax, wet coats, and the industrial cheese they melted over nachos during basketball season.

Jack stood in the back row with the other parents who had arrived late enough to avoid folding chairs but early enough to be seen making an effort. Kelly's class lined the risers under a banner that read SERVICE LEARNING SHOWCASE in cheerful blue letters. Somebody had taped paper lanterns along the wall. They were meant to be decorative.

To Jack they looked like evidence bags turned inside out.

He made himself stop thinking that way.

Kelly spotted him and gave the smallest possible wave. Jack returned it with the smallest possible salute. She made a face, but she stood taller afterward.

He had promised he would come. He had come. That mattered.

The first presentation was about a canned-food drive. The second was about interviewing elderly neighbors. The third involved a slide deck with too many fonts and a boy named Evan saying community resilience as if the words had been assigned to him by a hostile court.

Jack checked the exits. Gym doors left and right. Rear hall. Stage access. Habit, not fear. Maggie said there was no difference if it made people around him feel inspected.

"Jack?"

He turned. Denise Harrow stood beside the folded bleachers in a green raincoat, holding a paper cup of coffee from the school lobby. Her son had played soccer with Kelly since fourth grade. Denise had borrowed Jack's truck once when her basement flooded. She had sent him a Christmas card with a handwritten note about how good he was with the kids.

Now she looked at him as if she had been rehearsing concern.

"Hey, Denise."

"I wasn't sure you'd come."

The words arrived softly. That made them worse.

Jack kept his face neutral. "Kelly asked me to."

"Of course. That's good. I just meant, after everything."

The gym noise thinned. Not really. Children still shuffled paper. A microphone squealed. Parents coughed and whispered and checked phones. But Jack's attention narrowed until Denise's raincoat buttons became bright little points.

"After what?"

Denise's mouth tightened with instant regret. "I don't want to get involved."

"You brought it up."

"I know. I shouldn't have. I just heard there was an incident, and with your background, and Maggie trying to keep things stable, I thought..."

Jack waited.

Denise looked toward the risers. Kelly was laughing at something a girl beside her had whispered. She had no idea the room had changed shape.

"I thought everyone was being careful," Denise finished.

"Who is everyone?"

"Jack."

"Denise, who told you there was an incident?"

She lowered her voice. "It wasn't like gossip."

"Then it should have a source."

Her eyes flashed, hurt now, defensive. "This is exactly what I mean. You get very intense about wording."

"Because wording is how people do damage without touching anything."

He regretted it as soon as he said it. Not because it was untrue. Because Denise stepped back.

On the risers, Kelly's group began their presentation about veteran interviews. Jack heard his daughter's voice come through the speakers, thin with microphone nerves and brave anyway.

"My dad says service is not what happened to you," Kelly read from her index card. "It's what you keep doing after."

Something inside Jack folded.

Denise murmured, "I hope things calm down for all of you," and moved away before he could ask what things meant.

Jack watched Kelly finish her line. Parents clapped. The paper lanterns trembled in the gym air. He clapped too, because his daughter deserved the sound, even if his hands felt borrowed.

His personal phone buzzed.

Unknown number.

He let it go to voicemail.

It buzzed again before the first voicemail finished.

Unknown number.

He stepped into the hall.

The corridor outside the gym was lined with student art and laminated behavior expectations: Be Kind. Be Safe. Be Responsible. The words had rounded edges and bright borders. Jack hated how often harmless words learned to carry weight.

The phone buzzed a third time.

He answered.

"Mr. Mercer?" A woman's voice. Young. Professional. Reading from a screen but trying not to sound like it. "This is NorthStar Residential Monitoring. We received a panic activation from the residence on Ashford Bend. Are you currently on site?"

Jack looked through the small gym window. Kelly was handing the microphone to Evan. "No. I'm at my daughter's school."

"Can you confirm your full name?"

"Why is my name attached to a panic activation?"

A pause. Keyboard taps. "Sir, you are listed in the incident notes as a related contact."

"Related how?"

"I only have what is on the screen."

"Read it to me."

"I can't disclose all notes during an active emergency response."

"Then tell me whether I am being accused of something."

Another pause.

The corridor seemed to tilt slightly. A sixth-grade poster about kindness peeled away from the wall at one corner.

"Sir," the operator said, softer now, "officers have already been dispatched."

Jack closed his eyes.

There it was. Not truth. Movement.

"Dispatched where?"

"To the Ashford Bend residence."

"I am not there. I have not called your company. I have not touched that alarm system. I have not had access to that house since last week."

"I understand."

"No," Jack said. "You are recording. So record this exactly. I am at Ridgeway Middle School, standing outside the gym, during my daughter's assembly. There are witnesses. I did not initiate a panic alarm. I did not call NorthStar. I do not know why my name is in your notes."

"I can add your statement."

"No. Add what I said."

"Sir, that will be in the report."

The sentence landed with a small, final click.

Not believed. Not checked. Added.

In the report.

A door opened at the far end of the corridor. Principal Wexler stepped out of the office, holding a radio. He saw Jack and looked away too quickly.

Jack lowered the phone.

Through the gym window, Kelly looked toward the hall. She could always find him in a room. It was one of the private miracles of fatherhood, and now it felt like evidence waiting to be spoiled.

"Mr. Mercer?" the operator said.

"I'm here."

"Please remain available for law enforcement contact."

"Am I required to stay here?"

"I can't advise you on that."

"Did someone say I was at the residence?"

"I can't disclose all notes."

"Did someone say I called?"

"I can't disclose all notes."

"Did someone say I was dangerous?"

The operator did not answer quickly enough.

Jack's free hand found the locker beside him. Cold metal. Painted blue. A tiny dent near the handle. He focused on that dent because if he did not give his mind a physical object, it would run ahead to the place where sirens lived.

"Sir," she said, "officers may need to speak with you."

"They know where I am?"

"The notes include your current location."

Jack looked at the gym window again.

Kelly's presentation was over. She was searching the back row. His empty spot had become a question on her face.

"How did you get my current location?" Jack asked.

The operator's voice changed, almost imperceptibly. "It was provided by the caller."

"What caller?"

"I can't disclose all notes during an active emergency response."

Behind him, the corridor doors opened.

Two uniformed officers entered from the front lobby, walking with the careful calm of people trained not to look hurried around children. One had his hand near his radio. The other scanned the hallway, found Jack, and kept walking.

Principal Wexler stayed by the office door.

He looked sorry.

That frightened Jack more than anger would have.

"Sir?" the operator said.

"Officers are here," Jack said.

"Please cooperate with law enforcement."

"Tell me your name."

"Amanda."

"Amanda, preserve the call log. Preserve the caller ID. Preserve the full notes. Preserve the timestamp. Do not overwrite anything."

"Sir, I cannot take preservation instructions."

"Then put that in the report too."

The officers stopped six feet away.

The older one said, "Mr. Mercer?"

Jack ended the call and put the phone in his pocket, slowly, because sudden movement had become a language other people could translate without him.

"Yes."

"We need you to step with us."

From inside the gym, applause rose again, bright and ordinary.

Jack glanced once through the window.

Kelly saw the officers.

The room did not go silent. That was the worst part. Life kept making noise while his began to separate from it.

"Am I being detained?" Jack asked.

The younger officer shifted his weight.

The older one said, "We just need to talk."

Jack had spent enough years around official sentences to know when one had no back door.

"Then let's talk where my daughter can't see."

The older officer's face moved, just a little. Respect, maybe. Or pity wearing a badge.

They walked him down the corridor past the student art, past Be Safe, past Be Responsible, past Principal Wexler's office where a radio whispered in a voice too low to understand.

Jack counted the steps because counting was better than pleading.

At the lobby doors, he looked back.

Kelly stood in the gym entrance, small among parents, her face pale and furious with fear.

Jack wanted to say: I did not do anything.

He did not say it.

The wrong sentence could become the first sentence they wrote down.
