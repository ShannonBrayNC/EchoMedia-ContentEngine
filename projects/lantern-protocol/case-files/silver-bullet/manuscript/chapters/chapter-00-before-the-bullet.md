## Manuscript

The room was not the room.

Jack Mercer knew that before he opened his eyes.

The air was wrong. Too dry. Too hot. Full of dust he could taste behind his teeth. Somewhere beyond the dark, a radio coughed static and half a call sign. A door banged against a wall. Someone shouted for a medic with the cracked, furious voice of a man who already knew the medic would be late.

Jack could not see his hands.

That was always the first terror.

Not the noise. Not the heat. Not the red smear of light bleeding under the edge of the door. His hands. In the dream they were somewhere below him, gloved and numb and slow, holding a weapon he could not lift fast enough or a body he could not save or a flashlight that kept finding the wrong faces.

The radio coughed again.

Mercer, move.

He moved.

His right shoulder answered with fire.

The dream folded wrong. Afghanistan became Iraq became the little hallway outside his daughters' bedrooms, all of it spliced together by the same thin strip of light under a door. Jack reached for the handle and the handle moved away from him. The house had learned to breathe. The walls inhaled. The floor tilted. A child's voice said Dad, not loud, not afraid yet, and that was worse than screaming because it meant there was still time to fail.

Mercer, move.

He woke sitting upright in bed with his left hand locked around the edge of the nightstand and his right arm tucked against his ribs like something borrowed from a body that had not agreed to come back.

The bedroom was dark.

North Carolina dark. Not desert dark. Not convoy dark. Not the thick machine-dark of a room cleared too late.

His clock read 3:17 a.m.

Jack did not move at first. Moving too quickly gave the dream somewhere to hide. He breathed through his nose, counted four on the way in and six on the way out, and named the room without whispering.

Ceiling fan.

Laundry chair.

Blue curtains.

Phone charger.

Maggie's book on the floor, face down, because she folded pages even though he had bought her bookmarks for fifteen years.

Home.

Not there.

Here.

His shoulder throbbed in time with his pulse. That was real too. The war had left one kind of wound. Surgeons had left another. The VA had given both wounds names, percentages, and medication refill schedules, as if naming pain made it stand in line.

Jack flexed his fingers. One. Two. Three. Four. Five.

Then he checked the hallway.

He hated that part. Not because checking was irrational. Because sometimes it was not. He had spent too much of his life being paid to notice what other people missed, and the habit did not surrender simply because the mortgage was domestic and the enemy was memory.

He stood slowly, keeping his right arm close. His feet found the cool hardwood. The house creaked once, ordinary as an old dog.

He opened the bedroom door.

The hallway was quiet.

Kelly's door was cracked three inches, the way she had left it since she was six and decided monsters respected partial boundaries. Eden's door was shut, a warning to all living creatures that teenage sleep was a protected species. Downstairs, the refrigerator hummed. Outside, a truck passed on the road and faded toward town.

No one was afraid.

That mattered.

Jack stood there until his heart believed it.

Then he went to the bathroom, splashed cold water on his face, and looked at himself in the mirror.

Fifty-three years old. Bald. A little heavier than he wanted to be. Eyes bloodshot. Scar tissue under the right sleeve of his T-shirt. A man who had been trusted with rooms full of secrets and still sometimes needed to count towel hooks to convince his nervous system it was home.

He dried his face, took one pill from the orange bottle beside the sink, and wrote three words in the small notebook he kept in the drawer.

Dust. Hallway. Door.

He did not write the child's voice.

Some things did not need ink to remain.

When he returned to bed, Maggie was awake.

She lay on her side facing him, one hand tucked under her cheek. The phone on her nightstand glowed faintly before she turned it over.

"Bad one?" she asked.

"Short one."

That was not an answer, but it was one of the lies married people accepted because the fuller truth had sharp edges and everyone had work in the morning.

"You okay?"

"I'm here."

She watched him for a moment. "You want me to make coffee early?"

"No. Sleep."

"You sure?"

"Yeah."

Jack got back into bed carefully, shoulder first, then hip, then breath. Maggie did not touch him. Once, years ago, she would have. A hand on his back. Two fingers at his wrist. Not pity. Coordinates.

Now she rolled toward her phone again, thumb moving under the blanket where she thought he could not see the light.

Jack saw it.

He said nothing.

By 6:30 the house had forgiven the night.

Eden came downstairs wearing a hoodie with the name of a band she did not listen to and carrying three notebooks she had not opened. Kelly followed with wet hair, one sock, and the moral certainty that breakfast should include chocolate chips because "school is basically unpaid labor."

"School is taxpayer-funded labor," Jack said, sliding pancakes onto a plate.

"That sounds worse."

"It is. That's why we add blueberries."

Kelly peered at the stove. "Those are chocolate chips."

"Democracy requires compromise."

Eden dropped into a chair and stole one from the stack with her fingers. "Your campaign slogan should be 'Democracy requires compromise and chocolate.'"

"Too long for a yard sign."

"Not if the font is aggressive."

Jack pointed the spatula at her. "Never trust aggressive fonts. That's how nations fall."

Kelly laughed with her mouth full, and Eden tried not to, which meant she did. For a few minutes the kitchen was what Jack had fought every ghost to keep: two daughters at the table, syrup on the counter, the dog nosing at a backpack, the morning news muted on the little screen near the fridge, his name in the campaign ticker at the bottom from yesterday's town-hall replay.

MERCER PUSHES CIVIL LIBERTIES PLATFORM IN SENATE BID.

He still had not gotten used to seeing his name under a chyron. It made him feel like someone had printed a file label and stuck it to his forehead.

"Dad," Eden said, nodding toward the screen, "someone in the comments called you a constitutional goblin."

"That is the nicest thing anyone has said all week."

"I liked 'bald firewall guy,'" Kelly said.

"Also strong."

Eden scrolled her phone. "There are people mad you said due process applies to people they hate."

"That tends to happen."

"Are you worried?"

Jack flipped the last pancake. "About people misunderstanding due process? Constantly. About comments? No."

Kelly studied him. She was younger, but she saw more. Both girls did. They had grown up around briefings, court documents, campaign drafts, veteran appointments, medication bottles, laptop builds, and nights when their father had to sit alone on the porch until his breathing belonged to the present again.

They were not fragile about him.

They knew the difference between wounded and unsafe.

That would matter later, although Jack did not know it yet.

Later, adults with forms would speak around them. Reporters would write around them. Social media would decide around them. People who had never watched Jack wake from a nightmare and choose gentleness anyway would explain him to one another in confident little paragraphs.

But that morning, his daughters knew exactly who was in the kitchen.

Their father. Burned pancakes. Bad jokes. One good arm doing too much because asking for help still felt like losing twice.

Maggie entered at 6:48, dressed for work, hair perfect, phone already in hand.

"Morning," she said.

The girls answered. Jack slid a plate toward her.

"I have an early client call," she said.

"Five minutes," he said. "Sit."

It came out softer than the words looked. A request pretending to be a household order. Maggie's eyes flicked to the girls, then back to him.

"I can't."

"Okay."

He wrapped the pancake in foil because that was easier than asking why she had smiled at her phone at 3:17 in the morning.

The girls missed the look. Or pretended to. Children were merciful that way until adults forced them to stop.

After school drop-off, Jack drove to the county office with campaign calls stacked between client messages and a dull electric ache running from his shoulder into his neck. His phone kept lighting up in the cupholder.

Volunteer schedule.

Podcast request.

Clearance renewal reminder.

Recruiter asking whether he could support a six-month architecture engagement.

A text from Maggie.

Running late tonight. Don't wait up.

He did not answer right away.

At a red light, a second message appeared from an unknown number.

Same restaurant? 8 works. Missed you.

It appeared in the notification stack beneath Maggie's name because she had synced the family tablet to the wrong account again. Or because the universe had a cruel talent for filing things where they could not be unseen.

The light turned green.

A horn tapped behind him.

Jack drove.

He did not pull over. He did not call. He did not throw the phone. He did not become the kind of man strangers would later invent from the safest distance available.

He drove to work, parked, and sat for two full minutes with both hands on the wheel until the ache in his shoulder became something he could sort from the ache in his chest.

Then he took a screenshot.

Not because he wanted war.

Because records mattered.

That was the first thing Claire would later use against him.

He had not known the bullet was already in the room.

He had only heard the casing touch the floor.
