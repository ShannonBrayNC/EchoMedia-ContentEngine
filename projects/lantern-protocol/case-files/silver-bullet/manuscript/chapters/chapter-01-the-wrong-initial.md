# Chapter 1: The Wrong Initial

## Canon Sources

- Parent epic: #159
- Sprint: #160
- Addendum: #166

## Purpose

Fire the first dread signal: the wrong initial is no longer a harmless records error. It is attached to a pre-incident risk pathway that should not exist yet.

## POV

Jack Mercer

## Chapter Promise

Jack discovers that someone has touched his paperwork before anything has officially happened, and the system appears to be preparing to believe a story he has not yet heard.

## Manuscript

Jack Mercer noticed the wrong initial before the coffee finished brewing.

That was the thing Maggie used to tease him about, back when teasing was easier: he could miss an insult for three days, forget where he left his reading glasses, and stand in the pantry wondering why he had opened the door, but put one bad letter on a government form and something private would ring behind his eyes.

The form sat on his kitchen counter beneath Kelly's permission slip, the grocery list, and a chipped ceramic mug from Fort Leonard Wood. It was a county access renewal, routine enough to be boring. Jack handled records for people who hated records. His own access renewal came every June, printed on cheap white paper by a machine that always streaked the left margin blue.

He had signed it yesterday.

Somebody had initialed page three this morning.

J.M.

Jack stared at the letters while the coffeemaker hissed behind him. He did not use J.M. Not on county forms. Not on evidence receipts. Not on school paperwork. Not even on birthday cards if he could help it.

His mark was J.K.M.

Kelly came into the kitchen with one sneaker untied and a sweatshirt half-zipped over her school uniform. At thirteen, she had mastered the art of looking both asleep and personally offended by daylight.

"You are doing the face," she said.

Jack looked up. "What face?"

"The bad paperwork face."

"There are several bad paperwork faces."

"The one where you look like you're trying not to arrest a printer."

He almost smiled. It reached his mouth but stopped there, blocked by the letters. He slid the access form under the grocery list. "Tie your shoe."

"That was not denial."

"It was parenting."

Kelly dropped into the chair opposite him and tugged at the laces. Her hair fell across her cheek, dark and stubborn like Maggie's. The resemblance hit him in small ambushes. A tilt of the head. A refusal to rush a sentence. The way both of them could make silence feel like cross-examination.

"Maggie picking you up after practice?" he asked.

"If the client meeting doesn't eat her soul."

"Text me when you know."

"You say that every day."

"And every day remains undefeated."

She rolled her eyes, but she was smiling when she did it. Ordinary things helped: cereal dust on the counter, the soft thump of the newspaper against the porch, the neighbor's dog barking twice and giving up as if even it knew morning was too early for commitment.

Jack poured coffee and watched the steam rise. For a second he let himself stand inside the plainness of the room. His kitchen. His daughter. The day beginning without asking permission.

Then the county phone on the counter buzzed.

Access renewal received. Review pending.

He checked the sender twice. Internal system. Time stamped 6:14 a.m.

Jack had not submitted the form.

Kelly slung her backpack over one shoulder. "Dad?"

"Yeah."

"You okay?"

He put the phone facedown, too quickly. He saw her see it.

"Work thing," he said.

"Bad paperwork thing."

"Maybe."

At the door she hesitated, a hand on the knob. "You coming to the assembly this afternoon?"

"Wouldn't miss it."

"You missed the fall one."

"I was in Richmond helping a man convince three offices he was not dead."

"That's a good excuse."

"Top five."

She smiled again, smaller this time, and left. Jack listened to her steps on the porch, then the bus brakes two houses down. Only when the bus sighed away did he pull the form back out.

The wrong initial looked harmless. A little mistake. A letter missing from a life full of them.

Jack took a photo of page three. Then he took another. Then he opened the county portal and checked the submission history.

Submitted by: J. Mercer.

6:13 a.m.

From terminal: C-RISK-04.

Jack stood very still.

C-RISK-04 was not in his office. It was not in records. It was not even in the county building where he worked.

It belonged to the risk review suite two floors above the family safety annex, a room he had entered exactly once, three years ago, to repair a scanner no one admitted breaking.

He remembered the room because it had no windows.

The family safety annex had been added after a bond referendum no one understood but everyone supported because the words safety and family made opposition sound indecent. The suite upstairs had badge access, a copier that jammed on legal paper, and a laminated sign over the shred bin: PROTECTIVE MATERIALS ONLY.

Jack had asked what that meant.

The analyst waiting beside the scanner had smiled as if he were old-fashioned. "Records with living consequences."

All records had living consequences. Jack had not said it then because he still worked well with people who liked slogans. He had fixed the scanner, signed the service slip J.K.M., and gone back downstairs to the records office, where the consequences at least had file numbers.

Now C-RISK-04 sat in his submission history like a footprint in wet cement.

He opened the junk drawer and found the old habit kit: black pen, sticky notes, a ruler, the small flashlight Kelly had given him for Father's Day because "you inspect darkness recreationally." He placed the access renewal on the counter, squared the page against the tile grout, and photographed the signature line under full light. Then he checked the fibers around the initials. Not because he expected to solve anything with a phone camera. Because procedure kept fear from choosing the next move.

The paper had been folded once after he left it on the counter.

He had not folded it.

He looked toward the front door. Locked. Chain on. Deadbolt turned. The house made its ordinary morning noises: pipes ticking, refrigerator humming, the small settling pops that had terrified Kelly when she was six and convinced a man lived in the walls. Jack had spent two nights interviewing the vents until she laughed hard enough to sleep.

He went room by room anyway.

Kitchen. Living room. Half bath. Laundry. Back door. Windows. Nothing missing. Nothing moved in the way normal people meant moved.

But the access form had been touched, and the form had gone through a terminal in a room with no windows.

On the refrigerator, under a magnet from the county fair, Kelly's permission slip waited for his signature. He had signed that too. J.K.M. Thick black letters. A mark with a middle that belonged to him.

He photographed it, then hated the instinct. His daughter's permission slip did not need to become comparison evidence before breakfast.

But he left the photo on his phone.

The coffeemaker clicked off.

In the quiet afterward, the wrong initial seemed to darken on the page.

Jack opened the portal's attachment panel. He expected the renewal packet. He expected nothing else.

There was another file.

It had no thumbnail. Only a title and a timestamp.

PRE-INCIDENT DOMESTIC RISK REFERRAL - HOLD FOR REVIEW.

The file had been attached at 6:12 a.m., one minute before the forged submission.

Jack tried to open it. The portal denied him.

Restricted protective material.

He tried again. The denial repeated itself with the patience of a machine that knew patience was power.

Restricted protective material.

A car slowed outside the house.

Jack looked up.

Through the kitchen window, across the pale strip of morning lawn, he saw a gray sedan at the curb. Not parked. Paused. The driver's window was dark with reflection. He could not see the face inside.

Then the sedan rolled on.

Jack waited until it disappeared around the bend. Only then did he breathe.

His county phone buzzed again.

Review pending.

Below it, a second message appeared.

Do not attempt to contact protected party regarding this referral.

Protected party.

Jack read the words three times.

He did not know who they meant.

He knew exactly who they meant.

The report had not accused him of violence.

It had prepared the room to believe it.
