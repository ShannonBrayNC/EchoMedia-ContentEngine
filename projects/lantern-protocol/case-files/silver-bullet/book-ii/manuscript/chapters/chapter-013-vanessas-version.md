# Chapter 013: Vanessa's Version

Vanessa did not tell the story to Jack.

That was how Maggie knew it mattered.

The house was quiet in the strange way houses become quiet after too many revelations. Not peaceful. Exhausted. Rain drifted against the windows. The old refrigerator hummed from the kitchen. Somewhere downstairs Jack was arguing with a cache export and losing with dignity.

Maggie found Vanessa in the guest room.

The photograph was on the bed again.

Elias Voss.

The crossed-out lantern.

The sentence about The Null.

Vanessa sat beside it with her knees drawn up and both hands wrapped around a mug that had long since gone cold.

Maggie knocked lightly on the open door frame.

"You keep looking at that picture like it owes you money."

Vanessa laughed.

A small laugh.

A surprised laugh.

The kind people make when grief forgets itself for a second.

"Maybe it does."

Maggie sat in the chair beside the window.

Neither woman spoke immediately.

Silence was cheaper than trust.

Trust had to be earned.

Eventually Vanessa looked down at the photograph.

"She hated being photographed."

Maggie waited.

"Celia?"

Vanessa nodded.

"Not because she thought she looked bad. She said photographs let strangers decide they understood a person."

The sentence sounded familiar.

Not the words.

The shape.

People who had spent too long being interpreted eventually learned to fear summaries.

"You were close?" Maggie asked.

Vanessa smiled.

This time it hurt.

"She was my sister. We fought constantly."

"That sounds closer than most people manage."

Vanessa stared out the rain-streaked window.

"She translated everything."

"Languages?"

"People."

That answer landed.

Celia had been the kind of person who sat in uncomfortable rooms and somehow convinced everyone they had meant something better than what they actually said.

Family gatherings.

Court offices.

Relief centers.

Church basements.

Hospital waiting rooms.

She translated language.

Then translated motive.

Then translated fear.

And somehow made strangers feel less alone.

"That sounds exhausting," Maggie said.

"It was."

Vanessa smiled again.

"She was good at it anyway."

The room settled around the memory.

For the first time since arriving at Jack's house, Vanessa stopped behaving like a witness statement wearing expensive earrings.

She became a sister.

"When did she start getting worried?" Maggie asked.

Vanessa's expression changed.

The warmth retreated.

Not gone.

Protected.

"Before she disappeared."

Maggie remained silent.

Vanessa appreciated that.

People always rushed grief toward conclusions.

The dead deserved patience.

The missing deserved more.

"She started saying strange things."

"Such as?"

"She kept asking who owned a decision after enough people touched it."

Maggie blinked.

"That's not a normal question."

"No."

"What did she mean?"

Vanessa looked down at the mug.

"She said systems were learning how to use people after refusal."

The phrase hung between them.

Not as doctrine.

As memory.

"Useful after refusal," Maggie said quietly.

Vanessa nodded.

"At first I thought she was talking about work. Then she started documenting things."

"What things?"

"Approvals. Retentions. Policy exceptions. Places where a person's answer changed but the system kept moving as if it hadn't."

Maggie felt a chill.

Not because the idea was new.

Because Celia had noticed it before Voss.

Or at least before Voss wrote it down.

Downstairs, a floorboard creaked.

Jack.

Vanessa glanced toward the door.

"He is listening, isn't he?"

"No."

"You sound sure."

"Because he's grown. He would have listened six months ago."

Vanessa laughed.

A real one.

The sound surprised both of them.

Outside, thunder rolled somewhere beyond the trees.

Inside, the room became easier to breathe in.

Vanessa reached beneath the bed.

Maggie's posture changed instantly.

Not fear.

Readiness.

Vanessa pulled out a thin folder.

Black.

Unmarked.

The kind of object that knew exactly what it was doing in a thriller novel.

"You withheld something," Maggie said.

"Yes."

"Why?"

Vanessa looked embarrassed.

That surprised Maggie more than any conspiracy ever could.

"Because if Jack turned out to be wrong, I wanted one thing nobody could contaminate."

The answer was honest enough to hurt.

Maggie accepted it.

Not approved.

Accepted.

Vanessa opened the folder.

Inside was a single page.

Not a report.

Not a contract.

A note.

Handwritten.

Voss's handwriting matched the labels in the storage-unit index.

Three lines.

Nothing more.

```text
If Celia contacts you, do not use email.

If Celia disappears, preserve sequence.

If Black Lantern returns, run.
```

The room became very small.

Maggie read the note again.

Then a third time.

"You never showed this to Jack?"

"No."

"Why?"

Vanessa looked back toward the photograph.

"Because I was afraid he'd believe it."

Maggie understood immediately.

Some warnings were dangerous whether they were true or false.

Especially the true ones.

Downstairs, Jack called up from the kitchen.

"Maggie?"

His voice sounded normal.

Which meant trouble had found him again.

Maggie stood.

Vanessa closed the folder.

The note disappeared.

The words did not.

Black Lantern.

For the first time, the phrase felt less like a mystery.

And more like a threat.
