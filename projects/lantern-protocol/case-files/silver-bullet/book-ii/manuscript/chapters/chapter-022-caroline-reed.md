# Chapter 022: Caroline Reed

Caroline Reed answered on the third attempt.

That bothered Jack.

Not because she answered.

Because she had ignored the first two.

People who wanted to be found usually answered the first call. People who never wanted to be found answered none of them. The third-call people were different.

The third-call people were deciding.

The number had come from an old Black Lantern recovery contact record.

Revoked access. Twenty-one months inactive. No current organizational affiliation. No public professional profile. No recent conference appearances. No board memberships. No LinkedIn activity.

Nothing.

Caroline Reed had disappeared with enough precision to feel intentional.

The call connected.

No video. No introduction. No greeting.

Only one question.

"Who gave you my number?"

Jack looked at Maggie.

Maggie made a small motion with her hand.

Truth.

Just truth.

"We found your name in a Voss note."

Silence.

Long silence.

The kind that changed shape.

Not surprise.

Recognition.

When Caroline finally spoke again, her voice sounded older than Jack expected. Not elderly. Weathered. The voice of someone who had spent years arguing with institutions and losing often enough to become careful.

"Elias should have burned those notebooks."

Vanessa sat upright immediately.

"You knew him."

Another pause.

Then:

"Unfortunately."

Jack almost smiled.

Anyone who described knowing Elias Voss as unfortunate probably knew him extremely well.

Christina displayed a private transcription window.

Caroline noticed immediately.

"Is this being recorded?"

Jack answered carefully.

"Transcribed for investigative notes."

Another silence.

Then:

"Good."

That surprised everyone.

Caroline exhaled slowly.

"I assume you've found Black Lantern."

The room went still.

Not because of what she said.

Because of how casually she said it.

Not what is Black Lantern? Not how do you know about Black Lantern?

She skipped straight to:

You found it.

Jack chose his words carefully.

"We found references."

"That's worse."

Nobody liked that answer.

Vanessa leaned forward.

"My sister is connected to it."

The line went quiet.

Again.

Caroline did not answer immediately.

When she finally did, her voice changed. Softer. Human.

"I know."

Vanessa froze.

Everyone froze.

Jack felt the room narrow.

"You know Celia?"

"I knew of her."

"How?"

Another long pause.

Then:

"Because she became one of the reasons we shut Black Lantern down."

Nobody spoke.

Not even Maggie.

Caroline continued.

"You're asking the wrong question."

Jack hated that sentence. It usually meant the right question was worse.

"What question should we ask?"

Caroline answered immediately.

"What was Black Lantern protecting?"

Not who created it. Not who funded it. Not who ran it.

What was it protecting?

Christina created a note. Maggie wrote the same question on paper.

Good.

Redundancy was a form of respect.

Caroline finally explained. Not everything. Just enough.

"Black Lantern started after a series of record failures. Missing testimony. Lost interviews. Destroyed evidence. People whose stories disappeared because systems were incompetent, overloaded, or corrupt."

Jack listened carefully.

None of that sounded evil.

It sounded familiar.

Too familiar.

"We wanted preservation," Caroline said.

She laughed once. A bitter sound.

"We always start with preservation."

Vanessa stared at the phone.

"What did it become?"

Caroline answered without hesitation.

"It became certainty."

The room became quiet.

"We stopped preserving records. We started preserving conclusions."

That sentence hit harder than any conspiracy theory could.

Because it sounded possible.

Caroline continued.

"At first Black Lantern archived testimony. Then summaries. Then interpretations. Then confidence scores."

Jack felt a chill.

He already knew where the path ended.

"The system became better at describing witnesses than listening to them."

Nobody moved. Nobody interrupted.

Caroline's voice dropped.

"M. Sutter loved that."

There it was.

The name.

Finally.

Directly.

Not a transcript. Not a maintenance log. Not an invitation.

A witness.

A living witness.

Vanessa spoke first.

"What did he do?"

Caroline laughed again. No humor. No warmth. Only memory.

"He asked the question that killed Black Lantern."

Jack leaned forward.

"What question?"

Caroline answered.

"If the witness becomes unavailable, which version of them should the system trust?"

Nobody spoke.

Because everyone understood.

Immediately.

Celia. Room 414. Template 4B. Recovery accounts. Authorization retention.

Every road led back to the same cliff.

The witness disappeared.

The system continued.

Caroline continued.

"We argued for months. Elias fought him constantly. At first."

Jack caught the wording.

"At first?"

Another pause. Longer this time. More painful.

"Because eventually Elias stopped arguing."

Vanessa frowned.

"What does that mean?"

Caroline answered quietly.

"It means he thought Sutter was right."

The room froze.

Not because anyone believed it.

Because nobody wanted to.

Caroline continued before anyone could interrupt.

"Elias didn't agree morally. He agreed technically. The system could preserve authority. The system could preserve decisions. The system could preserve evidence. But it could never preserve a witness."

Silence.

"Elias realized that first. And that's when he started building something else."

Maggie looked up slowly.

"Lantern."

The line remained quiet.

Then:

"Yes."

Nobody moved. Nobody spoke. Nobody breathed correctly.

For the first time, the divide became visible.

Not Black Lantern versus Lantern. Not good versus evil. Not Voss versus Sutter.

A disagreement.

A philosophical fracture.

A fork in the road.

Black Lantern: preserve the record.

Lantern: preserve the witness.

Caroline finally exhaled.

"You need to understand something. The Ethics Review invitation?"

"Yes," Jack said.

"It isn't about you."

The room became still.

Caroline's answer arrived softly.

Almost gently.

"It's about Vanessa."

Nobody spoke.

Not even Vanessa.

Caroline continued.

"If Sutter believes Celia left an Anchor, he'll never stop looking for it."

The call disconnected.

No goodbye. No warning. No explanation.

Only silence.

Christina created a new evidence card.

```text
CAROLINE REED INTERVIEW

Confirmed:
- Former Black Lantern reviewer
- Knew Voss
- Knew Sutter
- Black Lantern originated as preservation system
- Major disagreement centered on witness replacement

Unconfirmed:
- Celia Anchor
- Sutter objective
- Ethics Review purpose

Priority: Critical
```

Jack stared at the final line.

Not the confirmed section.

The unconfirmed section.

One phrase. Two words.

**Celia Anchor**

The room had acquired a new mystery.

And somehow it felt older than all the others.
