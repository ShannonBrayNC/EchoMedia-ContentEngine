# Chapter 025: The Review

The building looked disappointingly normal.

Jack hated that.

Monsters should at least have the courtesy to decorate.

NorthStar Administrative Center sat three blocks from a coffee shop, two blocks from a law office, and directly across from a bank whose windows reflected the morning sun with corporate enthusiasm.

Nothing about it suggested Room 414.

Nothing suggested Black Lantern.

Nothing suggested a witness-preservation system that had somehow survived the people who built it.

It looked like a place where middle managers discussed policy updates.

Which, Jack increasingly suspected, was how most dangerous things survived.

Maggie drove. Not because Jack could not. Because nobody trusted him to leave if things went sideways. Least of all Jack.

Vanessa sat in the back seat. Silent. Watching. Thinking.

The silence was not fear. Not entirely. It was preparation.

Christina remained remote. Deliberately. No active connection to NorthStar systems. No facility access. No background collection. No network probing. No cleverness.

The rules had been established before sunrise.

Observe. Record. Leave.

Nothing else.

The lobby greeted them with polite neutrality. A receptionist. A visitor badge. A tray of bottled water. Muted artwork.

Everything carefully calibrated to communicate legitimacy.

Jack signed in.

The receptionist smiled.

"Mr. Mercer."

Not a question.

A statement.

Jack hated those.

She handed him a visitor badge.

Already printed.

Already waiting.

Maggie noticed immediately.

So did Vanessa.

Nobody commented.

Yet.

The elevator ride lasted twelve seconds. Long enough to become uncomfortable. Short enough to prevent conversation.

Fourth floor.

The doors opened.

Nobody moved.

Because the hallway should not have existed.

The public directory listed three floors. The building website listed three floors. The lobby directory listed three floors.

The elevator had stopped at four.

Maggie looked at Jack. Jack looked at Maggie.

Neither needed to speak.

The fourth floor existed.

Room 414 had always been real.

The hallway stretched ahead. Quiet. Clean. Unremarkable. The architecture of institutional confidence.

A sign waited beside the nearest door.

```text
ETHICS REVIEW SUITE
```

Nothing about Room 414. Nothing about Sutter. Nothing about Black Lantern.

Just enough truth to be useful.

A woman waited inside. Late sixties. Gray hair. Dark blazer. Sharp eyes.

Caroline Reed.

She looked exactly like someone who regretted being correct for several consecutive years.

Vanessa stopped walking.

Caroline noticed.

The expression on her face changed. Not dramatically. Humanly.

"Celia looked more like your father."

Vanessa froze.

Not because of the statement.

Because Caroline said it without hesitation. Without performance. Without needing a photograph.

Memory.

Real memory.

Caroline extended a hand.

Vanessa accepted it.

No speeches. No reunion. No sentimental shortcuts.

Just proof.

The room contained six chairs.

That bothered Maggie immediately.

Three visitors. One Caroline. One unknown reviewer.

Six chairs.

Too many.

She counted them twice.

Jack noticed.

Of course he noticed.

"What?"

"Six."

"Helpful."

"It will be."

She sounded irritated, which usually meant she was right.

The door opened.

M. Sutter entered.

Nobody announced him. Nobody introduced him. Nobody needed to.

He carried himself like a man who had spent decades walking into rooms already convinced he was correct.

Not arrogant.

Worse.

Settled.

Sutter looked older than Jack expected. Early seventies, maybe. Hard to tell. Some people aged normally. Others aged into institutions.

Sutter belonged to the second category.

His eyes settled briefly on Caroline. Then Vanessa. Then Maggie. Then Jack.

Recognition.

Not surprise.

"Mr. Mercer."

The same tone as the receptionist.

Not a question.

Jack disliked him immediately.

Which meant nothing.

Useful investigations were built on evidence, not instinct.

Sutter sat.

One chair remained empty.

Maggie noticed again.

Six chairs. Five occupants. One empty.

The chair bothered her more every minute.

Sutter folded his hands.

"I appreciate your attendance."

Caroline laughed softly.

"No you don't."

For the first time, Sutter smiled.

Tiny. Genuine. Dangerous.

"No. But I appreciate the opportunity."

"To do what?" Vanessa asked.

Sutter looked at her carefully, respectfully, the way one might examine a historical document before touching it.

"To prevent another failure."

Nobody liked that answer.

Especially because he believed it.

Jack leaned forward.

"Celia?"

Sutter's expression changed. Only slightly.

Pain.

Actual pain.

Not guilt. Not performance. Pain.

"Yes."

The room became still.

Sutter looked down at the table.

"When Celia disappeared, everyone asked the wrong question. The question was never where she went. The question was what she learned."

Vanessa stared.

Caroline closed her eyes.

Maggie wrote the sentence down immediately.

Jack felt the room narrowing. Again.

Not around Celia.

Around knowledge.

The Anchor. Room 414. Black Lantern.

All roads returning.

Sutter finally looked up. Directly at Jack.

"The Anchor is not Celia."

The same sentence as the message.

Only now it sounded heavier.

Real.

"What is it?" Jack asked.

Sutter considered the question long enough to be careful, not long enough to be evasive.

Then he pointed toward the empty chair.

Nobody liked that.

Nobody.

"That," Sutter said, "is the wrong question."

The room went silent.

Sutter continued.

"The correct question is this."

His eyes moved across all of them.

Jack. Maggie. Vanessa. Caroline.

Witnesses. Participants. People.

Finally:

"Who is supposed to be sitting there?"

Nobody spoke.

Nobody moved.

The empty chair remained empty.

For now.
