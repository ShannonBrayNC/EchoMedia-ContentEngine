# Chapter 027: What Celia Found

Nobody moved Vanessa out of the chair.

That mattered.

Jack noticed it immediately.

Sutter noticed that Jack noticed.

Maggie noticed both.

The review had become less about what was being said and more about what people allowed to remain true.

Vanessa stayed seated. The chair stayed occupied. The room adjusted around both facts.

For the first time since entering NorthStar, Jack stopped thinking about the chair itself.

The chair had never been the point.

The vacancy was.

Sutter folded his hands.

"Celia never found a person."

The statement landed awkwardly.

Not because it was confusing.

Because everyone had already started building theories around the opposite.

Vanessa frowned.

"Then what did she find?"

Caroline answered before Sutter could.

"A question."

Sutter looked irritated.

"That sounds dramatic."

"It was dramatic."

The first real edge entered the room.

Not disagreement.

History.

Caroline sat forward.

"Celia noticed something none of us wanted to notice."

Jack waited. So did Maggie. Even Sutter. Especially Sutter.

Caroline continued.

"Every system failed in the same direction."

Nobody spoke.

Because the sentence felt familiar.

Room 414. Black Lantern. Template 4B. NorthStar. The hearing that wasn't there.

The same direction.

The same gravity.

"What direction?" Vanessa asked.

Caroline looked toward the transcript screen. Toward the evidence cards. Toward the accumulated weight of the investigation.

"When uncertainty became expensive, the systems manufactured certainty."

Silence.

Jack felt something shift.

Not revelation.

Recognition.

The difference mattered.

Manufactured certainty.

Attendance lists. Recovery accounts. Authorization retention. Continuity language.

Not preserving truth. Preserving stability.

Sutter sighed.

"That isn't entirely fair."

Caroline laughed.

"There it is."

The room tightened.

"What?" Jack asked.

Caroline pointed toward Sutter.

"That's the argument."

Nobody interrupted.

Because finally they were reaching the fracture.

The real one.

Not Black Lantern. Not Lantern. Not technology.

Philosophy.

Sutter leaned back.

"Organizations cannot function without continuity."

Caroline nodded.

True.

"People cannot function without truth."

Also true.

The room became uncomfortable.

Because everybody was correct.

And everybody knew it.

Vanessa looked from one to the other.

"So what did Celia actually find?"

Caroline answered quietly.

"Proof."

Nobody liked that answer.

Not because proof was bad.

Because proof always created responsibility.

"What proof?" Maggie asked.

Caroline looked toward Sutter. Sutter looked toward the window.

Neither seemed eager to continue.

That told Jack more than the answer itself.

Finally Sutter spoke.

"Celia discovered the continuity review model."

The phrase sat in the room.

Nobody understood it immediately.

Good.

Important things usually arrived before explanation.

Christina displayed a note. No analysis. Only transcription.

```text
CONTINUITY REVIEW MODEL
```

Sutter continued.

"Room 414 wasn't a room."

Nobody spoke.

Because they had physically visited it.

"It was a classification. Review Level 414. The room existed later. The classification existed first."

Jack felt the world rearrange itself.

Not completely.

Enough.

Room 414 was not originally a location.

It was a process. A category. A way of thinking.

Caroline looked miserable.

Which made Jack trust her more.

She hated where the story was going.

That usually meant it was true.

Vanessa stared.

"What kind of classification?"

Nobody answered immediately.

Then Sutter did.

"The situations where preserving institutional continuity was considered more important than preserving participant certainty."

The room went cold.

Not supernatural cold.

Administrative cold.

The kind that arrived when a sentence explained too much.

Jack immediately understood why Celia cared. Why Voss cared. Why Caroline left. Why Black Lantern failed.

Because somebody had formalized it.

Uncertainty could be sacrificed if continuity benefited.

Vanessa stood fast. Angry.

"That's insane."

"No," Sutter said.

The answer came too quickly. Too honestly.

"No. It's common."

Nobody liked him after that.

Not even Caroline.

Especially not Caroline.

Because everyone knew he was not lying.

Sutter looked around the room.

Hospitals. Universities. Corporations. Governments. Charities.

Every institution eventually faced the same temptation.

Protect the process.

Protect the people.

Choose.

Celia had discovered the choice.

More importantly, she had documented it.

The room became very still.

Jack looked at Sutter.

"That's what she found."

Sutter nodded.

"Yes."

Not a person. Not an object. Not a conspiracy.

A framework. A model. A decision tree.

A machine for manufacturing certainty.

Caroline finally spoke.

"And Elias realized something worse. The model didn't need corruption. It produced the same outcome even when everyone involved meant well."

That landed harder than anything else.

Because corruption could be removed. Bad people could be replaced. Fraud could be prosecuted.

But good people following a broken model?

That was different.

That was harder.

That was systemic.

Vanessa sat down again. Not in the Anchor chair. In her original chair.

The movement mattered.

She was not trying to become the answer anymore.

She was listening. Learning. Growing.

Exactly what Celia had done.

Christina created a new evidence card.

```text
CELIA DISCOVERY

Confirmed:
Continuity Review Model

Characteristics:
- certainty generation
- continuity prioritization
- participant uncertainty reduction

Risk:
manufactured certainty

Status: Critical
```

Jack stared at the card. Then at Sutter. Then at Caroline.

For the first time, the mystery felt less like a puzzle.

And more like a warning.

Because if Celia had discovered the model, someone had decided she could not keep talking about it.
