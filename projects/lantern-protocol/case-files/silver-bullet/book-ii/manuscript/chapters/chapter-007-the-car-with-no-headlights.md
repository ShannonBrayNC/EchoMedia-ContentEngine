# Chapter 007: The Car with No Headlights

The car passed twice.

Jack did not run outside. That was progress. His body wanted old choreography: open door, demand answer, become footage. He stayed behind the curtain while Christina pulled porch camera frames into Echo and Maggie called the nonemergency line with a voice so calm it could have cut glass.

Vanessa stood away from the windows.

"You have done this before," Jack said.

"Been watched? Yes. Been believed about it? Less often."

The car was a dark sedan with a damaged left taillight and temporary tags too blurred by rain for a clean read. Christina marked the vehicle as an event, not proof.

Jack liked that less when the thing watched was his own house.

Maggie ended the call. "They'll increase patrol if a unit is available."

Everyone in the room understood the sentence as weather, not protection.

Echo's map now held four live threads: the reporter, Lena, Vanessa, and Voss. The old danger would have been connecting them too quickly. The new danger was refusing connection long enough for the pattern to hide inside their caution.

"We need a rule," Maggie said.

"We have rules," Jack answered.

"No. We have principles. We need an operating rule for when The Null touches a living person."

That phrasing entered the room and stayed.

A living person.

Celia might be alive. Lena was alive. Marcus was alive. Vanessa was alive and standing too close to Jack's wreckage with a face that made him want to offer answers before earning them. Maggie was alive and would pay for his mistakes because proximity had always been one of the cruelest forms of evidence.

Christina proposed the rule in three lines.

```text
NULL-CONTACT RULE
1. Preserve identity separately from role language.
2. Require living permission before public reconstruction.
3. Treat use-without-presence as active harm.
```

Vanessa read it twice.

"That would have saved Celia from being converted into a citation."

"Maybe," Jack said.

She looked at him. "You do not allow comfort to sit very long."

"Comfort gets subpoenaed."

Maggie almost smiled. Almost.

The reporter posted the follow-up at 9:02 p.m. It was worse than the first because it had learned from Jack's silence. The article framed Lantern as a precedent problem. It asked whether private AI systems should gather sensitive records from people in active legal, medical, or custody disputes. It quoted an unnamed ethics researcher, a family safety advocate, and one former law-enforcement analyst who warned that men accused of abuse often recast themselves as investigators.

It did not mention Lena's refusal.

It did not mention the path her name had taken.

It did not mention NorthStar.

It did mention Vanessa.

Not by full name.

Not yet.

**A second woman linked to Mercer has surfaced with claims involving a missing translator and the late Dr. Elias Voss, a conspiracy figure in several online communities.**

Vanessa's expression emptied.

"I came here four hours ago."

Jack felt the old courtroom nausea. The record had outrun the event again.

Christina displayed the article metadata.

```text
Draft timestamp: 14:11
Publication timestamp: 21:02
Claim includes event that occurred after draft timestamp
Inference: article updated from live source after Vanessa arrival
```

Maggie turned toward the dark window.

The car was back.

This time it stopped across the street.

Vanessa reached into her coat and removed the pocket recorder she had said stayed in storage. She set it on the table.

"Voss left one message with my sister's name in it," she said. "I lied because I wanted to see whether your system punished me for protecting it."

Jack looked at Maggie.

Maggie looked at the recorder.

Outside, the sedan's engine idled like a throat clearing before testimony.
