# Chapter 011: The Fourteen-Minute Window

The ticket existed for fourteen minutes.

Jack knew the number because Christina had replayed it twenty-three times.

Fourteen minutes.

Eight hundred and forty seconds.

Not enough time to drive across town.

Not enough time to read a novel chapter.

Apparently enough time to alter the trajectory of several lives.

The replay ran silently on the wall display while rain tapped against the kitchen windows. The interface looked harmless. Pale gray background. Blue navigation tabs. A search field no one would remember. The kind of software built by people who believed usefulness was the highest virtue.

The horror was not the screen.

The horror was the audience.

Christina highlighted the access log.

```text
PUBLIC EXPOSURE WINDOW
Duration: 14 minutes, 03 seconds

Observed access:
1. Legislative network
2. Contractor VPN
3. Archive mirror crawler

Additional anonymous traffic detected.
```

Vanessa stood behind Jack.

"That's it?"

Jack looked over his shoulder.

"What were you expecting?"

She folded her arms.

"I don't know. Something dramatic."

"The dramatic parts happen later."

Maggie slid a legal pad across the table.

"The dramatic parts happen because people ignore boring evidence."

Vanessa hated when Maggie was right.

Jack secretly hated it too.

Christina advanced the replay.

The help-desk ticket appeared exactly as it had existed during the exposure.

**Historical Interpreter Authorization Mismatch**

No flashing warnings.

No red banners.

No classified markings.

Just administrative confusion.

The sort of thing nobody notices until a disaster grows roots inside it.

The ticket referenced Celia Vale only once.

Every other entry referred to her as:

**Language Support Resource.**

**Translator.**

**Community Liaison.**

**Vendor Asset.**

**Volunteer Authority.**

Jack felt a familiar coldness.

The person vanished.

The function remained.

The Null Pattern again.

Not absence.

Use without presence.

Vanessa noticed his expression.

"You see it."

He nodded.

"She's still being used."

The room became quiet.

Not because the statement was surprising.

Because everyone knew it was true.

Christina zoomed into the access logs.

Three entries separated themselves from the rest.

Legislative network.

Contractor VPN.

Archive mirror.

The labels meant nothing.

Yet.

Maggie pointed to the legislative entry.

"Why would a legislative office look at this?"

"No idea."

"Do we know it was a person?"

"We know someone authenticated."

Vanessa stared at the timestamp.

"Maybe they were looking for her."

"Or looking for whoever was looking for her."

Nobody liked that possibility.

Christina opened another panel.

```text
ANOMALY DETECTED

Current ticket state:
No attachments

Historic request:
Attachment download event observed
```

Jack leaned forward.

"Replay."

The screen rolled backward.

Again.

Again.

Again.

On the sixth pass, he saw it.

A tiny icon.

Gone almost immediately.

Attachment.

Not present now.

Present then.

Someone had downloaded something.

Maggie wrote the observation down.

"Can we recover it?"

"No."

Vanessa exhaled sharply.

"Then we're stuck."

"No," Jack said.

"We're behind."

There was a difference.

Christina highlighted the download event.

A filename surfaced from cached metadata.

For half a second nobody spoke.

The letters seemed ordinary.

Almost boring.

**CELIA_AUTH_HISTORY.xlsx**

Vanessa sat down.

Slowly.

The room tilted around her.

Not because the file existed.

Because somebody thought it was important enough to remove.

Jack looked at the name.

Authorization history.

A life reduced to permissions.

A woman transformed into workflow.

Somewhere, buried inside that spreadsheet, was a sequence of decisions somebody no longer wanted visible.

Christina displayed one final note.

```text
Historic file detected.

Current file unavailable.

Recovery probability:
Unknown.
```

Unknown.

Jack preferred unknown to impossible.

Unknown meant there was still a road.

The problem was finding it before someone else reached the end first.

Outside, thunder rolled across the trees.

Inside, the replay ended.

Fourteen minutes.

Eight hundred and forty seconds.

And somewhere in those fourteen minutes, someone had carried a missing piece of Celia Vale out into the world.
