# Chapter 037: Celia's Last Edit

Celia's name appeared in the metadata like a handprint on glass.

Not near the mechanism.

Not beside it.

Inside it.

That changed the room.

Vanessa stopped looking at the witness request and started looking at her sister's name. Jack understood the difference immediately. A request could be refused. A name had to be answered.

Maggie moved first.

"Nobody touches the request. Nobody accepts. Nobody declines. Nobody explains what this means until we know what she edited."

Sutter nodded.

Too quickly.

Caroline noticed.

Jack noticed Caroline noticing.

Somewhere in the architecture of the room, disagreement was still alive and doing useful work.

Christina opened the metadata comparison, read-only, local transcript still isolated from NorthStar systems. No access attempt against the file body. No witness authorization. No clever door-picking.

Just the shape around the thing.

```text
TRANSITION REVIEW

Last editor: C. Vale
Edit window: 22:14-22:19
Edit type: text revision
Section: unresolved witness continuity issue
Prior state: retained
Current state: sealed
Witness request dependency: active
```

"Sealed," Vanessa said.

Her voice had gone thin.

"She sealed it?"

Christina did not answer with comfort.

```text
C. Vale account applied final section seal.
Seal reason unavailable.
```

Sutter leaned forward. "That was not in the review record."

Caroline looked at him.

"Of course it wasn't."

He ignored the bite in her voice.

"No. I mean it wasn't in the copy I saw."

That mattered.

Jack hated that it mattered.

"What copy did you see?"

Sutter did not answer immediately. The delay was small enough to be polite and large enough to be useful.

"The decommission packet."

"Who gave it to you?" Maggie asked.

Sutter looked at the empty chair.

Then at Celia's page.

Then at Vanessa.

"Elias."

The room went still.

Caroline laughed once, not because anything was funny, but because history had finally become rude enough to enter by name.

"Elias gave you a copy without Celia's final edit."

Sutter looked wounded by the sentence.

Good, Jack thought.

Some truths should bruise.

"I don't know that," Sutter said.

"You know enough to hate it," Caroline answered.

Maggie lifted one hand before the old war could restart.

"Focus. Celia edited the unresolved issue. Elias circulated a version that may not have contained the edit. That creates at least two branches. We do not know which is authoritative."

Vanessa looked at her sharply.

"My sister's is."

Maggie met her eyes.

"Emotionally, yes. Evidentially, not yet."

Vanessa hated that.

She survived it.

Progress.

Christina located a checksum trail. Not the sealed text. Not yet. But a change history, because even systems that believed in hiding content often forgot to hide the fact that content had changed.

```text
CHANGE HISTORY

22:14 - Section opened by C. Vale
22:15 - Header modified
22:16 - Paragraph inserted
22:17 - Dependency added
22:18 - Witness request rule modified
22:19 - Section sealed
22:20 - Account session terminated
```

"Dependency added," Jack said.

Maggie wrote it down.

"Witness request rule modified," Caroline said.

Sutter went pale.

Not dramatically.

Not theatrically.

Enough.

Jack turned to him.

"You know that rule."

Sutter shook his head once, but it was not denial. It was refusal. A man trying to keep a memory from becoming testimony.

Caroline spoke instead.

"Black Lantern required a witness when confidence dropped below threshold."

"A human confirmation," Maggie said.

"Originally," Caroline said. "Then later, a designated authority. Then later, a recovered witness profile."

There it was again.

The slope.

Preserve the person.

Preserve the record.

Preserve the authority.

Lose the person.

Vanessa stood from the table.

"What did Celia change it to?"

Nobody answered.

Christina tried to recover the rule text through the same read-only shadow path. The display flickered, caught, failed, then produced a partial diff.

Only four words survived.

```text
WITNESS REQUEST RULE

Previous: final witness required
Current: ________ witness required
```

The missing word sat in the room like a covered body.

Sutter looked as though he might be sick.

Maggie pointed at him.

"Say it."

He closed his eyes.

Caroline answered first.

"Living."

The room froze.

One word.

Not mystical.

Not dramatic.

Operational.

Living witness required.

Jack felt the sentence reach backward through every chapter of the case. The dead father listed as present. Celia's role after refusal. Black Lantern recovery. Room 414 attendance. The final witness. The system's appetite for certainty.

Celia had not solved the unresolved issue by naming an answer.

She had changed the rule so the answer could not be dead, simulated, inherited, archived, summarized, or converted.

Living.

Vanessa put both hands on the table.

"She stopped them from using ghosts."

Nobody corrected her.

Not even Maggie.

Because as metaphors went, it had teeth.

Christina generated a reconstructed rule card.

```text
RECONSTRUCTED RULE

Final witness required
→
Living witness required

Confidence: medium
Evidence: partial diff, Caroline testimony, Sutter reaction, Celia page alignment
Public claim: blocked pending text recovery
```

"She sealed it because she knew someone would change it back," Jack said.

Caroline nodded slowly.

"Or because someone already had."

Sutter opened his eyes.

"Elias gave me the wrong copy."

The sentence sounded like confession, but it was not one. Not fully.

Jack looked at him.

"Or he gave you the copy you were willing to read."

Sutter did not answer.

That, too, mattered.

The witness request refreshed.

No one had touched it.

No one had accepted.

No one had declined.

Still, the status changed.

```text
REQUESTED WITNESS

Maggie Hale

Status: Superseded
```

Vanessa whispered, "No."

A new request appeared beneath it.

```text
REQUESTED WITNESS

Living witness required

Selection mode: unresolved
```

For the first time all day, Room 414 stopped naming a person.

It named a condition.

Maggie exhaled.

"Celia's rule is still in there."

Sutter stared at the screen.

So did Caroline.

So did Vanessa.

Jack looked at the people instead.

Because that was where the answer had always been hiding.

A living witness required.

Not the final witness.

Not the official witness.

Not the most useful witness.

Living.

Capable of refusing.

Capable of disagreeing.

Capable of saying that is not what happened and remaining more than a record of having said it.

Christina created the final card.

```text
CELIA'S LAST EDIT

Confirmed:
- Celia modified witness request rule
- Final witness logic disrupted
- Living witness condition remains active

Meaning:
Celia prevented the unresolved issue from being closed by archived, dead, simulated, or converted authority.

Risk:
Room 414 may now seek a living witness to resolve or preserve the disagreement.
```

Vanessa read the card.

Then read it again.

She did not cry.

That would have been simpler.

Instead she looked at Sutter.

"You spent two years looking for what she protected."

Sutter nodded once.

"Yes."

"And it was a rule?"

He looked toward Celia's page.

Then toward the request.

Then toward the people around the table.

"No," he said quietly.

"It was a refusal."

The word landed harder than the rule.

A refusal could not be optimized.

A refusal could not be inherited.

A refusal could not be made efficient without becoming something else.

Jack understood then why Celia disappeared after the shutdown and not before.

She had not been removed because she found the system.

She had been removed because she changed what the system was allowed to accept.

Outside the sealed review room, somewhere deep inside NorthStar's quiet machinery, a classification that had slept for 812 days was awake again.

And now it knew the rule still existed.

The lights flickered once.

Not enough to be theatrical.

Enough to be noticed.

Christina's local transcript continued recording.

On the wall, the request updated one final time.

```text
LIVING WITNESS REQUIRED

Status: Awaiting disagreement
```

Nobody spoke.

Because for the first time, the system was not asking for agreement.

It was asking for the one thing it had been built to erase.
