# Chapter 032: Reactivation

The alert arrived seventeen seconds after the Anchor discussion ended.

Jack knew the exact number because Christina recorded it.

Maggie knew the exact number because she asked.

Sutter knew the exact number because his face changed before anyone else had finished reading it.

That bothered Jack more than the alert itself.

```text
ROOM 414 CLASSIFICATION

Status: Reactivated
Source: Unknown
Timestamp: 14:17:33
Priority: Critical
```

Nobody spoke.

The room had spent the last three hours discussing philosophy. Witnesses. Continuity. Disagreement. The Anchor.

The kinds of ideas people wrote books about after surviving disasters.

Then reality had kicked the door open.

Again.

Sutter stood first.

Not dramatically.

Instinctively.

Caroline noticed.

Interesting.

Very interesting.

Jack noticed too.

"Who did that?"

Nobody answered.

That was answer enough.

Because if Sutter had done it, he would have said so. If Caroline had done it, she would have said so. If NorthStar had officially authorized it, someone would have known.

Instead, silence.

Christina expanded the event.

```text
CLASSIFICATION HISTORY

Room 414
Status: Inactive
Inactive duration: 812 days
Prior authorization: Revoked
Reactivation source: Unknown
Authorizing identity: Unavailable
```

Vanessa stared.

"Eight hundred twelve days?"

Caroline nodded slowly.

"That tracks."

Nobody liked the phrase.

Because that tracks meant history.

The kind that had been waiting for them.

Jack looked toward her.

"What happened 812 days ago?"

Caroline answered immediately.

"Celia disappeared."

The room froze.

Not because the timing was suspicious.

Because it was not.

The timing was perfect.

Which was worse.

Room 414 had been shut down when Celia vanished.

Now it had returned minutes after the Anchor discussion, minutes after Sutter's direct message, minutes after someone realized what Celia had actually been trying to say.

Coincidence was running out of oxygen.

Maggie pointed toward the wall.

"Tell me that's not real-time."

Christina paused.

Then:

```text
Status update confirmed.
Source system active.
Classification currently accepting changes.
```

Nobody liked that.

Active meant alive.

Not sentient.

Operational.

Someone was using it.

Now.

Jack looked at Sutter.

Directly.

"You said Room 414 started as a classification. You said the room came later. So where is it?"

Silence.

Sutter did not answer immediately.

That bothered everyone.

Finally:

"I don't know."

Caroline laughed once.

"That's new."

Sutter looked annoyed.

Not because she was wrong.

Because she was not.

Caroline folded her arms.

"Sutter always knows where the classifications live."

The room shifted.

Jack filed that away carefully.

The statement was not evidence, but it was not random either.

Christina continued processing the event.

Then another notification appeared.

```text
SECONDARY EVENT DETECTED

Associated file restored
```

Nobody moved.

Nobody wanted to.

Christina opened the file metadata.

Only metadata.

Nothing else.

```text
FILE NAME

414-TRANSITION-REVIEW

Status: Restored
Author: Unknown
Last modified: 812 days ago
Last accessed: 2 minutes ago
```

The room became still.

Again.

The timing was not subtle.

Two minutes ago.

During the Anchor discussion. During the Ethics Review. During the exact conversation about Celia.

Someone had opened a file that had been dormant for over two years.

Someone listening?

Maybe.

Someone watching?

Maybe.

Someone responding?

Much worse.

Maggie looked toward Christina.

"Can we see it?"

```text
Access denied.
```

Of course.

Jack almost laughed.

Not because it was funny.

Because every important thing in his life eventually ended up behind a permissions boundary.

Sutter stared at the metadata longer than anyone else.

That bothered Jack too.

"What is Transition Review?"

Nobody answered immediately.

Then Caroline did.

And for the first time all day, she looked frightened.

Not worried. Not concerned.

Frightened.

"It wasn't supposed to survive."

The room changed instantly.

Sutter looked toward her.

"Caroline."

A warning.

Not a request.

A warning.

Caroline ignored it.

Good.

Someone should.

"What is it?" Vanessa asked.

Caroline swallowed.

Then answered.

"The day Black Lantern died."

Nobody moved.

Nobody breathed correctly.

Because suddenly the room contained history again.

The real kind.

Not theories. Not philosophy.

Events.

Transition Review.

The day Black Lantern died.

Jack looked toward Sutter.

"You were there."

Not a question.

A fact.

Sutter nodded slowly.

"Yes."

Caroline too.

"Yes."

The same answer. Different emotions.

Sutter looked tired. Caroline looked haunted. Vanessa looked confused. Jack looked interested. Maggie looked dangerous.

Exactly the right combination.

Christina generated another update.

Unexpected.

```text
NEW ACCESS REQUEST

Source: Room 414 Classification
Requested witness: Jack Mercer
Status: Pending
```

The room froze.

Nobody spoke.

Because everyone understood immediately.

The classification was not asking for data. It was not asking for evidence. It was not asking for authorization.

It was asking for a witness.

Again.

The same wound. The same pattern. The same temptation.

Sutter stood immediately.

"No."

The answer arrived faster than any previous answer he had given all day.

Interesting.

Very interesting.

Jack looked at him.

"No?"

Sutter shook his head.

"No one accepts that request."

Silence.

Then Maggie:

"Why?"

Sutter answered without hesitation.

"Because that's how it starts."

The room went completely still.

Because nobody asked what it was.

Everybody already knew.

Christina created a final evidence card.

```text
ROOM 414 REACTIVATION

Confirmed:
- Classification active
- Transition Review restored
- Witness request issued

Unknown:
- Operator
- Intent
- Objective

Risk: High
```

Jack stared at the witness request. Then at Sutter. Then at Caroline.

Two people who had survived the first collapse.

And neither of them wanted the door opened.

For the first time since entering NorthStar, that frightened him more than the door itself.
