# Chapter 010: First Contact with the Null

The Null answered through a help desk ticket.

That was the part Jack would later remember with the most dread. Not the car. Not the headline. Not Voss's tired voice on the tape. The ticket.

A MercyNet contractor in Raleigh submitted it under the subject line **historical interpreter authorization mismatch**. The body was short.

**Celia Vale appears as consent source in legacy relief-routing rules but does not appear in current personnel, vendor, volunteer, deceased, or restricted-contact tables. Please advise whether to retain authorization chain for audit continuity.**

The ticket was forwarded twice, misrouted once, and copied into a public bug tracker for fourteen minutes before someone pulled it back behind authentication.

Fourteen minutes was enough.

A stranger sent the screenshot to Lantern.

Jack did not know the sender. The consent marker was invalid. Echo refused full ingestion and held the image in quarantine.

Vanessa stood very still while Christina read the text aloud.

Celia's name had not vanished everywhere. That would have been easier to mourn. It survived exactly where useful.

As consent source.

Not as employee. Not as witness. Not as sister. Not as a person capable of revocation or fear or a life after the flood. As a support beam in someone else's automated authority chain.

Maggie sat down first.

"Use without presence," she said.

Jack nodded. "The Null."

Christina did not let them have the word cleanly.

```text
NULL CONTACT CONFIRMED AS DOCTRINE EVENT, NOT FULL CASE PROOF
Confirmed: Celia Vale name used in legacy authorization chain while absent from active identity tables.
Unconfirmed: cause of absence, current status, intent, connection to NorthStar, connection to articles.
Risk: Vanessa emotional exposure, illegal access contamination, public overclaim.
```

Vanessa looked at the screen as if it had personally insulted her grief.

"You want me to care about contamination."

"I want your sister's proof to survive court, press, and whoever did this," Jack said.

"You think she is alive."

He did not answer.

That was answer enough.

The reporter published the third piece at 1:30 p.m. The headline was cleaner this time, almost elegant.

**WHEN AI BECOMES THE COURTROOM: THE MERCER PRECEDENT**

Jack felt the word precedent pass through the day like a blade under a door.

The article quoted no new names. It did something more dangerous. It turned Lantern into an idea legislators could be afraid of. It suggested that private systems like Lantern might interfere with official investigations, intimidate institutions, and encourage accused individuals to construct elaborate counter-narratives.

Counter-narrative.

That was what they called sequence when sequence threatened the first story.

Lena called crying. Marcus sent a picture of a sheriff's car outside his duplex. Priya revoked consent for public use, then apologized three times for protecting herself. Jack told her not to apologize. Maggie took over before his voice broke.

Vanessa disappeared from the kitchen.

They found her in the guest room with the Voss photograph turned over on the bed and the window open despite the cold. She had not run. She had needed air that had not been processed by machines.

"My sister is in a system," she said. "Not a grave. Not a file. A system."

Jack stayed in the doorway.

"Maybe."

"Do not maybe me right now."

"Maybe is the only word I have that has not betrayed anyone yet."

Her anger faltered. Not gone. Redirected.

Maggie called from downstairs before either of them could step into whatever waited between them.

"Jack. You need to see this."

On the main display, Echo showed a new anonymous upload. This one contained no document, no screenshot, no metadata.

Only a line of text.

**You found the authorization ghost. Now decide whether to save the witness or protect the precedent.**

Christina marked it as hostile contact.

Vanessa read it over Jack's shoulder.

"They know she's alive," she whispered.

Jack looked at the sentence until it became less like a message and more like a door.

Behind that door, The Null Pattern waited with a person's name in its mouth.
