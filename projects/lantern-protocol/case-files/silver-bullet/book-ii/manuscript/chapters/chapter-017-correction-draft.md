# Chapter 017: Correction Draft

The first rule of impossible evidence was to insult the word impossible until it left the room.

Jack wrote that on the legal pad beneath Maggie's blue line, then crossed it out.

Too clever.

Too close to comfort.

The kitchen had become a lab with dirty coffee mugs. Three laptops sat open. Maggie's binder occupied the center of the table like a courthouse that had decided to become portable. Vanessa sat near the window, not quite part of the investigation and not quite outside it. Her phone lay face down beside her untouched water glass.

Christina held the contradiction on the wall.

```text
PUBLIC ARTICLE UPDATE
Timestamp: 00:14
Phrase: preservation from publication

LOCAL DRAFT
Created: 00:37
Phrase: preservation from publication

Temporal conflict: external publication predates local draft creation by 23 minutes.
```

Twenty-three minutes.

Jack had spent too much of his life being harmed by things small enough for strangers to dismiss.

One sentence.

One timestamp.

One form.

One unchecked box.

One phrase arriving before it should.

Maggie opened the binder to the printed article.

"We start by proving the clock," she said.

Vanessa looked at the wall.

"What if the clock is not the problem?"

"Then we prove that second."

"That sounds slow."

Maggie did not look up.

"That sounds admissible."

Jack let them have that exchange without stepping between it. Growth, apparently, was sometimes just keeping your mouth shut while two dangerous women built guardrails around your ruin.

Christina began with the local machine.

```text
LOCAL SYSTEM CHECK
System clock: synchronized
NTP source: verified
Draft application timestamp: local creation confirmed
Autosave history: available
Clipboard history: partial
Network status: active during draft window
```

"Partial clipboard history?" Maggie asked.

Jack grimaced.

"Windows being Windows."

"I need less poetry."

"Clipboard retention did not capture the phrase before the draft."

"Better."

Vanessa leaned forward.

"So the phrase did not come from his computer?"

"No," Jack said. "It means we have not proven that it did."

Christina moved to the browser cache.

No copy of the phrase before 00:37.

No sent email.

No uploaded draft.

No form submission.

No cloud sync event tied to the abandoned response.

Every negative result should have made Jack feel cleaner.

Instead each one made the room narrower.

If the phrase had not left through the obvious doors, either the doors were not obvious or the house had a window they had not found.

Maggie flipped to the article printout.

"Now the public side."

The reporter's article had three visible revisions and two hidden update markers embedded in the page source. The site did not show those to readers. Readers got a smooth surface. The page kept its scars in places only machines and suspicious people bothered to look.

Christina displayed the revision stack.

```text
ARTICLE REVISION HISTORY
23:48 initial publication
00:14 correction appended
00:52 paragraph reorder
01:06 metadata refresh
```

"Who made the correction?" Vanessa asked.

```text
Visible author: staff desk
Internal user ID: redacted by CMS export
Session origin: publisher VPN
```

"Helpful," Vanessa said.

"It is helpful," Maggie said.

Vanessa gave her a look.

Maggie tapped the timestamp.

"Publisher VPN means it may not be the reporter. It may be an editor, legal, syndication, automated update, or someone with access. We do not narrow too fast."

Jack watched Vanessa absorb the correction without liking it.

That, too, counted as progress.

Christina pulled the article's correction text into a comparison window.

**Mercer's system reportedly distinguishes preservation from publication, though critics question whether vulnerable participants understand the difference.**

Jack had typed in his abandoned draft:

**Lantern preserves records. It does not publish them. Participants control the difference between preservation and publication.**

The phrase was close.

Not exact.

That mattered.

Exact theft would have been cleaner.

Near theft was worse. It meant transformation. A human hand, a model, a template, or an editorial process had carried the idea and bent it into the article's voice.

Maggie circled two words.

**reportedly distinguishes**

"This sounds like someone summarizing a claim," she said.

"Or laundering one," Jack said.

She did not correct him.

Christina opened a withheld comparison layer, then stopped.

Amber banner.

```text
UNCONFIRMED LINK AVAILABLE
Relation: correction phrase / Template 4B-R language family
Confidence: low
Recommendation: do not map as causal
```

Jack looked at Maggie.

Maggie looked at him.

"No," she said.

"I did not ask."

"Your face asked."

Vanessa almost smiled.

Christina retained the link outside the active map.

The house breathed again.

For maybe four seconds.

Then Lena called.

Jack did not answer immediately. That had become another kind of progress. He looked to Maggie, who nodded once.

He put Lena on speaker.

"Are you safe?" he asked.

"I think so," Lena said.

She did not sound safe.

She sounded like someone trying not to become the kind of person everyone had to worry about.

"They changed the agreement again."

Jack closed his eyes.

"Send it to the intake address. Not my phone."

"I know. I did."

Christina confirmed receipt.

Lena continued.

"There's a new sentence. It says I acknowledge that preservation does not equal publication."

The kitchen lost all sound.

Maggie reached for the table.

Vanessa stood.

Jack opened his eyes.

"Read that again."

Lena did.

Slowly.

"I acknowledge that preservation does not equal publication, and that any independent review of institutional records may create additional risk to affected parties."

Christina aligned the sentence across three windows.

Jack's draft.

The article correction.

Lena's revised agreement.

Different words.

Same spine.

Maggie's voice went low.

"When was her agreement revised?"

Christina answered.

```text
LENA AGREEMENT REVISION
Document generated: 00:19
Delivered: 07:42
Phrase: preservation does not equal publication
```

Five minutes after the article correction.

Eighteen minutes before Jack's draft.

Vanessa whispered, "That is not a leak from him."

"No," Maggie said. "It is a shared source."

Jack felt relief try to enter him and refused it at the door.

Shared source did not mean safe.

Shared source meant architecture.

Lena said, "What does that mean?"

No one answered quickly.

Jack chose the cleanest truth.

"It means the phrase may not have come from us. It may have reached us last."

Lena was quiet for a long time.

"So I'm not crazy."

The sentence split him open a little.

"No," Jack said. "You're not."

After the call ended, Christina added the agreement to quarantine and created a new comparison card.

```text
CORRECTION DRAFT EVENT
Confirmed: phrase family appears in external publication and Lena agreement before local draft creation.
Unconfirmed: origin, mechanism, author, intent.
Most likely categories:
1. shared template language
2. compromised external communication chain
3. automated editorial/legal language insertion
4. unknown prior source
```

Vanessa looked at the fourth item.

"Unknown prior source."

"Not supernatural," Maggie said immediately.

"I did not say supernatural."

"You were about to make your eyebrows do it."

Despite everything, Jack laughed once.

It surprised him.

It did not last.

Christina opened a final panel.

```text
NEXT REQUIRED CONTROL
Device/account isolation recommended for all subjects with recent phrase exposure.
Priority: Vanessa Vale device, due to prior Black Lantern account anomaly.
```

Vanessa's face changed before she could hide it.

Jack saw fear.

Not guilt.

Fear.

Maggie saw it too.

She softened her voice without softening the boundary.

"Phone on the counter. Your choice. Your terms. But if we keep going, we cannot pretend the phone is not in the room."

Vanessa looked at the phone beside the water glass.

For a moment, Jack thought she would refuse.

Then she picked it up and held it like something that might bite.

"No private messages unrelated to Celia."

"Agreed," Maggie said.

"No photos."

"Agreed."

"No call history except unknown numbers from the last forty-eight hours."

Jack nodded.

"Agreed."

Vanessa placed the phone face up on the counter.

No one touched it.

Not yet.

The screen woke by itself.

One notification waited there.

No sender name.

No app icon Jack recognized.

Only a subject line.

**BLACK LANTERN RECOVERY: ACCOUNT VERIFICATION REQUIRED**

Vanessa stepped back.

The phone stayed lit.

The house seemed to lean toward it.
