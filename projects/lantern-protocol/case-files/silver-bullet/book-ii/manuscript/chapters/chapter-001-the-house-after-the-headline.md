# Chapter 001: The House After the Headline

The house had learned to speak softly around Jack Mercer.

Floorboards cooled before dawn. Pipes clicked behind the walls with the patient rhythm of something counting. The refrigerator hummed in the kitchen where the first version of Lantern had once sat across three machines, two borrowed monitors, and one man too angry to sleep but too injured to break anything larger than his own life.

The headline arrived before the coffee finished brewing.

**DISGRACED VETERAN TURNS ALLEGATION ENGINE INTO PRIVATE AI PROJECT**

Jack read it once and felt nothing. That frightened him more than pain would have. Pain, at least, announced a location. This was colder, a clean removal of air. The story beneath the headline did not say he had been proven guilty. It did not have to. It arranged the nouns in the old formation: veteran, domestic violence, custody, extremist, database, artificial intelligence. Every word stood politely beside the next one and let the reader build the cage.

Maggie found him standing at the counter with his left hand flat against the granite and his right arm tucked against his ribs like a thing he had agreed not to trust.

"Do not answer it yet," she said.

The reporter had used his full name, quoted a retired attorney who had never met him, and embedded two screenshots from the old public case page. One showed the dismissed charge. The other showed the original accusation. The dismissal sat below the accusation like an apology printed after the wound.

Christina opened on the wall display without being summoned.

```text
PUBLIC NARRATIVE EVENT DETECTED
Risk category: reputational recurrence
Primary harm vector: accusation context survives correction
Recommended first action: preserve, do not engage
```

Preserve, do not engage.

That was the rule Jack had written after the third person told him to explain himself more clearly. Explanation had been a staircase that kept turning into a chute. Lantern had been built in that impossible hallway, not to win arguments, but to keep the order of events from being folded into someone else's summary.

In the article, the archive became the frightening thing. Not the accusation. Not the paperwork that kept breeding after its parent facts died. The machine that remembered. The man who refused to forget.

Maggie scrolled to the middle of the article and stopped.

"Jack."

He knew from her voice that the day had just grown another mouth.

The article included a name he had never released.

Lena Ortiz.

A nurse in Virginia. One of the first people to contact him after the Book One exposure. Lena had asked for help comparing personnel memos, hospital incident reports, and a child-safety referral that had moved through three offices with the same phrase repeated in each one.

**Pattern of instability.**

Jack had told her he could preserve what she sent. He had told her he could not save her life for her.

Now her name sat under his headline.

"Who had access?" Maggie asked.

Christina answered with restraint.

```text
Known access: Jack Mercer, Maggie Hale, Echo custody index, Lena Ortiz.
Unknown access: journalist source, archived public copy, derivative screenshot chain.
Constraint: do not infer breach before custody comparison.
```

Jack hated the constraint. He loved it because he hated it.

Outside, morning light entered the kitchen with the delicacy of a trespasser. His shoulder pulsed. The old fear woke in the joint before it reached his thoughts.

Maggie set the phone face down.

"We start where the evidence starts," she said. "Sequence. Permission. Chain."

"And if the source is inside?"

"Then we prove it. If the source is outside, we prove that too."

The first rule of surviving a Silver Bullet was not innocence. Innocence was too fragile to trust with survival.

The first rule was sequence.

Jack opened the case file. The screen lit his face in the pale blue of an old emergency.
