## Objective

Lock in the professional and financial devastation caused by the first arrest and DV-related allegations.

The first arrest must not be treated as only a personal humiliation or legal scare. For Jack, it is a career kill-switch. His professional life depends on trust, clearance eligibility, customer confidence, secure access, travel, reputation, and the ability to pass background scrutiny. Once the DV-related arrest enters the record stream, those systems begin closing before truth is heard.

## Core Story Rule

A DV-related charge can make Jack functionally unemployable even before guilt is proven.

The story should show that Jack wants to work, is capable of valuable work, and has technical brilliance, but the accusation creates practical barriers that block normal employment channels.

## Required Consequences

After the first arrest / DVPO event, show:

- security clearance review or suspension
- loss of cleared work access
- employer/customer risk response
- loss of government or enterprise trust
- inability to access customer systems
- background-check fear
- recruiters going quiet
- campaign/public contacts distancing themselves
- consulting opportunities disappearing
- travel and vehicle access problems
- money drain from legal costs and survival needs

## Dependence on Friends

Jack must become dependent on friends for ordinary life functions:

- rides
- charging devices
- retrieving property
- temporary housing
- printing/scanning documents
- watching kids or coordinating family needs
- basic logistics while he cannot safely contact people or go places himself

This should feel humiliating and scary, not comedic.

Required line candidate:

> He had built systems for agencies that trusted him with secrets. Now he needed a friend to drive him to a phone charger.

## Employability Horror

Use this as a recurring dread layer:

- Jack can work but cannot be hired.
- Jack can prove value but cannot clear intake screens.
- Jack can explain the accusation but explanation itself sounds risky.
- The public allegation creates a private blacklist effect.
- The system does not have to convict him to starve him.

Required line candidate:

> The charge did not have to become a conviction to become a credential.

## Political Race Context

Because Jack is in a political race, the arrest also attacks public viability:

- opponents do not need proof
- supporters go quiet
- volunteers hesitate
- public perception shifts faster than evidence can travel
- proof exists, but asking for it requires courage

Required line candidate:

> The proof was there. The problem was that proof required courage from people who only had curiosity.

## Acceptance Criteria

- Clearance/work collapse appears early after the first arrest.
- Jack's unemployability is shown as practical and immediate.
- Jack depends on friends for basic logistics.
- Employment loss is tied to DV allegations, clearance trust, and public risk perception.
- The story preserves Jack's competence: he is not unable to work; he is blocked from being allowed to work.
- The financial pressure makes the later plea/deal pressure feel more dangerous.
